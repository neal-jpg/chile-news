Chile's Economy Flirts With Technical Recession After Fifth Straight Monthly Drop

Chile's central bank reported Wednesday that the country's economic activity index (IMACEC — think a monthly GDP snapshot) fell 0.9% in May, the fifth consecutive monthly decline. That puts Chile on the doorstep of a technical recession for the first time since the 2008 financial crisis. Unemployment hit 9.4% in the March–May period, a five-year high.

Chile's main business federation (the CPC — roughly the Business Roundtable) called on President Kast, Congress, and the private sector to work in unison: "All the chips need to be on the table." Economists have slashed their full-year GDP growth projections to just 1–1.5%, down from earlier expectations. One investment bank cut its forecast to 1.3%.

The Kast government — which took office March 11 after a landslide win on a platform of economic revival — says it inherited a "stalled country" and is pushing investment-friendly reforms: cutting permit timelines, reducing regulatory friction, and improving hiring conditions. The next two IMACEC readings will determine whether Chile can pull back from the edge.

Ex-Security Minister Faces Impeachment Threat Over Police Snooping

Chile's comptroller — a government watchdog with binding legal authority, think a GAO with teeth — ruled that former Security Minister Trinidad Steinert exceeded her powers by ordering national police to hand over intelligence files on detectives who were actively investigating a criminal network. The ruling found she violated the "duty of abstention," a legal requirement to stay out of cases where she had a conflict of interest.

Left-wing opposition deputies are now openly discussing a constitutional accusation against Steinert — Chile's version of a congressional impeachment. The Communist Party says it has enough votes to advance one. The Senate president (a Kast ally) urged opponents to focus on "real problems." President Kast appealed Thursday for dialogue, calling security and jobs the country's true emergencies — not political warfare.

Top Venezuela Gang Boss Extradited from Colombia, Arrives in Santiago

Dayonis Junior Orozco — alias "Botija" — landed in Chile Thursday after being extradited from Colombia, where he was captured in 2024. He is one of the highest-ranking leaders of the Tren de Aragua, the Venezuelan criminal organization that has spread across Latin America and carried out some of Chile's most high-profile recent murders.

Orozco is accused of ordering the murders of Ronald Ojeda (a Venezuelan military defector who sought asylum in Chile and was abducted and killed in 2024) and a Chilean Carabineros captain. Justice Minister Martín Arrau called the extradition "a blow to the Tren de Aragua" and confirmed Orozco will be held in Chile's maximum-security prison (REPAS). Chile is actively building new high-security capacity to house transnational gang leaders.

Chile's Shadow Economy Hits $5.7 Billion a Year — Business Groups Push 45-Point Plan

A new report from Chile's main business federation (the CPC) estimates that illegal markets — contraband cigarettes, illicit online gambling, counterfeit goods, and criminal lending — generate $5.7 billion per year, roughly 1.5% of GDP. That's comparable to the entire economic output of one of Chile's midsize regions, and it costs the government $1.5 billion in lost tax revenue annually.

The federation published 45 policy proposals to dismantle illicit markets, organized around three pillars: illicit trade, illicit finance, and illegal extraction of natural resources. Surveys attached to the report found that one in three Chileans has bought from informal markets, and 68% associate illegal commerce directly with organized crime.

"Gag Law 2.0" Keeps Moving in Senate Over Press Freedom Objections

A Senate bill that would toughen criminal penalties for leaking materials from active criminal investigations is still advancing — despite a delay secured by left-leaning senators — and press freedom groups are alarmed. Chile's Federation of Media has called the bill a "democratic regression," warning that one contested clause could expose journalists to criminal prosecution simply for publishing leaked documents, even if they had no role in obtaining them.

Backers insist the bill targets prosecutors, defense lawyers, and defendants with formal access to case files — not reporters. Critics say the language is broad enough to chill investigative journalism. Think of it as Chile's version of the US debate over reporter shield laws: who gets protected when publishing leaked government material, and who doesn't.

"Twelve Years of Sermons, 100 Days of Reality"

El Mostrador · Left-Leaning

A columnist at El Mostrador takes stock of Kast's first 100 days, arguing that Chile's Republican Party "entered La Moneda as the backbone of a government without its own congressional majorities" — a situation familiar to any American who's watched a new president try to govern without controlling the House and Senate. After twelve years in opposition, preaching about what the left was doing wrong on crime, the economy, and governance, Kast's coalition is discovering that governing is harder than campaigning. The column is sharp but substantive — less a victory lap from the left than a genuine challenge to the right to show what it can actually deliver.
