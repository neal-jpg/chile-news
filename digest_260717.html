Top Headlines
Weather Emergency

Storm arrives in full force: three dead, 600,000 without power, classes cancelled across central Chile

The storm that was building all week arrived overnight with devastating force. Three people have died nationwide: a road worker struck by a falling tree while clearing a highway in the Biobío region, a man who fell from his roof while cleaning clogged gutters in Araucanía, and one person killed by an electrical discharge in Santiago. More than 590,000 households lost power across the center-south; port operations are suspended along much of the coast; and the government cancelled Friday school in six regions including Santiago.

The meteorological office describes this as an "atmospheric river" — a concentrated band of Pacific moisture, like the Pineapple Express that periodically hammers California — funneling back-to-back frontal systems into Chile. The Metropolitan Region is projected to receive 100–150mm of total rainfall through Sunday, comparable to some of the worst storms in recent memory. Mining regulators activated crisis committees to monitor landslide risks in the northern copper zones, where Chile's copper deposits — roughly 25% of global supply — sit in mountain passes exposed to heavy rain.

President Kast spent Thursday at the Navy's hydrological center monitoring conditions and urged Chileans to prepare emergency kits, avoid driving, and check on vulnerable neighbors. SENAPRED (Chile's FEMA equivalent) identified over 2,300 high-risk points across the country. The Armed Forces remain on standby for evacuations. Conditions are expected to ease by Sunday evening.

Economy

Senate passes Kast's sweeping economic reform 26–24 after a 12-hour overnight session

Chile's Senate approved the "megarreforma" — President Kast's flagship economic overhaul — Thursday morning by the narrowest possible margin: 26 in favor, 24 against, after a session that stretched past midnight. The centerpiece is a gradual cut in the corporate income tax from 27% to 23% by 2029, plus a 20-year freeze on tax conditions for investments above $350 million. Finance Minister Jorge Quiroz called it "the beginning of Chile's economic recovery"; the left called it a giveaway to large companies. Think of it as roughly equivalent to the 2017 Trump tax cuts, but in a Chilean Senate where the right barely has the votes to pass anything.

The bill now needs approval in the Chamber of Deputies, where right-wing groups hold a slim majority and the centrist PDG (Partido de la Gente, a populist center party) is the kingmaker. Opposition senators — including Christian Democrat Yasna Provoste, who called one clause a "tax amnesty" — announced they will challenge key provisions before Chile's Constitutional Tribunal. Minister Quiroz isn't waiting: he's already drafting a second package covering capital markets, labor flexibility, and investment facilitation measures.

Politics · Culture

K-pop fans march to La Moneda after Kast government blocks BTS from playing the National Stadium

The Instituto Nacional de Deportes (Chile's equivalent of a national sports authority) blocked three BTS concerts scheduled for October at Estadio Nacional, arguing the band's elaborate 360-degree stage setup would destroy the pitch and conflict with a national soccer team match and the annual Teletón fundraiser. BTS's fans — known worldwide as the ARMY — organized a march down the Alameda to La Moneda on July 5, with thousands chanting "BTS at the Nacional" and holding signs in both Korean and Spanish. The story went international immediately, painting the episode as an unexpected early stumble for Kast.

Interior Minister Claudio Alvarado responded by saying the government is "evaluating alternatives" and invited the production company to submit a revised stage proposal. The concerts are scheduled for October 14, 16, and 17 — a tight timeline for a redesign. Senator Paulina Vodanovic (PS) accused the government of "putting bureaucracy above culture and the economy," since the shows would generate tens of millions of dollars in tourism and ticket revenue. Still unresolved as of today.

Economy · Environment

The same storm soaking Santiago is threatening Chile's copper operations — and global supply chains

While Santiago braces for flooding, Chile's mining regulator Sernageomin has activated its crisis committee, and copper companies convened emergency meetings Thursday to assess landslide and mudslide risks in the high-altitude mine zones. Chile produces roughly 25% of the world's copper — the metal that goes into every EV battery, smartphone, power transformer, and plumbing fitting made on earth. When Chile's mountain passes flood, supply disruptions ripple into global commodity markets within weeks.

The storm's timing compounds pressure on an already strained economic picture: IPSA (Chile's main stock index) fell 0.70% Thursday, with lithium producer SQM-B leading the decline at −2.7%, and copper export revenues have not recovered enough to offset a 34% drop in US-bound exports so far this year. For the Kast government, which is betting its economic reform on a copper-led recovery, this weekend's weather is more than an inconvenience.

Interesting / Offbeat

Chile's biggest Catholic festival — 200,000 pilgrims in the Atacama desert — comes to Santiago in 52 photographs

The Fiesta de la Virgen del Carmen de La Tirana is something Americans have no real equivalent for: every July, more than 200,000 pilgrims converge on a tiny Atacama desert town of about 800 residents to dance, pray, and parade in elaborate hand-made costumes for days on end before the final procession. It's roughly Mardi Gras meets a tent revival, held in the driest place on earth, and it's been going on for centuries. A photography exhibition by Ricardo Herrera Rojas — "La Tirana: Fe, Devoción y Memoria," 52 images spanning decades of the festival — is showing in Santiago this month. Worth seeking out even during a storm weekend.

Today's monster storm is also giving Santiago the cleanest air it'll see all winter

Santiago sits in a bowl-shaped valley surrounded by mountains on three sides, making it one of Latin America's worst smog traps in winter — wood-burning stoves and cold-air inversions cook up a thick particulate haze that regularly hits "unhealthy" levels. The AQI in Las Condes was hovering around 60–70 (moderate) most of this week. Today's torrential rain is pressure-washing the valley: heavy precipitation drops fine particulate from the air almost instantly, and the strong winds behind the front will flush what's left by tomorrow morning. Enjoy the view of the Andes from your window — the mountains will be razor-sharp by Saturday, assuming you still have power.

Opinion / Editorial
El Mostrador · Left / Progressive · July 17

"¿Dónde están los líderes de oposición que nuestro medioambiente necesita?"

A July 17 column in El Mostrador — published on the same morning the storm knocked out power to 600,000 Chileans — asks where the left's environmental leaders have gone. The author argues that progressive politicians are calculating their every move based on polls and electoral calendars instead of taking courageous environmental stands: "While parts of Chile dry, burn, and flood, our leaders are asking 'how do I survive politically?' rather than 'how do the people in my district survive?'" The column calls this posture morally indefensible, arriving at a moment when the climate argument is literally falling from the sky.

El Líbero · Right / Conservative · July 2026

"La megarreforma, un imperativo moral"

A column by Magdalena Merbilháa in El Líbero (Chile's leading right-leaning commentary site) argues that cutting corporate taxes and freeing up investment isn't a free-market ideology — it's a moral obligation, because high unemployment and stagnant growth cause direct human suffering. "You cannot call yourself compassionate while defending the tax rates that are keeping 9% of Chileans out of work," the piece argues. It pushes back on critics who equate lower taxes with gutted public services, contending that a growing economy funds better services than a stagnant one with nominally high rates.

Indie Rock

Los Tres — XCLNT

Released April 10, "XCLNT" is Los Tres' first studio album with their original lineup in 26 years — and it was recorded at Abbey Road Studios in London. For context: Los Tres are Chile's R.E.M. or The Replacements — a beloved early-90s alternative band whose literate, cueca-inflected rock shaped a generation of Chilean music and whose catalog is still obsessively studied. "XCLNT" sounds like the band never left: angular guitars, cueca rhythms, Álvaro Henríquez's unmistakable raspy delivery, and lyrics that are simultaneously Chilean-specific and universally recognizable. Lead single "Cantar y Amar" has been on heavy streaming rotation since March. If you want to understand where Chilean rock comes from, this is the record to start with.

Folk Indie

Pescao — Uno

One of the most charming Chilean debuts of the year: Santiago trio Pescao (Tomás Hurtado on vocals, Clemente Boetsch on guitars, Andrés Elgueta on drums) released their first album this July after spending a year publishing singles. "Uno" collects twelve songs built around folk, 1960s pop, surf rock, and Latin American trova — recorded largely in the band members' own rooms, with a deliberately warm, lo-fi palette that prioritizes collective playing over production polish. The result sounds personal rather than manufactured: think a young Nano Stern meeting a Santiago apartment in winter. For fans of Chinoy, early Francisca Valenzuela, or anyone who wants Chilean indie without the reverb-drenched affectation.

Punk

Superbronca — Consume

New Santiago band Superbronca just dropped "Consume," a debut punk record aimed squarely at consumer culture, alienation, and the contradictions of urban modern life. Ten songs, two years in the making, produced by members who've been in the Chilean indie-rock scene for years (projects include Machuca, Mástil, and Vandalik). The album is deliberately uncomfortable — sharp, sardonic, and specific about the ways Santiago eats people alive. For fans of The Clash, Soda Stereo's more angular side, or Chilean punk acts Eterna Inocencia and Soziedad Alkoholika. Available on streaming now.
