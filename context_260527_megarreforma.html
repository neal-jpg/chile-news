What is the Megarreforma?

The short version

The megarreforma is President Kast's flagship economic reform package — a broad overhaul of Chile's tax and public finance system that the new right-wing government is trying to push through Congress. It's called "mega" because it bundles multiple reforms into one package rather than passing them one by one.

What's in it

The details are still being negotiated, but the core elements include cuts to corporate taxes, changes to how municipalities are funded, and reductions in some consumption taxes (like VAT on medicines and diapers — the latter added specifically to win swing votes). It's broadly a supply-side reform agenda: lower taxes on business to stimulate investment and growth.

Think of it as roughly analogous to the 2017 Trump tax cuts — a right-wing government trying to pass a large tax reform bill with thin margins in the legislature, making deals with swing votes to get it across the line.

Why it's hard to pass

Kast's coalition doesn't have a commanding majority in the Senate. They need to pick off swing votes from smaller parties — including the PDG (a centrist populist party) — to reach the numbers they need. Getting those votes requires concessions, and concessions make the core coalition nervous.

The constitutional accusation against former Economy Minister Grau is making things worse: it's poisoning the bipartisan atmosphere in Congress right when the government needs goodwill. Several Chile Vamos senators have publicly said the impeachment filing is bad timing.

What the opposition says

The left — including The Clinic's sources and Communist Party congresswoman Irací Hassler — argue the megarreforma doesn't have genuine popular support and that the government is using culture-war attacks (calling the opposition "Communist") to distract from that. It's a familiar dynamic: a reforming government insisting its agenda is popular, an opposition insisting it's being rammed through.
