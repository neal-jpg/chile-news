Kast's Economic "Mega-Bill" Squeaks Through the Senate — By Exactly One Vote

Chile's Senate on June 24 approved the general motion of President José Antonio Kast's sweeping "megarreforma" by the thinnest possible margin: 26 votes in favor, 23 against, one abstention — every ruling-coalition senator holding the line, not a single opposition vote crossing the aisle. The 40-measure package would cut Chile's corporate tax rate from 27% to 23% over five years, create a 25-year tax stability guarantee shielding major investment projects from future royalty hikes, and slash red tape on large mining and infrastructure permits. Think of it as Chile's version of a pro-business tax-and-deregulation package — the administration's central bet that unlocking stalled private investment is the fastest route out of the country's economic slump.

The opposition is furious. Center-left lawmakers called the vote a "legislative steamroller" and accused the government of ignoring their requests for real negotiation. At least 20 opposition senators had sent a formal letter demanding the government open talks before the detailed committee stage — which now begins, with an amendment deadline of July 6. Finance Minister Jorge Quiroz acknowledged the narrow win but expressed confidence the government would gain "more support" in committee. That's a tall order: the coalition needs at least some opposition buy-in on specifics, and the trust deficit is significant.

Impeachment Trial for Ex-Finance Chief Grau Heads to Senate — Vote Tuesday

The Chamber of Deputies voted 77–68 on June 23 to approve a constitutional accusation against Nicolás Grau, Boric's former Economy and Finance Minister, sending the case to the Senate for a final vote on June 30. The charges: Grau allegedly understated the 2026 fiscal deficit and concealed spending pressures in his final budget, leaving the incoming Kast government holding a worse balance sheet than publicly reported. A constitutional accusation is Chile's version of an impeachment — it can strip a public official of their post and bar them from office for five years.

The Clinic reports the accusation arrives at the Senate "weakened." A two-thirds majority is needed to convict (think of the U.S. Senate's two-thirds bar for removing an impeached president), and several centrist senators are publicly uncommitted. Grau says he'll face the chamber "with tranquility" and maintains no wrongdoing occurred. Watch for the Senate vote this Tuesday.

Moody's Warns: Years of Deficit Overruns Have Eroded Chile's Fiscal Credibility

Moody's Ratings flagged Thursday that Chile now has "a more constrained fiscal margin than its peers" — the cost of several years of budget overruns under the previous Boric administration. The agency said the Kast government's ability to actually meet its deficit targets will be "fundamental" to Chile's credit standing. The government needs to cut the structural deficit from 3.7% of GDP in 2025 to 2.6% this year and 1.8% by 2027 — ambitions that Kast has framed as austerity but that depend on growth that hasn't yet materialized.

On markets, the peso weakened to $923 per dollar this week, driven by a strong U.S. dollar and persistent hawkish signals from the Fed — reminding investors that Chile's open, dollar-linked economy remains exposed to external shocks no matter who's governing.

Kast Puts Business on Notice: Prepare for a "Critical Six Months" on Jobs

President Kast told business executives this week that Chile is heading into "a particularly critical period" for employment over the next six months — and urged them directly to keep hiring. The backdrop is stark: unemployment is running at 9.1%, its highest level in nearly five years, while the Central Bank just slashed its 2026 GDP growth forecast to 1%–1.75%, roughly halving its March estimate. Kast framed the economic strain as a Boric-era inheritance but was also unusually candid that the turnaround will be slow — an admission that sets up a difficult political test.

"Terror Drug" Seized for the First Time in Antofagasta After Three Simultaneous Raids

Carabineros busted three drug networks in Antofagasta on Thursday, arresting 19 people across simultaneous operations — and made an alarming find: 220 pills of a synthetic substance dubbed the "terror drug," so named because it induces acute panic rather than euphoria (think a bad LSD trip without any of the high). It's the first time the drug has been detected in the Antofagasta region, marking what authorities say is a northward spread of Chile's synthetic narcotics market. The coordinated raids in one of Chile's key mining and port cities suggest organized supply chains rather than casual distribution.

Kast Gets Into a Very Public Argument With a Child Who Wouldn't Shake His Hand

At an official ceremony in Villarrica on Friday — where Kast was handing land titles to Mapuche families — a small boy refused to greet the president. Kast didn't let it go: he engaged the child's mother, who shot back that the president "has all of Chile in bad shape" and that her son knows it. The clip went viral. In a twist that sent the internet into overdrive, Carabineros later detained the mother on pre-existing fraud warrants — a detail that gave both sides plenty to argue about. Left opposition politicians called Kast "arrogant" for confronting a minor; his supporters pointed to the arrest record. Either way, it's the kind of image moment that follows a president for years.

34 Years, One Week's Notice: Santiago's Beloved Artisan Fair at Pío Nono Must Go

The artisan market at Pío Nono — a Santiago institution for 34 years, tucked in front of the University of Chile's Law School in the heart of the Bellavista/Lastarria zone — received official notice last week that it must clear out within seven days to make room for Metro Line 7 construction. Vendors say they were blindsided: "It's all been one big deception." Line 7 has been in planning for years, promising to connect Las Condes with the south of the city, but no one apparently communicated to the market's occupants that their days were numbered. A small piece of Santiago bohemia disappears.

"El SIMCE no se puede eliminar con tijeras presupuestarias"

El Mostrador · Left-Leaning · June 27, 2026

SIMCE is Chile's national standardized test — the rough equivalent of No Child Left Behind–era state assessments in the U.S. It has been controversial for years: teachers' unions and much of the Chilean left want it gone, arguing it narrows curriculum and disadvantages low-income schools. This June 27 column from El Mostrador pushes back on the method, not the outcome: whatever one thinks of SIMCE, defunding or eliminating it through administrative budget cuts is the wrong move. A decision this consequential to Chilean education deserves a proper democratic debate in the legislature, not quiet death-by-spreadsheet. It's a signal that education policy is about to become one of the sharper domestic battlegrounds of the Kast era.
