National · Humanitarian

Chile Mourns: Four Nationals Confirmed Dead in Venezuela Earthquakes as New Tremor Strikes

Chile's Foreign Ministry confirmed on June 29 that four Chilean nationals have now died in the catastrophic double earthquake that struck northern Venezuela on June 24 — two magnitude 7.2 and 7.5 quakes that hit just 39 seconds apart near San Felipe and Yumare. The total death toll in Venezuela has reached 1,450, with an estimated 50,000 people still missing under rubble. A fifth tremor shook Caracas and the coastal city of La Guaira on June 29, further complicating search and rescue operations.

Among Chile's dead is Erika Ramírez, 44, who was in a La Guaira restaurant when a wall collapsed, killing her instantly while her husband was pulled out alive after 30 hours in the rubble. The Chilean government has activated consular emergency services and is coordinating with Venezuelan authorities to account for the estimated 15,000 Chileans living in Venezuela. Chile also donated 30,000 diphtheria and tetanus vaccines to aid relief efforts. For context: Venezuela and Chile have had a strained diplomatic relationship for years over Venezuela's authoritarian drift, but humanitarian cooperation between the two countries has continued through back channels.

Kast's Big Economic Bill Enters Line-by-Line Gauntlet — Opposition Signals Room to Deal

After narrowly clearing a Senate threshold vote 26–23 on June 24, Kast's sweeping "National Reconstruction" bill now faces the harder part: an article-by-article debate in which the government has almost no margin for error. The package's centerpiece is a corporate tax cut from 27% to 23% and a controversial 25-year tax-stability guarantee for large investments — both of which have drawn opposition fire. Amendments are due July 6.

The mood shifted subtly over the weekend: Socialist senators signaled they are willing to negotiate on specific provisions, and former Economy Minister Carlos Ominami (see Opinion, below) published a column calling on the opposition to engage constructively rather than play pure obstruction. The government, in turn, has quietly indicated openness to modifications. The dynamic now resembles the early stages of a congressional sausage-making process more than a binary pass-or-fail showdown.

Winter Flu Surge Peaks: Viral Positivity at 52%, Pediatric ICUs Over 75% Full

Chile's winter respiratory season has hit its peak: the Health Ministry's weekly winter campaign report recorded 52% viral positivity in the latest sample week — the highest rate of 2026. Both Influenza A (28.7% of positive tests) and the less common Influenza B (23.2%) are circulating simultaneously, a double strain burden that strains healthcare capacity. Respiratory cases now account for 37.7% of all primary care visits nationally.

The most alarming numbers are in pediatric intensive care: units are at 75.6% capacity, with nearly 30% of pediatric ICU beds occupied by respiratory cases. Children under 4 saw hospitalizations spike 23% in a single week. Free flu vaccines remain available at public health centers for at-risk groups (over-65s, pregnant women, children under 2, people with chronic conditions) — vaccination coverage is at 75.6% nationally. If you haven't had yours, now is the time. Expect longer emergency room wait times through mid-July.

Politics

Kast at 100 Days: Crime Down, Economy Limping, Coalition Under Strain

President Kast crossed the 100-day mark last week with a Criteria poll showing 39% approval and 51% disapproval — a 17-point drop from his inauguration figures before partially recovering after a June cabinet reshuffle. His clearest win is on security: homicides dropped 13.7% compared to the same period in 2025, a number his government is citing prominently. Two ministers have already been replaced — the communications secretary and the security minister — an unusual rate of turnover for this early in a term.

The economy is the governing wound. GDP growth is projected at just 1–1.75% for 2026 and unemployment stands at 9.1%, a five-year high. Forty-nine percent of Chileans told Criteria the government's performance fell short of their expectations, vs. 14% who say it exceeded them. The dynamic is familiar to observers of new right-wing governments: strong on the law-and-order campaign promise, but the economy is slower to respond to policy signals than voters expect — and the timeline for the megarreforma's effects is measured in years, not months.

Immigration · Human Rights

200+ Haitian Children Still Missing in Chile — New Voices Push to Reframe Them as Refugees

The investigation into more than 200 Haitian children who entered Chile on charter flights in 2025 and cannot be located continues, with the government's inter-institutional task force still at work. Chronology published by Interferencia this week reveals the extent of systemic failure: in one case, family reunification permits were approved for three minors whose "father" had died a month earlier; on another flight, 124 children boarded in Haiti but only 44 landed in Santiago after an unexplained Lima stopover.

A new front opened today with an opinion piece in El Mostrador (see below) arguing that the legal framing itself is wrong: these children, who fled a failed state, should be classified as refugees under international law — not economic migrants. The distinction matters because refugees have stronger legal protections and the state has higher obligations toward them. Criminal investigations into possible trafficking, bribery, and malfeasance are ongoing.

Forty Years of Craft, One Week to Leave: Metro Line 7 Evicts Pío Nono's Beloved Artisan Fair

If you've ever browsed handmade jewelry and ceramics in the shady Parque Gómez Rojas in Bellavista, you've seen the Feria Artesanal Pío Nono — 18 families who have worked that stretch since the 1980s. As of tomorrow, July 1, they have to be gone. Metro notified them last week that construction on Line 7's Baquedano station entrance begins immediately and the fair must vacate. A promised relocation site — space in front of Universidad San Sebastián — was quietly dropped by the Municipality of Recoleta without explanation. Most vendors are elderly and have spent decades at the spot; they describe the notice as "una semana y un engaño" (a week's notice and a betrayal). The group is exploring a legal protection appeal (like a temporary restraining order) that might buy a few weeks, but metro officials say the construction timeline is firm and compensation will be offered individually. A slice of Santiago's informal cultural fabric, gone — for a subway line nobody has ridden yet.

Far-Right President, 100 Mapuche Land Titles: Kast in Temuco Makes a Pointed Symbolic Move

In a scene that would have seemed unlikely on the campaign trail, President Kast traveled to Temuco last week and personally handed over 100 individual property titles to Mapuche families in Temuco and Padre Las Casas. The event was framed as an advance in "legal property rights" — giving families formal title to land they occupy but don't legally own — rather than the collective land transfers that indigenous rights groups have long demanded and the Chilean state has only partially delivered. The government's bet: that individual property titles are more durable than community grants and that indigenous Chileans, like everyone else, ultimately want legal ownership over their homes. Critics on the left called it a repackaging of the status quo that avoids the deeper territorial question. Whatever the policy debate, it is worth noting that the most conservative Chilean president since the Pinochet era made a high-profile point of sitting down in Araucanía for an act of land recognition — a region that has seen years of political violence and unresolved conflict.

El Mostrador  ·  Left-Leaning  ·  Carlos Ominami

"La última oportunidad" — The Last Chance

Carlos Ominami — economy minister in the 1990s under Aylwin and a Socialist senator for two decades — argues today that the megarreforma's 26–23 Senate threshold vote is not a defeat for the opposition but an opening. He writes that Socialist senators have already tabled specific alternative proposals that the government should take seriously, and that a cross-party economic reform — even a watered-down one — would be better for Chile than a government bill passed by one vote that gets challenged in the Constitutional Tribunal or gutted in the fine print. Translation for American readers: think of a senior Democratic elder statesman calling on his party to cut a deal on a Republican economic bill rather than run out the clock. Notable that it's coming from the left, not the center.

"Reactivación: el costo de seguir esperando" — The Cost of Waiting

In DF's business pages, economist Gabriela Clivio makes the hard-numbers case for urgency: at 1–1.75% growth, Chile's public finances have no sustainable trajectory. The government's own projections show the National Reconstruction bill will widen the fiscal deficit for several years before any growth dividend shows up — meaning the window to act is narrow. The article's implicit argument is that the opposition, by playing for delay, is betting on economic pain to score political points; and the government, by accepting a negotiated compromise, gives up some of its maximalist agenda but gains a bill that might actually survive legal challenge.
