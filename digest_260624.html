Immigration Crisis

Senate Moves to Grill Ministers Over Chile's Missing Haitian Children

Chile's Senate is expected to summon cabinet ministers this week to testify about the country's most consuming scandal: more than 200 Haitian children who arrived on government-authorized charter flights in 2025 cannot be located in official records. The children were processed through Chile's Migration Service accompanied by adults whose family ties to them were never verified, then dispersed to addresses that nobody followed up on. A Comptroller General report identified breakdowns across multiple agencies — the Migration Service, Civil Aviation Authority, and the Under-secretariat for Children — and criminal complaints are open for human trafficking, migrant smuggling, and corruption.

The politics span two administrations: the flights and failures happened under ex-President Boric, but the Kast government now owns the investigation. The most alarming data point: one flight departed Haiti with 124 children and landed in Santiago with only 44 — 80 children vanished during an unscheduled Lima stopover. Ex-Ante reported this week that investigators have since located many of those children in good condition, pointing to "administrative collapse" rather than a live trafficking network — but the inquiry is far from resolved, and the moral gravity is not diminished by bureaucratic explanations.

Congress · Politics

Chamber Passes Accusation Against Ex-Finance Chief Grau — and Fractures the Right

In a 77–68 vote Tuesday, the Chamber of Deputies approved a constitutional accusation against Nicolás Grau, who served as Finance Minister under former President Boric. Chile's constitutional accusation is roughly the equivalent of a congressional impeachment: it allows Congress to censure and remove sitting or recent officials for alleged abuses of power. Grau is already out of office, so the practical stakes are lower, but the political fallout is real. The filing came from the Republican and Libertarian parties — the hard-right flank of Kast's coalition — who accuse Grau of concealing the true scale of Chile's fiscal deficit. Grau says it's politically motivated. The case now moves to the Senate. Center-right analysts at Ex-Ante argue the move backfired: the left unified in Grau's defense and the governing coalition is visibly divided.

Economy · Legislation

Kast's Tax-Cut Package Hits a Senate Wall as Opposition Demands Unbundling

The "Miscellaneous Law" — Kast's flagship economic reform — is stalled in the Senate after passing the Chamber with 90 votes. The bill bundles two very different things: wildfire reconstruction aid for the communities devastated by the 2024–25 fires (broadly popular) and sweeping corporate tax cuts (controversial) — dropping the top corporate rate from 27% to 23% by 2029 and locking in 25-year tax certainty for large investments over $50 million. Opposition senators are refusing to vote on the package as a whole; they want the reconstruction money passed separately so it cannot be used as leverage for the tax cuts. The government says the bundling is deliberate; critics call it a hostage strategy. Timeline uncertain.

Labor · Economy

Kast Pushes a 30-Hour Flex Workweek — Labor Unions Are Furious

The government placed urgent priority on a labor bill that would let employers and workers agree to schedules of up to 30 hours per week — roughly 6-hour days — with a requirement that employers give at least 24-hour notice before each shift. Billed as a job-creation tool in retail, tourism, and services sectors facing 9.1% unemployment, the measure is Chile's version of the gig-economy flexibility debate. Chile's main labor federation (CUT) is flatly opposed: they say it guts hard-won protections from the 40-hour workweek law passed under Boric and could slash wages and eliminate full-time benefits. The bill enters Senate committee debate this week.

Politics · Kast Government

100 Days In: Kast at 36% Approval, Promises Outrunning Reality

More than 100 days into his presidency, José Antonio Kast is polling at 36% approval and 57% disapproval (Cadem). The candidate who campaigned on rapid results for crime and economic growth has already made Chile's fastest cabinet change since the return of democracy in 1990 — firing his Security Minister after just 69 days — and has dialed back his 3.5–4% GDP growth target (Fitch projects 1.6% for 2026). His party lacks a Senate majority, making his legislative agenda dependent on negotiation. France 24 summarized the first 100 days as "turbulent," noting Kast is adjusting his promises to match what's actually achievable.

Science from Chile

Chile Is Building the Southern Hemisphere's Only Gamma-Ray Observatory Deep in the Atacama

Construction is advancing on the SWGO — Southern Wide-field Gamma-ray Observatory — in San Pedro de Atacama at 4,770 meters above sea level, just a few kilometers from the ALMA radio telescope. When complete, it will be the only facility in the Southern Hemisphere capable of detecting ultra-high-energy gamma rays emitted by black holes, neutron stars, supernovae, and gamma-ray bursts. The SWGO consists of roughly 3,700 water tanks fitted with light-sensitive sensors spread over nearly a square kilometer of desert plateau; particles from space hit water in the tanks and produce faint flashes of blue light that the sensors record. Its southern position means it's the only observatory that can study the center of the Milky Way — only visible from the Southern Hemisphere. An international consortium of 15+ countries is funding it, and Chile put up the best site. Astronomy is genuinely one of the things Chile does better than almost anyone on Earth.

The Clinic · Left-Leaning · June 18

"¿Dónde Están?" — Where Are They?

The Clinic's editors published a stark editorial demanding full accountability for the missing Haitian children. Titled simply "Where Are They?", the piece traces the failure through the Migration Service, the Civil Aviation Authority, and the Under-secretariat for Children under two successive governments — neither of which flagged what was happening until a journalist and the Comptroller General did. The editorial argues this is a test of moral seriousness: Chile is measured not just by whether its institutions fail (they sometimes will), but by how rapidly and honestly it responds when the victims are the most vulnerable. The Clinic frames the crisis as a symptom of the broader failure mode it has criticized for years: an administrative state that processes paperwork without moral attention to what the paperwork represents.

Ex-Ante · Center-Right · June 23

The Constitutional Accusation Against Grau Blew Up in the Right's Face

Ex-Ante — Chile's premier policy journalism site, broadly center-right — ran a sharp analysis arguing that the Republican and Libertarian parties committed a serious strategic error. Their goal was to punish the Boric-era left and signal fiscal accountability. The result was the opposite: the left rallied to Grau's defense, Chile Vamos (the mainstream right) is visibly uncomfortable voting for an accusation it considers overreach, and the opposition now has a "victim" narrative heading into the Senate phase. Ex-Ante's argument echoes something US readers will recognize: the harder-right flank of a coalition using procedural weapons that energize the base but alienate the center and strengthen opponents.
