What is "Caso Audio"?

The short version

Chile's most serious corruption scandal since the return to democracy, triggered by a leaked recording suggesting that a top-tier attorney was running a bribery operation spanning judges, tax officials, and politicians. The case opened in late 2023 and is still widening in 2026 — now pulling in President Kast's ambassador nominee to Israel.

How it works in Chile

In November 2023, a 105-minute audio conversation was leaked to prosecutors. In it, star defense attorney Luis Hermosilla appeared to discuss orchestrating bribes to judges and officials at Chile's Internal Revenue Service (SII) on behalf of wealthy clients. Hermosilla was not an outsider: he had been a personal adviser to former President Sebastián Piñera, sat on corporate boards, and represented some of Chile's richest families.

The recording set off a sweeping criminal investigation. Multiple judges were suspended. Several lawyers and businesspeople were charged with influence trafficking and money laundering. Hermosilla has been under house arrest since late 2023. Prosecutors are seeking 14 years in prison and expect a trial by late 2026. The case is formally called Caso Audio-Factop — "Factop" refers to a family-owned financial firm whose principals are also charged with separate crimes that became entangled in the same investigation.

Picture a tape surfacing in which a top Washington D.C. defense attorney — someone who had advised a recent president and counted senators and CEOs as clients — openly discusses bribing federal judges and IRS officials. That recording sets off a two-year DOJ investigation that touches the federal bench, Senate staffers, and lobbyists across party lines. That is roughly the scale and shock of Caso Audio in Chile.

Why it matters today

President Kast's newly nominated ambassador to Israel, Gabriel Zaliasnik — a prominent Santiago attorney and former president of the Santiago Chamber of Commerce — is under criminal investigation by the Valparaíso prosecutor's office. Prosecutors say WhatsApp chats between Zaliasnik and Hermosilla suggest Zaliasnik sought to influence judicial appointments. The government is defending Zaliasnik for now but acknowledged it would reconsider his nomination if he is formally charged.

For a government that ran partly on anti-corruption and clean-governance messaging, having an early appointee tangled in the country's premier corruption case is an uncomfortable signal — and the opposition is pressing hard on it.
