Politics · The Trafficking Scandal

Kast Summons All Three Branches of Government Over Suspected Trafficking of Haitian Children

The fast-moving scandal of the year took over Chilean headlines: prosecutors are investigating the organized entry of more than 200 Haitian minors who arrived in 2025, allegedly through a misused "family reunification" visa, with possible trafficking — and even, in the darkest version, organ trafficking — under examination. President Kast responded with an extraordinary move, summoning the Supreme Court, both chambers of Congress, the national Prosecutor's Office and the Comptroller General to a single table to coordinate a response. (The Contraloría, or Comptroller General, is Chile's constitutional watchdog over government legality — think the GAO and an inspector general rolled into one independent office.)

For an American reader, picture a president convening Congress, the Supreme Court, the Attorney General and a federal watchdog all at once over a child-trafficking case at the border — a deliberate show of "all hands, all branches." Prosecutors have widened the probe to three airlines that allegedly flew the children in. For a Kast government built on a hard-line immigration message, the case is both a vindication of its warnings and a stress test of whether the state can actually act.

Economy · The Budget Fight

Government Scrambles for Votes to Raise the Debt Ceiling — and the Math Is Tight

The Kast administration is hunting for votes to pass a bill letting it borrow more, the latest skirmish in a wider "megaproject" fiscal reform. The wildcard is Franco Parisi's populist PDG party, which is wavering, while the centrist Christian Democrats (DC) signal they'll negotiate with the Finance Ministry. The opposition, meanwhile, openly concedes it's in the legislative minority — even as it pursues a constitutional accusation (Chile's impeachment) against former finance minister Nicolás Grau. It's the familiar Washington spectacle of a debt-ceiling vote where a handful of swing legislators hold all the leverage.

Economy · Monetary Policy

Central Bank Holds the Key Rate at 4.5%, Signals "Risks Are Balancing Out"

Chile's independent Central Bank — the local equivalent of the Federal Reserve — left its benchmark interest rate parked at 4.5%, saying "the balance of risks for inflation has been evening out." Economists read it as a steady-as-she-goes stance and think the rate could stay put through all of 2026. After a stretch of inflation jitters, it's the kind of "we're comfortable waiting" message the Fed sends when it wants to cool speculation about its next move without committing to one.

Policy · Wages

Congress Signs Off on a Minimum-Wage Bump to 553,553 Pesos

Congress finalized an increase that lifts Chile's monthly minimum wage to 553,553 pesos (roughly US$600), an uptick of about 14,000 pesos, applied retroactively to May 1. Labor Minister Rau pledged the raise would fully offset the year's inflation. Chile sets its floor wage nationally through an annual government-labor negotiation rather than the state-by-state patchwork of the US — so when it moves, it moves for the whole country at once, and the headline figure becomes an instant political scorecard.

Politics · Coalition Strain

Kast Forces Out a Coalition Partner's Appointee, and the Friction Shows

President Kast removed Daniela Castro, the deputy minister for women, an appointee tied to Renovación Nacional (RN) — the more moderate, establishment-conservative party in his governing bloc. RN's "contained irritation," as one outlet put it, is an early sign of the strains inside a right-wing coalition that spans Kast's hard-line Republicans and the old-guard center-right. Picture a new president quietly pushing out a cabinet pick that a key ally in his own coalition had championed: the alliance holds, but the resentment lingers.

Society · Animal Welfare

After "Ley Cholito," Here Comes "Ley Balto": Jail Time for Animal Abusers

Building on the earlier "Ley Cholito" animal-protection law, a group of deputies wants a new "Ley Balto" that would put serious animal abusers behind bars for real and bar them from jobs working with animals. Chile has a soft spot for its street dogs (the quiltros), and naming laws after specific animals is a very Chilean way of turning public affection into legislation — a reminder that not every bill in Congress is about the budget.

Business · Only-in-Chile

A Toy Company Is Building an Open-Air Mall Just for Kids

Ansaldo Toys, one of Chile's biggest toy distributors, is betting roughly 50,000 UF (a few million dollars) on an unusual format in the coastal town of Concón: an open-air "strip center" designed exclusively around children, slated to open in 2027. It's a quirky retail experiment at a moment when plenty of Chilean malls are struggling — equal parts nostalgia play and gamble that families still want somewhere physical to take the kids.

"Divisions in the Opposition Over the Naín-Retamal Law"

La Tercera's editorial board argues that the right is fracturing over how far to push its flagship "Naín-Retamal" agenda — the police self-defense and tough-on-crime laws — with RN floating a "2.0" version that others resist. For a US reader, it reads like a center-right editorial scolding its own side for blowing a winning law-and-order issue through internal squabbling.

"El costo de la sobreideología"

Writing in El Mercurio, Paulina Vodanovic — president of the Socialist Party and a senator from the left — argues that letting ideology override pragmatism carries a real political and governing cost, a pointed bit of self-criticism aimed partly at her own side after the left's electoral losses. It's the kind of "we have to stop talking only to ourselves" column you'd hear from a US Democrat urging the party to trade purity for results.
