Politics

Chile's Right Splinters: "Cowardly Right" vs. "Courageous Right" Threatens Kast's Governing Majority

President José Antonio Kast spent much of this week scrambling to hold his own governing coalition together after a public rupture between the far-right and center-right flanks. The immediate trigger: Kast's Republican Party pushed a constitutional accusation against former left-wing Economy Minister Nicolás Grau — and it went down in flames in the Senate, with zero of the four charges approved. It was the worst defeat for such an offensive since Chile's return to democracy in 1990. The failure exposed a festering internal feud: Kast loyalists from the Republican Party began calling their center-right partners in Chile Vamos (UDI and Renovación Nacional) the "la derecha cobarde" — the cowardly right — because key Chile Vamos senators broke ranks and refused to support the accusation. The slur, borrowed from Spain where Vox used it against the Partido Popular, lit up social media and embarrassed government ministers who belong to the "cowardly" parties.

Kast convened an emergency meeting with ruling-party presidents at La Moneda on Friday, acknowledging "coordination problems" and dangling the prospect of formalizing a broader governing coalition. His framing was optimistic — "Chile will return to being a beacon" — but the underlying tension remains. Think of it as the Freedom Caucus vs. the establishment Republicans: Kast's base wants to use every parliamentary weapon against the left, while the pragmatic wing knows a razor-thin Senate majority (26 of 50 senators) means they need every vote for the Megarreforma and can't afford fratricidal fights. Historian Lucía Santa Cruz, a conservative voice, put it plainly: the insults antagonize the very people without whom Kast couldn't have won or governed.

Education / Society

Students March on Ministry of Education — Protests Against Kast's School Reform Heat Up

Chile's national college-student federation, Confech — roughly equivalent to a US national student union — organized a major march in Santiago today, targeting the Ministry of Education. The trigger is Kast's proposed education reform, which would pause the expansion of the public school network and increase subsidies and autonomy for private schools. Critics say it reverses fifteen years of pro-public-education policy. The march follows earlier mobilizations in March (3,500 attendees, 14 detained) and June (4,500 attendees, 35 detained) that ended with riot police deploying tear gas and water cannons. Today's is expected to be similarly tense.

The fight maps onto a debate Americans will recognize: public versus private schooling, school choice, and the question of whether government money should follow the student (to any school) or fund the public system. Chile had been moving steadily toward a more public model since 2011's student uprising became a national movement — Kast's reform is seen by opponents as rolling that back. The government argues it is restoring parental freedom and removing bureaucratic obstacles to quality education.

Congress

Megarreforma Enters Line-by-Line Battle: Deadlines Extended, Kast Wants Done by July 31

After barely squeaking through the Senate's general concept vote (26–23, a single vote) last week, Kast's flagship economic omnibus bill now moves into the detailed, article-by-article committee fight. The Environment Committee begins Monday, July 6; the government agreed to extend amendment submission deadlines — Environment through Monday night, Labor through Wednesday, Finance through Friday. Kast has said he wants the full bill dispatched from the Senate before August begins, warning he'll push through with a one-vote majority if needed.

The environment chapter is the most contested. Senator Iván Moreira (UDI, the center-right party — recall, the "cowardly right") said this week he sees "no space" for compromise on the environmental deregulation provisions, while government negotiators are trying to find middle ground to avoid losing the swing votes they need. The bill bundles corporate tax cuts from 27% to 23%, faster environmental permitting for mines and infrastructure, and reconstruction funds for fire-affected regions. The opposition calls it "a disguised corporate tax reform dressed up as reconstruction."

Crime / Security

First Extradition in Murdered Venezuelan Officer Case: Tren de Aragua Suspect Charged

Dayonis Orozco Castillo, extradited from Colombia, was formally charged this week in Santiago for his alleged role in the kidnapping and murder of former Venezuelan Lieutenant Colonel Ronald Ojeda — a prominent critic of Nicolás Maduro who disappeared from his Santiago apartment in 2024. Orozco is linked to Los Piratas de Aragua, a Tren de Aragua offshoot. The prosecution confirmed that extraditions from the United States (suspect "El Turko") and from Colombia (Carlos Bobby and a third suspect) are also in progress.

In a separate but related development, an 11-member Tren de Aragua cell that operated in the Viña del Mar–Limache–Villa Alemana corridor of Valparaíso was convicted this week on all charges, including murder, kidnapping, drug trafficking, and money laundering. The ringleaders face more than 150 years in prison. The dual developments — an extradition and a conviction — mark rare wins for Chilean prosecutors in what has been a grinding, multi-year effort to dismantle the Venezuelan gang's foothold in Chile.

Economy / Security

Chile's Illegal Economy Moves $5.7 Billion a Year — Business Lobby Calls It a "Structural Threat"

Chile's top business federation, the CPC (Confederación de la Producción y del Comercio — roughly the US Chamber of Commerce), released a major report this week warning that illegal markets have become a "structural threat" to the country's development. Drug trafficking, contraband, piracy, and informal labor together move an estimated US$5.7 billion annually and cost the state more than US$1.5 billion in lost tax revenue. Cigarette smuggling alone costs the treasury US$1 billion a year. The CPC says what were once isolated incidents have metastasized into a parallel economic ecosystem operated by organized crime — one that directly undercuts legal businesses.

The report, titled "Por un Chile sin Economía Ilícita," argues this is no longer just a law-enforcement problem but an economic one: illicit markets undercut legal businesses on price, which weakens formal companies, which reduces tax revenue, which further defunds the enforcement and regulatory capacity needed to combat the problem. The CPC is asking the government to treat illegal-economy enforcement as economic policy — not just a crime issue. The report drops at a particularly awkward moment, as Chile is simultaneously dealing with five months of economic contraction and a governing coalition fracturing over unrelated fights.

Interesting & Offbeat

Atacama Museum Gets a Geek Upgrade: Console History Meets Pre-Columbian Mummies in Copiapó

The Museo Regional de Atacama — inaugurated earlier this year in Copiapó as one of Chile's largest-ever public cultural investments (roughly 19 billion pesos, ~$21 million USD) — launched a surprise exhibition this week: "Replay," a walk through the history of video game consoles from the 1970s through today, housed in the same building as pre-Columbian mummies, meteorites, and Andean archaeological artifacts. The umbrella event, "Atacama Juega: Cultura Geek," ran July 2–4 and included a companion exhibition on the history of toys. The museum's seven permanent rooms cover the Atacama's geology, paleontology, archaeology, history, biodiversity, and astronomy — so it turns out the desert that gave us the world's most powerful telescopes was ready to display the Atari 2600 as well.

"Las decisiones del gobierno: tenemos un elefante en la habitación"

A column published today in El Mostrador argues that Kast's Megarreforma openly defies recommendations from Chile's Independent Fiscal Council (roughly equivalent to the nonpartisan Congressional Budget Office) and contradicts peer-reviewed evidence from education researchers. The piece notes that a government whose approval rating has slid to roughly 30% cannot credibly claim a popular mandate for dismantling fifteen years of public-education progress. The "elephant in the room" is a government driving through sweeping structural changes with a single-vote Senate majority while the economy contracts, public support erodes, and its own coalition is publicly calling each other cowards.

Ex-Ante  ·  Center / Analysis  ·  Pepe Auth · July 2, 2026

"¿Correrá la misma suerte la centroderecha que la centroizquierda?"

Veteran centrist analyst Pepe Auth — a former congressman and member of the center-left PPD, writing now in center-right Ex-Ante — asks whether the right's current governing coalition is building the same self-destruct mechanism that eventually drove the Concertación and Frente Amplio into the wilderness. Auth argues the "cowardly right" insults directed at Chile Vamos from Kast's Republicans mirror the ideological purity tests that fractured the Concertación from the inside. His conclusion is pointed: the left didn't lose power because the right was better — it lost because its factions couldn't agree on what winning meant. The right appears to be learning exactly the same lesson, the hard way.
