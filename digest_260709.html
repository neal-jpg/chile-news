National · History

Chile Marks 200 Years of the Presidency — Kast Gathers All Living Ex-Presidents at La Moneda

Today, July 9, 2026, marks exactly two centuries since Manuel Blanco Encalada became Chile's first constitutionally elected president in 1826 — an unbroken institutional line that now reaches its 36th holder, José Antonio Kast. The government is commemorating the bicentennial with three ceremonies: an 11 a.m. flag tribute at Plaza de la Ciudadanía honoring the 77 Chilean soldiers killed at the Battle of La Concepción on this date in 1882 during the War of the Pacific; a formal state lunch at La Moneda at noon hosted by Kast and First Lady María Pía Adriasola; and a 3 p.m. central ceremony at the Museo Nacional de Historia Natural.

Former presidents Gabriel Boric (2022–2026) and Eduardo Frei Ruiz-Tagle (1994–2000) confirmed attendance, as did Cecilia Morel and daughter Magdalena Piñera Morel representing the late Sebastián Piñera. The notable absence is Michelle Bachelet — Chile's only two-term president since the return of democracy — who is abroad pursuing her candidacy for UN Secretary-General. The image of right-wing Kast hosting left-wing Boric for a shared institutional moment carries weight in a divided country: think of it as a US Presidents Day ceremony where Trump, Biden, and Obama all show up at the White House out of respect for the office itself.

Economy · Politics

Tax Deal Locks In Key Senate Votes for "Megarreforma" — But a Court Challenge Looms

The PPD party's Senate caucus reached an agreement with Finance Minister Jorge Quiroz on the biggest sticking point in Kast's National Reconstruction Plan — "tax invariability," which guarantees investors that tax rates won't change for years. Under the deal, investments of $500 million or more keep the original 25-year guarantee; mid-size projects ($350M max) get 15 years; smaller ones (up to $100M) get 10 years — significantly scaled back from the original flat 25-year offer. A 1.5% permanent surcharge was also added for companies opting into the regime. The PPD caucus won't take that provision to the courts and effectively delivers four crucial Senate votes ahead of the scheduled floor vote July 21–22.

The full Megarreforma bundles several major changes: a corporate tax cut from 27% to 23% (phased over several years, similar in structure to the 2017 US Tax Cuts and Jobs Act corporate rate reduction), an employment tax credit, a temporary VAT break on new home purchases, and property tax relief for homeowners over 65. But not everyone is on board: the PPD's national leadership overruled its own senators and announced it will still challenge the bill at Chile's Constitutional Court (TC) — roughly equivalent to the US Supreme Court. The Socialist Party has signaled the same. The TC challenge is the opposition's Hail Mary to block a bill they can't stop in the chamber.

Security · Foreign Policy

Kast's Anti-Crime Alliance Now Spans Six Nations — Uruguay Latest to Join the "Santiago Agreement"

Chile has been quietly building a regional security bloc: the "Santiago Agreement," a diplomatic framework for coordinating against organized crime that now counts Argentina, Peru, Bolivia, Ecuador, Paraguay, and Uruguay as signatories. Presidents Kast and Uruguay's Yamandú Orsi cemented the bilateral piece at a meeting in Montevideo on July 2, and Kast used his Mercosur summit appearance to push for a full hemispheric architecture — coordinated border management, shared police intelligence databases, and joint pursuit of illicit money flows.

The target is transnational gangs — most prominently the Venezuelan Tren de Aragua, which became a flashpoint in US politics under Trump and has been operating in Chile's northern cities. Chile's Border Shield (physical barriers across the Arica, Tarapacá, and Antofagasta regions) is nearing completion under a 90-day deadline Kast set upon taking office. Kast has framed the initiative as part of his broader pitch that "the right-wing government is the only one serious about security" — and the regional alignment with non-left-wing governments (Uruguay's Orsi is center-left but pro-cooperation) gives it a bipartisan sheen.

Santiago Gets No Rain — But Salamanca Just Logged 15 Inches in One Season

While Santiago endures yet another dry, smoggy winter day, the town of Salamanca in Coquimbo region (about 300 km north) has recorded 379 millimeters of rainfall this season — roughly 15 inches, and more than Santiago typically receives in an entire winter. The Río San Juan overflowed, flooding homes and triggering landslides. The contrast captures Chile's brutal precipitation geography: a capital city chronically starved of water is only a few hours from towns getting emergency-level rainfall. Climate scientists note the pattern is consistent with a long-term trend of uneven distribution — more intense precipitation in concentrated bursts, longer dry spells in between.

"It's Cold in the City" — Chile's Winter Homelessness Problem Demands a State Response

The Clinic's editorial board argues the Chilean state must stop looking away from the 21,750 people living on the street — a number the 2024 Census counted and that has grown every year since 2020. In winter, when Santiago overnight temperatures fall to single digits, the editorial says this is a matter of life and death, not a scheduling question. The piece calls on the Kast government to treat street homelessness as an emergency, not a social footnote, noting that housing policies favoring homeowners and investors do nothing for people who have no address at all. Left-leaning; critical of both previous governments and the current one for failing to reverse the trend.

El Líbero · Right-leaning · July 2026

"Fiscal Prudence Can't Stay a Right-Wing Preference"

Conservative columnist Pepe Arias writes in El Líbero that Chile's long-term debt risk is too large for one political coalition to solve alone, and that sound public finances must become a shared national commitment — not just the governing right's hobby horse. Arias argues that left-wing parties undercut their own social programs by running deficits that force future austerity, and calls on the opposition to negotiate budget discipline across the aisle rather than simply blocking Kast's Megarreforma. A rare piece on the right that frames fiscal restraint as a gift to the left's goals, not their enemy. Right-leaning; critical of opposition obstruction but also implicitly of Kast's spending plans.

Kids 🌲 Outdoors

Teleférico Metropolitano · Cerro San Cristóbal

The cable car up San Cristóbal is one of Santiago's best clear-day winter outings. The ride is short (~6 minutes) and the views of the Andes from the summit are best midday, when the morning inversion usually lifts. Parquemet trails are open for walking; the Zoo is inside the park. Winter hours roughly 10 am – 6 pm. Round-trip about $6,000 CLP for adults, discounted for kids. Metro: Baquedano, then walk across Pío Nono bridge and follow signs — or take the lower funicular from Pedro de Valdivia Norte. Note: check the air quality first — on environmental alert days (like today) the views are hazy.

Zenith Sins — The Mirror

Nicolás Letelier, keyboardist for the legendary Chilean funk-rock group Chancho en Piedra, has unveiled the debut album of his new band Zenith Sins: "The Mirror," a conceptual prog rock/metal record structured as a journey through the human mind. The five-piece (Letelier on keys, Diego Ponce on vocals, Javier Flores guitar, Cristián Vargas bass, Sebastián Farías drums) draws comparisons to Queensrÿche and mid-era Tool — ambitious, theatrical, and distinctly Chilean in its lyrical DNA. For fans of: concept records, Porcupine Tree, late Fates Warning.

Lalo Ibeas (ex-Chancho en Piedra) → Pascual Arroyo

To complete the picture: Lalo Ibeas, Chancho en Piedra's frontman, launched his own solo project earlier this year under the name "Pascual Arroyo" — a cumbia-heavy departure from the funk-rock that made him famous. The debut album "Lo Comido y Lo Bailado (Me Van a Echar)" draws on Chilean popular tradition and features a warm, dancefloor-friendly sound that's nothing like his previous work. The three simultaneous departures (Lalo → cumbia, the Ilabacas → indie, Letelier → prog metal) suggest Chancho en Piedra's three-year hiatus is quietly becoming permanent.
