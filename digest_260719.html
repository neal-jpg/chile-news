Top Headlines
Emergency · National

Storm Kills at Least 4 and May Produce July's Heaviest Rainfall Since Records Began

A chain of frontal systems backed by an intense atmospheric river has battered Chile for six days running, killing at least four people and knocking out power to more than 500,000 homes across ten regions. In Santiago, the deaths include a man electrocuted in Cerro Navia when he leaned against a metal bus shelter energized by a downed cable, and another victim found near the Central Market. Two more deaths were reported in the Biobío and La Araucanía regions.

The national weather service considers the event "one of the most significant in recent years" — and it doesn't rule out July 2026 ending as the rainiest July since Chilean rainfall records began in the 1860s. Santiago typically gets about 60 mm of rain in an entire July; some stations may record double that this weekend alone. SENAPRED (Chile's FEMA) ordered evacuations in the La Ensenada sector of Ovalle, Coquimbo, after the Río Grande overflowed; at least 2,500 people are isolated in various regions. Santiago Mayor Mario Desbordes activated emergency operations and reported flooding in multiple city neighborhoods.

Public Safety

A Carabinero Died Hunting the Man Who Killed Another Carabinero in 2020 — Chile Demands a Tougher Law

Cabo Marco Cosme Barquero, 32, died Saturday after being shot in the face on July 15 during an operation in Valdivia to capture Carlos Cancino Tapia — known as "El Rana" — one of the fugitives linked to the 2020 murder of Cabo Eugenio Naín. The government declared three days of national mourning (July 18–20); flags are flying at half-staff across Santiago today. President Kast called it a "cowardly murder" and promised Cancino Tapia will "spend the rest of his life in prison."

The death immediately reignited the debate over the 2022 Naín-Retamal law — Chile's "Blue Lives Matter" statute — with right-wing lawmakers and the president both demanding a "Ley Naín 2.0" with even tougher mandatory sentences and expanded self-defense rights for officers. The political sting: the Communist Party (PC) had been pushing to repeal parts of the existing law, and critics are now calling that effort "indignant" and "complicit with crime." The PC's position is now a major liability heading into the next congressional session.

Economy · Politics

Megarreforma Clears Senate, Heads to Chamber — Then the Courts

Thursday's Senate vote (26–24) sent Finance Minister Quiroz's Ley de Reconstrucción Nacional — the megarreforma — to the Chamber of Deputies for a third and final review. The bill's five pillars include rebuilding communities devastated by the 2024 fires in Ñuble, Biobío, and Valparaíso; reactivating construction; and a gradual corporate tax cut from 27% to 23%. Quiroz called it "the project that makes growth possible."

But the opposition has already filed constitutional challenges for multiple clauses, and the government itself said it may challenge the Senate's late-added ban on charging interest on interest (anatocismo) — the unusual spectacle of a government suing over its own law. Think of it as a bill that passed Congress but will spend years in the federal courts before anyone knows exactly what it actually does. The Chamber vote is expected this week.

Interesting / Offbeat
Labor · Storm

A Worker Vanished Crossing a Stream During the Storm. Now His Employer Is Under Investigation.

A construction coordinator disappeared in Curepto, a small town in the Maule region, while trying to cross a swollen stream during the height of the storm. Rescue operations continue. The Labor Directorate announced it will investigate his employer — citing a legal obligation on companies to continuously assess risks and implement safety measures during weather emergencies, and requiring proof that such measures were in place.

The case highlights a broader question the storm raised across Chile: when workers are required to be in the field during dangerous conditions, who is legally responsible? The investigation may set a precedent for employer liability during climate emergencies.

Opinion / Editorial
"Política Pirata"
The Clinic · Left-leaning · Danilo Herrera · July 18

Herrera argues that the Kast government used exactly the kind of backroom deal-making it spent years attacking when the center-left Concertación did it — all to squeeze the megarreforma through the Senate 26–24. The frame is "pirate politics": any administration that claims to be above old-school horse-trading while simultaneously working the phones to flip two votes at midnight is just flying a cleaner flag. A sharp critique from the left that will sting because it's not factually wrong.

"Security: A Necessary Agenda for Long-Term Growth"
Ex-Ante · Right-leaning · Roberto Cases · July 2026

Cases makes the economic case for the Ley Naín 2.0 push: rising crime has cost Chile roughly 0.6 percentage points of annual GDP growth for a decade, meaning the economy would have averaged 2.6% annual growth instead of 2% had crime not risen. The column reframes the security debate — usually pitched as values or law-and-order — as a supply-side economic argument, and it hits harder in the week of Cabo Cosme's funeral. Think Reagan-era "broken windows" economics, but with Chilean data.

Pop Indie

Francisca Valenzuela — "SOS"

Released July 9. The third and most emotionally raw single from her forthcoming album MALDITA (out July 30 on Frantastic Records). Piano-forward with swelling strings; the lyric maps anxiety, disconnection, and the weight of other people's expectations. The album's overarching theme is feminine rage through motherhood and postpartum — a creative space Valenzuela has been building toward across this pre-release campaign. Previous singles "BUGAMBILIA" and "MALACARA" were more defiant; "SOS" is the collapse that comes after. For fans of: Natalia Lafourcade's quieter moments, Alanis Morissette's confessional mode. One of Chile's most-watched domestic releases of the year.

Rock Folk/Folklórico 🎸 Scene

Red Rockódromo 2026 — 95 Bands, 16 Regions, One National Circuit

Chile's arts council (CNCA) launched the 2026 edition of Red Rockódromo on July 10 in Copiapó, Atacama — the driest desert on earth, which makes a certain poetic sense for a music circuit. Selected from 1,628 applications across all 16 Chilean regions, 95 projects (bands and solo artists) will travel a full national circuit running through October, including stops in Magallanes at the southern tip of the continent. It's Chile's closest equivalent to a government-funded battle of the bands — built on the premise that the music scene is too concentrated in Santiago. Watch the Rockaxis coverage for the full lineup and regional schedules.

Hotel Records — "Compilado Chile 2026"

Hotel Records issued its 2026 compilation earlier this year after an open call that drew more than 100 submissions; 15 acts were selected from across the country. Rock and indie are the spine, with excursions into folk and electronic. The result reads like a postal snapshot of where Chile's independent scene is right now — less polished than the big-label stuff, more interesting. It's free on Bandcamp and a good starting point if you want to explore beyond the names you already know.
