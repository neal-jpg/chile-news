Left and Right United: A Rare Cross-Party Coalition Moves to Reform Chile's Impeachment-Like Tool

In a moment of genuine political rarity, senators spanning Chile's entire ideological spectrum — from the establishment center-right UDI to the left-wing Frente Amplio — jointly filed a constitutional reform Friday to overhaul the acusación constitucional, Chile's closest equivalent to an impeachment proceeding. The proposal would double the signatures required to launch an accusation against a minister, from 10 to 20, and add a new prerequisite: an interpellation (a formal question-and-answer session in Congress) must happen first, to exhaust political dialogue before the nuclear option is deployed. The reform also raises the vote threshold for approval and tightens the legal standard, requiring infractions to be "manifest and fully verifiable."

The context is impossible to ignore. Kast-aligned libertarians and Republicans are currently pursuing a constitutional accusation against Nicolás Grau, former Finance Minister under President Boric. That accusation — over alleged fiscal mismanagement — has generated broad unease even among some Kast supporters, who worry the tool is being used as a political cudgel rather than a genuine oversight mechanism. Think of it as the Chilean equivalent of both parties agreeing to reform the filibuster after decades of using it as a weapon: the tool isn't going away, but both sides have gotten burned enough to want guardrails. For their part, Kast's Republicanos went further — seizing the moment to call for restoring the old constitutional quorum that existed before 2019, an even stricter standard that would require a supermajority.

Security · Law

Kast's "Vandalism Registry" Gets Fast-Tracked: 33 Behaviors, Lost Benefits, and Sharp Pushback

The Kast government formally submitted legislation to create a public national registry of people convicted of vandalism and "incivilities" — and gave it suma urgencia status, meaning Congress must act within 30 days. The registry would cover 33 categories of conduct, ranging from serious crimes like arson and drug trafficking all the way down to graffiti, public drinking, and failing to pay the metro fare. People listed could lose social benefits including subsidized education and pension top-ups. The bill commands 87% popular support in polls, reflecting deep public frustration with visible disorder in Chilean cities.

Critics from the opposition and the judiciary are raising constitutional red flags. A BioBioChile column called the law "an open door to arbitrariness," arguing that undefined terms like "incivility" give authorities dangerously broad discretion. One judge attacked the registry publicly, warning it would punish poverty rather than crime. The opposition faces a difficult position: voting against a bill that 87% of Chileans support is politically treacherous, even if the legal concerns are legitimate. Think of it as a Chilean version of stop-and-frisk or broken-windows policing, but formalized in legislation and tied to benefit eligibility.

Politics · Center-Right

UDI's Power Broker Steps Aside: Longueira Hands the Wheel to Alessandri in Unity Ticket

Pablo Longueira — the man who effectively built Chile's center-right UDI party and led it as president for six years — announced Thursday he will not run for party president after all. Instead, he'll take the secretary general slot on a unified ticket headed by younger congressman Jorge Alessandri. The announcement was made at the Jaime Guzmán memorial — a deeply symbolic venue for the UDI, named after the party's founding intellectual who was assassinated in 1991. The move is designed to project unity heading into what the UDI expects to be a period of heightened influence under the Kast government.

The backstory is typically Chilean: Longueira is a brilliant but polarizing figure who carries real baggage — a past investigation into campaign finance irregularities, a political style that many in his own party find divisive. His allies say privately that the optics of a Longueira-led UDI would hand ammunition to the left. So he steps back to secretary general — still in the room where decisions are made, still pulling levers, but not the face. Think of it as Karl Rove choosing to be chief of staff rather than run for Senate. The UDI's internal election is coming in the next few weeks.

Economy · Labor Market

Unemployment Stuck at a 5-Year High as Central Bank Cuts Growth Forecast to Near-Zero

Two data points released this week together paint a bleak picture of the Chilean economy Kast inherited. The Banco Central's June monetary policy report (IPoM) slashed the 2026 GDP growth forecast from 1.5–2.5% down to just 1–1.75%, blaming a weak first quarter in copper mining, agriculture, and fishing, plus lower summer tourism. Separately, labor market data shows unemployment holding at 9.1% — the highest in nearly five years and the 38th consecutive month above 8%. Nearly a million Chileans are out of work; youth unemployment is at 22.8%, and for young women it's 28%.

Kast entered office promising to cut unemployment to 6% and grow the economy at 4% annually — targets that now look very far away. The Central Bank's silver lining: it projects 2027 could see 2–3% growth if investment recovers. But that requires the broader economic confidence Kast is still working to establish. For context: Chile's economy grew at 12% in the post-pandemic boom year of 2021, making today's near-stagnation feel especially stark. The $6.2 billion emergency borrowing bill, which cleared the lower house last week, now heads to the Senate as the government scrambles for short-term fiscal room.

Fiscal · Congress

$6.2 Billion Debt Bill Heads to Senate — and the Vote Will Be Tight

After the Chamber of Deputies approved the Kast government's request to borrow an additional $6.2 billion — with critical support from centrist DC and PDG legislators — the bill now moves to the Senate, where the math is more uncertain. Finance Minister Jorge Quiroz framed the borrowing as unavoidable: the 2026 budget was structurally underfunded by the outgoing Boric administration, leaving a $3.8 billion deficit gap, $900 million in exchange-rate losses, and $1.5 billion in mandatory state payments to cover. The opposition, led by the left-wing Frente Amplio and Partido Socialista, says the government is using the fiscal mess to justify cuts and simultaneously push a tax reform that favors higher earners.

This is Chile's most politically combustible fiscal fight in years — roughly equivalent to a U.S. debt-ceiling standoff where the new party in power is blaming the previous administration for the shortfall while the opposition accuses them of manufacturing the crisis for political cover. Senate debate is expected to begin next week. Passage is likely but not certain; the government needs centrist votes to cross the finish line.

Why Is Every Elite Footballer at the World Cup Wearing Pink Cleats?

The Clinic dug into one of the 2026 World Cup's strangest aesthetic phenomena: Nike, Adidas, and Puma all chose hot pink as their flagship boot color for the tournament. The result is that nearly every elite player on the field — from Mbappé to Vinicius Jr. to most of the Chilean national team — is running around in what looks like the same shade of rose. The piece profiles the exceptions (Cristiano Ronaldo, apparently committed to white) and traces the marketing calculus: sports footwear brands coordinate their flagship colorways to maximize visibility, and this cycle someone decided pink was the color. Chile has its own World Cup angle this week: referee Cristián Garay made his tournament debut, officiating Canada's 6–0 demolition of Qatar in Group B.

Tomorrow Is the Solstice — and Santiago's Mountain Season Is at Its Peak

June 21 is the winter solstice in the Southern Hemisphere — the year's shortest day, and the official start of astronomical winter. But for Santiago residents, the practical meaning is ski season: Valle Nevado, La Parva, and El Colorado are all less than 90 minutes from the city and received fresh snowfall this week. Forecasters are calling for a wetter-than-average winter thanks to El Niño conditions, which is good news for the Andes snowpack and the drought-stressed reservoirs that supply Santiago's water. If you've been meaning to go — this weekend is a strong option, just expect traffic on the access roads.

"The Vandalism Registry and the Degradation of the Rule of Law"

An op-ed in El Mostrador takes direct aim at Kast's registry bill, arguing that legally undefined terms like "incivility" hand the government broad discretionary power to strip benefits from anyone who runs afoul of loosely written rules. The author's sharpest line: "There will be no less vandalism due to a registry that deprives rights from those who vandalized or protested publicly; there will be more exclusion and more accumulated rage." The piece frames the bill not as public-safety policy but as "a bet on cruelty" — punishing visible poverty and dissent while doing nothing to address the economic drivers of disorder. Worth reading alongside the 87% approval number: popular support for a law and constitutional soundness of a law are not the same thing.

FT: Chile's New Right-Wing Government Is Looking for a "Fresh Start" After a Rocky Honeymoon

The Financial Times published an analysis of Kast's first months in office, republished by Diario Financiero: the government's security-first image is holding up in polls, but the economic picture — growth near stagnation, unemployment at a five-year high, and a $6.2B emergency debt request — is making the broader narrative harder to sustain. The piece notes that Kast won the election largely on economic frustration with Boric's government, and that the pressure is now on him to deliver visible results. His administration is betting that toughness on crime can substitute for economic progress while it waits for conditions to turn. That bet gets harder to hold as the months pass.
