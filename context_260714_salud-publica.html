Chile's Public Health System — Fonasa, Isapres, and the Waiting-List Crisis

The basics. Chile runs a dual health insurance system. Every salaried worker pays 7% of their wages into health coverage and must choose between two options: Fonasa (Fondo Nacional de Salud), the government insurer, or an Isapre (private health insurance company). About 80% of Chileans use Fonasa; roughly 18% use Isapres. The rest go uninsured.

How it works in Chile. Fonasa patients can see public-system doctors at heavily subsidized rates, but they must join a queue. For routine checkups or emergency care, the system works reasonably well. For specialist consultations or non-urgent surgeries — replacing a knee, removing a gallbladder, repairing a hernia — the queues can run hundreds of days. Isapre subscribers get faster access but pay much higher premiums (often two to three times the Fonasa contribution), making private insurance a middle-class luxury.

The American analogy. Imagine Medicaid — the low-income U.S. health program — was mandatory for 80% of the country and funded by a payroll tax, but chronically underfunded relative to demand. Meanwhile, 18% of people had real private insurance with tolerable wait times. That's roughly Chile's split. The key difference: there's no Medicare equivalent for the elderly, and no broad employer-sponsored coverage market. Fonasa is it for most people, cradle to grave.

The waiting-list crisis. As of mid-2026, nearly 400,000 people are waiting for surgery in the public system — the worst backlog on record — and 2.5 million are on some kind of healthcare waiting list (surgeries, specialist visits, or ongoing treatments). A joint report from the Centro de Estudios Públicos (CEP) and CIPS released July 14, 2026, warns the crisis goes deeper than queue length: Chile's cancer care infrastructure is under-resourced, and 35% of all healthcare spending now comes directly out of families' pockets — unusually high for an OECD member country.

Why it's politically explosive. Long Fonasa queues push lower-income patients into private care they can barely afford, creating out-of-pocket debt. President Kast ran on fiscal discipline; critics argue the 2026 budget cuts and sluggish reform have lengthened waits. An extraordinary 84% of Chileans in the CEP survey said they fear being unable to afford healthcare not covered by their insurer — a figure that crosses income and party lines.
