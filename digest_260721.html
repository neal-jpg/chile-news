Top Headlines
Emergency

Storm Death Toll Hits 10 as Chile Declares State of Disaster in Desert North

Chile's worst winter storm in at least two decades claimed its tenth life on Tuesday, with four more people still missing and 17 injured. Nearly 100,000 residents remain cut off from road access across the Coquimbo and Atacama regions — two of Chile's driest territories, where the storm dumped a month's worth of rain in an hour. More than 1,100 homes are destroyed or heavily damaged, and 150,000 customers have lost electricity. Preliminary damage estimates put the bill at roughly $400 million USD.

President Kast issued a formal estado de catástrofe (state of disaster) for the two regions on Monday, unlocking military deployment for rescue and logistics and allowing the Finance Ministry to redirect budget funds without waiting for Congress. The Army is operating ground convoys to isolated communities while naval vessels work coastlines where roads have washed out. SENAPRED (Chile's version of FEMA) confirmed the toll Tuesday morning; search and rescue operations continue in flooded ravines outside La Serena and Copiapó — the regional capitals of Coquimbo and Atacama, both larger than Albuquerque, NM.

Economy

Chile Edges Toward Recession as Kast Disapproval Hits 58%

Chile's economic activity index (a monthly GDP proxy) fell 0.9% in May — the fifth consecutive monthly contraction — pushing Latin America's wealthiest per-capita economy close to a technical recession (defined as two straight quarters of negative growth). The Central Bank now projects annual GDP growth of just 1–1.3% for 2026. Unemployment has climbed to 9.4%, and economists say formal private-sector hiring has essentially frozen: companies aren't adding workers, and the informal labor market is expanding to absorb the slack.

The political fallout is now visible in the polls. A July Cadem survey — Chile's equivalent of a Gallup tracking poll — put President Kast's disapproval at 58% and approval at 38%, with the economy displacing public safety as the top voter complaint. This matters because Kast ran in 2025 as a pro-market, business-friendly candidate who would reactivate growth after the left's economic stumbles. Voters elected him as a kind of Ron DeSantis–style economic manager; the numbers say it isn't working out that way.

Legislature

Congress Scrambles on Reconstruction Bill as Finance Ministry Reroutes Funds

Finance Minister Quiroz confirmed Tuesday that the government is reallocating existing budget reserves to cover immediate emergency costs from the storm — without waiting for Congressional approval. Meanwhile, a "national reconstruction and economic reactivation" bill that had been inching through the Senate all month is being fast-tracked to fold in disaster-area relief provisions. The bill was originally designed to boost housing construction and create formal jobs; the government now wants it to also include expedited permits for storm-damaged home rebuilding in Coquimbo and Atacama.

Think of it like Congress simultaneously debating the Infrastructure Bill while FEMA is flying helicopters over a hurricane zone. The Senate is under pressure to pass something quickly, but opposition senators argue the reconstruction package lacks the accountability mechanisms needed to prevent contracting fraud — a recurring issue after Chile's 2010 earthquake, when billions in reconstruction contracts ended in criminal investigations.

Culture

Metro Santiago Invites All Chileans to Design 21 Murals for Future Línea 7

"La Ruta del Encuentro" — an open contest launched July 14 by Culture Minister Francisco Undurraga — invites any Chilean artist, muralist, or collective to submit a proposal for one of seven large-format murals that will adorn the 19 stations of Santiago's future Línea 7. The remaining 14 murals will come from invited artists. Submissions are open through September 14; seven winning designs receive two million pesos each (~$2,100 USD). A 21-member jury from art, architecture, and communications selects finalists; the public votes in late October.

This is one of the most ambitious public-art commissions Chile has undertaken — comparable in scale to the mural programs on New York City's MTA or DC's Metro system, but notably open to emerging and non-professional voices. The line itself, still under construction, will connect far-southeastern Santiago to the city center.

Chile Still Has a Law Giving Husbands Default Control Over Marital Assets

A column this week by former Undersecretary for Women Daniela Castro (The Clinic, July 17) is drawing renewed attention to a quirk in Chilean family law: the default marriage property regime is still the sociedad conyugal, a Napoleonic-era system from Chile's 1857 Civil Code that gives husbands legal administration of joint marital assets unless spouses affirmatively choose a different regime at the time of marriage. Think of it as a law requiring married women to have a husband's sign-off on joint financial decisions — still on the books, and still the default if you don't opt out. The Senate passed a reform abolishing it in 2024, but enabling legislation has stalled under the Kast government, which has not prioritized moving it forward.

Opinion / Editorial

The Clinic · Left-leaning · July 17

"The Danger of What Gets Normalized"

Former Undersecretary for Women Daniela Castro writes that the current political climate is quietly normalizing ideas that erode gender equity — using the stalled sociedad conyugal reform as exhibit A. Her argument: it's not just that the law hasn't been fixed; it's that the conversation about fixing it has been deprioritized so completely that many Chileans don't know the problem still exists. She frames this as a broader pattern of "soft rollback" — not dramatic reversals, but benign neglect that lets discriminatory defaults persist. A pointed critique of how conservative governance can hold back social progress without making a single loud move.

The Clinic · Center-right · July 18

"It's Not That We Don't Know — We Just Can't Say It"

Jaime Mañalich — former Health Minister under President Piñera (think HHS Secretary), now a columnist — uses the July storm as a case study in Chile's institutional communication failure. His argument: Chile has excellent meteorological data and knows well in advance when major storm systems are coming, but government agencies consistently fail to translate that technical knowledge into clear, actionable public guidance. The forecasters knew this storm was historic; the messaging was muddled. Mañalich is center-right and no fan of the current government's management style, making this a rare "my own side is bungling this" critique — harder to dismiss as partisan.

Indie Rock Various

Various Artists — Compilado Chile 2026 (Hotel Records × Algorecords)

The first joint release from the merger of Hotel Records Chile and Algorecords — two of the country's most important indie labels — drops as a 15-track snapshot of where the Chilean underground is right now. More than 100 acts submitted; 15 made the cut. Algorecords has been nurturing Chilean alternative since the early 2000s (think Sub Pop scale for Chile); Hotel Records brings a newer wave. The result is a genre-spanning sampler: rock, dreampop, folk-pop, noise, and hybrids. Streaming on all platforms — a strong starting point if you want to explore what's happening well below the mainstream radar.

Folk/Folklórico Classical

Alfilera — "Savia" (music video, from Detrás de las Palabras)

Cellist and composer Alfilera released the music video for "Savia," a track from her debut album — one of the more distinctive sounds in the Chilean experimental scene right now. Her music sits at the intersection of contemporary classical, Chilean folk, and art-song: textured, unhurried, with the cello doing things the instrument usually doesn't. Think Joanna Newsom if she were from the Maule valley and listened to Arvo Pärt. Quiet and unusual in the best possible way. Available on all platforms.

Metal

Mr. Matanga — "Apágate Ya!" (live studio single)

Nu-metal from Osorno — a small city in Chile's southern lake district, better known for its German immigrant heritage and rain than for heavy music. Mr. Matanga recorded this single live in session in July: chaotic, cathartic, and DIY in a way that recalls Limp Bizkit or early Korn more than current metal. A left-field choice for the scene, which tends toward prog or alt-metal, but the raw energy is undeniable. For fans of early 2000s nu-metal who don't mind that the lyrics are in Spanish.

📊 Charts

Context: Where Chile Sits in Its Own Charts

Rosalía's four Movistar Arena shows this week have dominated Chilean music conversation — and the Spotify Chile Top 50 largely reflects that, alongside the usual Latin urban/reggaeton dominance. Chilean artists are a small minority on the national chart; the vibrant local underground that produces releases like the ones above coexists almost entirely separately from what the algorithms surface to casual listeners. If you want to hear what Chile actually makes, the Rockaxis website, Futuro Chile radio station, and the "Rock Chileno por Rockaxis" Spotify playlist are more useful than any chart.
