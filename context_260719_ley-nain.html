The Naín-Retamal Law — and Why Everyone Is Calling for "Ley Naín 2.0"

Where it started. In August 2020, Cabo Eugenio Naín was shot and killed in Ercilla, a small town in the Araucanía region, during an operation against armed groups linked to the Mapuche land conflict. In May 2021, Carabinero Claudio Retamal was shot in the face during a similar operation in the same region. Both cases became symbols of how exposed Chile's police officers were when facing armed suspects — and how the existing legal framework left officers in legal limbo when they fired their weapons in self-defense.

What the law does. Congress passed the Ley Naín-Retamal in 2022. Its two main effects: it stiffens prison sentences for anyone who attacks or kills a police officer (adding years regardless of other circumstances), and it creates a presumption that an officer acted in self-defense when certain conditions are met — a shooting during a pursuitof a dangerous suspect, for example. Critics called that second part a "license to shoot first"; supporters said it was the bare minimum needed to protect officers who face split-second decisions in life-or-death situations. Think of it like the debate over qualified immunity in the US, but sharper — a law specifically triggered by officers getting killed in the line of duty.

Why it's controversial. The Communist Party (PC) — part of Chile's opposition — has argued that the self-defense presumption goes too far and could shield officers who use excessive force. The PC introduced legislation to partially repeal that provision. To Chile's right wing, this was already inflammatory; after the death of Cabo Marco Cosme in July 2026, it became politically explosive. If you want an American analogy: imagine the Democratic House proposing to limit "Blue Lives Matter" sentencing enhancements the week after a high-profile officer funeral.

What "Ley Naín 2.0" would mean. Several right-wing lawmakers have proposed a tougher follow-up law. Key ideas under discussion: expanding the self-defense presumption to cover more scenarios, making sentences for killing a police officer non-negotiable (no plea deals, no time-off), and potentially introducing capital punishment — a proposal by libertarian MP Johannes Kaiser that has little chance of passing but signals the mood on the right. The political push comes directly from the death of Cabo Marco Cosme Barquero, 32, who died July 18, 2026 after being shot during an operation to capture one of the suspects in the original Naín murder six years earlier.
