What is Chile's IMACEC — and Why Does It Keep Falling?

What it is

The Indicador Mensual de Actividad Económica (IMACEC) is Chile's monthly economic activity index, published by the Banco Central de Chile roughly four weeks after the end of each month. It measures output across all major sectors — industry, commerce, services, and mining — adjusted for seasonal swings, and is expressed as a percentage change versus the same month one year earlier.

How it works in Chile

Chile reports official quarterly GDP numbers like most countries, but the IMACEC fills the gap between quarters by giving markets and policymakers a fast read on whether the economy is expanding or contracting. A single negative IMACEC reading means the economy was smaller than a year ago in that month. The Banco Central also publishes a seasonally-adjusted monthly change figure, which lets analysts see the trend without the calendar noise.

The indicator covers most of the economy but excludes some agricultural activity; a separate mining IMACEC is often flagged because Chile's copper sector can swing the overall number dramatically. When copper mines slow production — due to labor disputes, equipment failures, or low grades — the mining IMACEC drags the headline figure down even if the rest of the economy is holding up.

The American analogy

Think of it as a hybrid between the US monthly jobs report and the ISM Manufacturing Index: a fast-moving, closely watched number that tells investors and politicians whether the economy is heating up or cooling down — before the official quarterly GDP figure confirms it.

Why it matters right now

Chile posted its fifth consecutive monthly IMACEC decline in May 2026 (–0.9% year-on-year). That five-month streak has raised serious questions about whether Chile is heading into a "technical recession" — the standard definition being two consecutive quarters of GDP contraction. The first quarter of 2026 already contracted 0.5%. If the second quarter follows suit, the technical threshold is met.

Finance Minister Jorge Quiroz insists June was a "turning point" driven by recovering copper prices and improving consumer confidence. But the IMF cut its 2026 Chile growth forecast this week to 1.8%, and unemployment sits at 9.4% — the highest in years. Whether the IMACEC turns positive in June (data due in early August) will be the most watched economic number in Chile this summer.
