What is Chile's "Proyecto de Reconstrucción Nacional"?

The short version

The Proyecto de Reconstrucción Nacional (National Reconstruction Bill) is President Kast's sweeping omnibus legislation — a single mega-bill that bundles together major changes in taxes, immigration enforcement, housing policy, employment law, social spending, and higher-education financing, all packaged as one take-it-or-leave-it proposal.

Why call it "Reconstruction"?

Kast's political brand is built on the idea that he inherited a country in crisis — economically stagnant, fiscally irresponsible, and institutionally weakened by four years of Gabriel Boric's left-wing government. The "Reconstruction" framing declares an emergency that justifies speed and scale. Rather than debating each reform topic separately, the administration argues Chile needs to act fast across multiple fronts at once.

Think of it as roughly analogous to how a new US administration might pass a sweeping reconciliation bill — but instead of just a budget measure, this bundles immigration rules, tax law, education policy, and social programs into a single vote. It's a high-stakes legislative bet: win it all at once, or risk losing each piece individually.

What's actually in it

The core elements include: supply-side tax changes aimed at stimulating investment; a hardened immigration enforcement framework; housing and urban development measures; adjustments to labor law; and a restructuring of how higher education is financed (particularly the controversial CAE student loan system). Critics note that lumping these together forces legislators into an all-or-nothing vote, reducing their ability to support some reforms while opposing others.

The political argument against it

Left-leaning critics at outlets like El Mostrador argue the omnibus approach is a power play: by framing Chile as "broken," Kast creates artificial urgency that concentrates legislative power in the executive branch. Each of these topics — immigration, taxes, education — would normally generate months of independent hearings, amendments, and debate. Packaging them together shortcircuits that process. It's the legislative equivalent of attaching riders to a must-pass spending bill in Washington.

Where it stands

As of late June 2026, the bill is advancing through Congress but has generated significant pushback even from within Kast's right-wing coalition. The government is pushing for approval before the end of September. Its passage would be Kast's biggest legislative win; failure would be a major setback heading into the second half of his four-year term.
