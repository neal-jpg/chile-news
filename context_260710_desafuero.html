Desafuero: What Congressional Immunity Means in Chile

In Chile, sitting senators and members of Congress are protected by a constitutional shield called fuero parlamentario — parliamentary immunity. This protection means a legislator cannot be criminally arrested, charged, or tried without a special court procedure first stripping that immunity. That procedure is the desafuero.

Think of it like the US Constitution's "Speech or Debate" clause, which protects members of Congress from civil lawsuits over legislative acts — except Chile's version is broader and covers all criminal proceedings, not just legislative conduct. It exists to prevent governments from arresting opposition lawmakers on politically motivated charges. The trade-off is that it can also shield legislators who commit genuine crimes from prompt accountability.

The desafuero process works like this: a prosecutor or court asks Chile's Court of Appeals to lift the accused legislator's immunity. The appeals court reviews the evidence and votes. If it votes to strip the immunity, the legislator can then be formally charged and faces suspension from Congress. The Supreme Court can review the decision on appeal. The whole process can take weeks to months — which matters enormously if that senator's vote is crucial on pending legislation.

Why it matters now: Senator Miguel Ángel Calisto (an independent aligned with the governing coalition) is being investigated for alleged fraud against the state. The Puerto Montt Court of Appeals was set to hear the desafuero request in July 2026, but prosecutors reopened the underlying fraud investigation, triggering a procedural pause. Because Calisto's vote is essential to the government's thin Senate majority on the megarreforma economic package, his potential suspension — or even his medical absence — could derail a bill Kast has staked his economic agenda on. Unlike a US senator, if Calisto is suspended, his seat cannot be filled by a party substitute; independents who enter the Senate outside a party pact leave a permanent gap.
