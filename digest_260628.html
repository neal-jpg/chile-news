Senate Vote on Grau Impeachment is Tuesday — and It Has Already Cracked Chile’s Right Wide Open

Chile’s Senate votes Tuesday on whether to convict former Finance Minister Nicolás Grau on a constitutional accusation — and the political fallout from the effort is already bigger than the case itself. The charges: that Grau, serving under ex-President Boric, deliberately understated the fiscal deficit and concealed spending pressures in his final budget, leaving the incoming Kast government holding a worse balance sheet than reported. The accusation passed the Chamber of Deputies 77–68 on June 23, pushed by Libertarian and Republican lawmakers from the radical wing of the governing right. But it landed like a grenade inside the coalition: moderate “Chile Vamos” parties — particularly Renovación Nacional (RN), the centrist partner in the governing alliance — are furious. RN publicly accused the Republicans of “political thuggery” for leaning on its lawmakers to back the libelo. Several Chile Vamos senators in the upper chamber have already signaled reservations.

Think of it as the Chilean version of the Trump–establishment Republican split. Kast’s Republicanos and the Libertarians want aggressive action to brand the left as corrupt and incompetent; the Chile Vamos wing worries this kind of political warfare will damage institutions and blow back on them electorally. For Grau’s part, he says he committed no wrongdoing and will face the Senate “with tranquility.” A two-thirds majority is required to convict — the same bar the U.S. Senate faces for removing an impeached president — and analysts say the prosecution arrives at the upper chamber “weakened.” Tuesday’s vote is as much a test of whether moderate Chilean conservatism can hold the line against its own radical flank as it is a verdict on Nicolás Grau.

Chile Sends Rescue Brigades and Aid Plane to Earthquake-Ravaged Venezuela

Twin earthquakes — magnitude 7.1 and 7.5, striking within a minute of each other — devastated Venezuela’s coast last week. Chile, no stranger to catastrophic seismic events, responded quickly: rescue brigades flew to La Guaira and a Chilean Air Force cargo plane delivered humanitarian supplies. Interior Minister Máximo Pavez confirmed the teams are operating autonomously in Venezuela for up to ten days. President Kast called Venezuelan interim president Delcy Rodríguez personally to convey Chile’s solidarity and pledge support.

The response is notable given the complicated political relationship between Kast’s far-right government and Venezuela’s Maduro-era leadership — a reminder that humanitarian need tends to cut through diplomatic friction.

Economy · Labor

Kast’s “Severance for All” Reform Divides Business Groups and Unions

The Labor Ministry is actively studying a reform to replace Chile’s existing severance system with an “indemnización a todo evento” — severance paid regardless of why a worker leaves, including voluntary resignation. Under the proposal, employers would contribute roughly 1.8% of salary each month into a personal fund the worker could draw down at any separation. That’s a significant departure from the current system, which pays one month per year worked (capped at 11 years) only when the employer dismisses someone. Business groups are cautiously supportive; the CUT — Chile’s main labor federation, the rough equivalent of the AFL-CIO — worries the change could weaken worker bargaining power and shift risk onto employees.

Código Azul Activated Across Seven Regions as Overnight Temps Head Toward -4°C

The government activated “Código Azul” (Blue Code) for seven regions — Santiago Metro, Valparaíso, O’Higgins, Maule, Ñuble, Biobío, and La Araucanía — as temperatures in valleys and foothills are forecast to drop to -4°C. The protocol, which triggers when temps hit at or below 0°C (or under 5°C with snow or sleet), mobilizes additional emergency shelter capacity and social services teams to reach people experiencing homelessness before conditions become dangerous. It’s a routine winter measure in Chile, activated multiple times per season, but a reminder that winter depth is now fully here.

Coquimbo Is Becoming Chile’s “City of Murals” — One Giant Canvas at a Time

The coastal city of Coquimbo, better known for seafood and clear skies than gallery openings, is quietly building one of Chile’s most ambitious public art projects. A new phase will add 1,350 square meters of large-format mural space across four residential towers, extending the Spencer Condominium open-air gallery that has already made the neighborhood a destination. The city is investing roughly 95 million pesos in this phase. It’s a rare feel-good municipal story — and a genuine reason to make the drive up the Pan-American to the Norte Chico coast if the Santiago winter has you restless.
