What is a Constitutional Accusation?

The short version

A acusación constitucional is Chile's version of impeachment. It's a formal charge that lawmakers file against a current or former government official, alleging they violated the constitution or the laws. If enough votes are there, the official can be removed from office — or, if they've already left, barred from holding public office for up to five years.

How it works

A group of members of the Chamber of Deputies (Chile's House of Representatives) files the charges. The Chamber then votes on whether the accusation proceeds — a simple majority is enough. If it passes, the case moves to the Senate, which acts as the jury and votes on guilt. A two-thirds majority is needed to convict.

Unlike the US, Chile allows constitutional accusations against former officials for up to six months after they leave office. That's why ex-Economy Minister Nicolás Grau — who served under the previous Boric administration — can be targeted even though he's no longer in government.

Think of it like US impeachment, but faster, more common, and available even after someone has already left office. It's used more frequently in Chile and carries a lower political bar to file.

Why it matters today

Two far-right parties — the Libertarios and Republicans — filed an accusation against Grau over how the public debt was calculated and disclosed to Congress. Even within Chile's right, the move is controversial: the mainstream center-right coalition (Chile Vamos) is resisting joining, worried the fight will poison the atmosphere in Congress just as the new Kast government is trying to pass a major economic reform.

Has this happened before?

Yes, frequently. Constitutional accusations have been filed against judges, ministers, and even President Piñera (twice). Most don't succeed in the Senate, but filing one is politically damaging on its own — it forces debate, dominates news cycles, and signals that the opposition means business.
