Politics

Chile's House Passes Impeachment-Style Charge Against Boric's Ex-Finance Chief — Senate Vote Next

Chile's Chamber of Deputies voted 77–68 on June 23 to approve a constitutional accusation against Nicolás Grau, who served as Finance Minister under former President Boric. The right-wing coalition backing President Kast accused Grau — a technocrat from the left-wing Frente Amplio (think: progressive wing of the Democratic Party) — of unconstitutionally misrepresenting government debt projections in a public finance report. Grau flatly denied wrongdoing, saying "we will go calmly to the Senate, I have committed no illegality."

The case now moves to the Senate, which is expected to vote during the upcoming "semana regional" (Chile's equivalent of a congressional recess week, when lawmakers return to their districts). A conviction requires a majority in the Senate and would bar Grau from holding public office for five years. The vote was tighter than expected and exposed cracks in Kast's coalition — several members of allied parties expressed reservations about the process, leading a cross-party group to separately propose reforms that would raise the bar for filing constitutional accusations in the first place.

Legislation

Senate Barely Clears Kast's Big Economic Bill — Now the Real Fight Begins

The Chilean Senate voted 26–23 on June 24 to approve the general concept of Kast's sweeping "National Reconstruction" bill — a wide-ranging package combining corporate tax cuts (27% down to 23%), deregulation of investment permits, a new universal childcare system, and labor market reforms. With just one vote to spare, the government can celebrate the threshold but has very little margin in what comes next: an article-by-article debate where any single provision can be amended or stripped by the opposition.

Commentators are already drawing comparisons to Argentina's experience with Milei's "Omnibus Bill" — passed in principle with fanfare, then hollowed out clause by clause in Congress. Amendment deadlines in Chile's Senate run to July 6. The Frente Amplio and Socialist opposition have signaled they intend to fight every contentious article.

Economy

Unemployment Hits 9.1% — Highest in Five Years — As Government Rushes 50,000-Job Plan

Chile's unemployment rate climbed to 9.1%, the highest level in nearly five years, driven by more people entering the job market than the economy is creating positions for. Women are hardest hit at 10.5% unemployed. A Cadem survey found 82% of Chileans believe there is a "labor emergency." Kast's response: an interministerial employment board tasked with generating 50,000 new jobs before October, targeting women and the most affected regions.

The government is also floating a controversial overhaul of Chile's severance system — replacing the existing lump-sum payment owed upon dismissal with an employer-funded savings account paid out at any job separation (think: a portable 401k-style redundancy account). Business groups support the idea as a way to reduce hiring risk; the CUT labor federation calls it a rollback of worker protections disguised as a reform. A Cadem poll found 45% in favor, 45% opposed.

Society

100,000+ March in Santiago Pride Parade — First Under Far-Right Kast Government

More than 100,000 people filled the Alameda on Saturday for Santiago's 26th annual Pride March, making it one of the largest turnouts in years — and the first major LGBTQ+ mobilization under President Kast, whose campaign emphasized traditional family values. Organizers demanded reform of Chile's anti-discrimination Zamudio Law and warned of growing threats to LGBTQ+ rights domestically and globally. Representatives from 10 embassies, including the US, UK, Germany, and the Netherlands, marched in solidarity.

The march was largely peaceful, though a confrontation broke out near a Santiago metro station between the official organizer group (Movilh) and a dissident bloc over ideological differences within the movement. No serious injuries were reported. Organizers characterized the large turnout as a direct response to the political climate under the new government.

Courts · Education

Chile's Highest Court Strikes Down Key Parts of Kast's "Protected Schools" Law

Chile's Constitutional Tribunal — the equivalent of the US Supreme Court — declared four key provisions of Kast's "Protected Schools" bill unconstitutional on June 24, handing the government its first major judicial setback. The struck-down clauses would have allowed school principals to search students' backpacks for weapons or drugs and established an intervention list for "disruptive schools." The court ruled the measures violated students' rights and exceeded executive authority. Seven of the court's members were appointed under the previous Boric government.

Education Minister Sichel responded defiantly, saying he would use every mechanism the law permits to act on security threats in schools — even without the struck-down provisions. The Kast administration has made school safety a signature theme, citing rising incidents of violence and drug trafficking inside schools.

Holiday Exodus: 400,000 Cars Flee Santiago for the Long Weekend

San Pedro y San Pablo, the Catholic feast day honoring St. Peter and St. Paul, falls on a Monday this year — giving Chileans a three-day weekend and triggering the seasonal exodus from the capital. Authorities projected over 400,000 vehicles departing the Santiago metropolitan region, with Route 68 toward Viña del Mar and Route 5 Norte toward La Serena seeing the heaviest congestion. Highway agencies activated a special traffic plan with reduced overnight tolls for trucks and reversible lanes. An estimated 900,000 people are traveling by intercity bus. If you stayed behind: enjoy the rare calm. Return traffic is expected to peak tonight and tomorrow morning.

Chile's Public TV Is Broke. The Government Wants to Rescue It — On Its Own Terms.

Televisión Nacional de Chile (TVN) — the country's state-owned broadcaster, roughly equivalent to PBS in the US — is effectively insolvent after a decade of declining ad revenue and mounting debt. The Kast government has proposed a $30 million endowment fund to keep the channel alive, but has simultaneously signaled it wants to restructure TVN's governance model and convened a special commission to define a sustainable long-term financing approach. Critics on the left worry the rescue will give Kast-aligned directors editorial influence over the only nationwide free-to-air public broadcaster — an uncomfortable dynamic regardless of which party is in charge.
