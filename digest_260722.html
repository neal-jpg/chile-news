Top Headlines
Economy · Politics

Kast's flagship economic reform clears Congress — corporate taxes heading down, constitutional fight just beginning

Chile's Chamber of Deputies voted 85–59 on Tuesday to approve the bulk of President José Antonio Kast's sweeping tax and economic overhaul — the biggest legislative win of his government since taking office in March. The centerpiece: a phased cut in the corporate income tax from 27% to 23% by 2029, bringing Chile closer to the OECD average. It also includes a one-year VAT exemption on home purchases and a controversial 20-year tax-invariability clause that freezes fiscal conditions for major investors — think a long-term "no-touching" guarantee from the federal government.

One disputed article on municipal compensation was sent to a joint committee for a final fix, but the bill is effectively law. Finance Minister Jorge Quiroz — the reform's main architect — called it a "decisive victory for growth." The left is already preparing a challenge at Chile's Constitutional Tribunal, roughly equivalent to the US Supreme Court ruling on whether a new law crosses constitutional lines. Critics say the reform costs Chile about $4 billion annually in lost revenue that would otherwise fund education, healthcare, and pensions.

Emergency · Reconstruction

10 dead, 100,000+ isolated — and the hardest-hit regions were left out of the reconstruction fund

Chile's July 2026 winter storm has now killed at least 10 people, left 4 missing, and isolated more than 100,000 residents in some of the country's most vulnerable communities. Over 23,000 homes sustained damage across multiple regions. Class suspensions continue today in dozens of communes — including several in the Metro Region — as officials assess structural damage to schools.

The government announced a $400 billion peso (roughly $400 million) reconstruction fund, but only for Ñuble, Biobío, and Valparaíso. Finance Minister Quiroz called it "impossible" to add Atacama and Coquimbo — the two regions that took the worst beating from the storm. Regional lawmakers from those areas immediately pushed back, calling it a political abandonment. The state of catastrophe decree remains active in Coquimbo and in Atacama's Huasco province.

Politics · Polls

Crisis gives Kast a three-point bump — but he's still at or near record-low approval

A Publimicro survey this week showed President Kast's approval ticking up to 39% as the public rallied around the emergency response. But that bounce comes off a grim baseline: a Pulso Ciudadano poll earlier this month put him at just 28.9% — the lowest of his presidency. Disapproval consistently runs above 55%.

Political analysts note that Housing Minister Iván Poduje, not Kast, has been the government's most visible face during the crisis — on the ground in flooded zones while the president coordinated from La Moneda. It's the FEMA-director dynamic: when disaster response works, the field manager gets the credit. Whether the storm week gives Kast a sustained bump, or whether the controversy over leaving Atacama and Coquimbo out of the reconstruction fund erodes it, is the political question of the week.

World Cup · Local Color

Argentine expats in Providencia went from World Cup party to police intervention after losing the final to Spain

A pizzeria near Parque Bustamante became a minor international incident Sunday night when a crowd of Argentine residents gathered to watch the 2026 FIFA World Cup final. Argentina lost in extra time to Spain — and then a Chilean in the room shouted "Arriba España," which in this context is roughly the equivalent of rubbing a Sox loss in a Yankees fan's face using their own rival's success. Carabineros arrived as the situation escalated; an Argentine fan ended up on the ground after pushing the Chilean. One Argentine woman who reportedly identified herself as Chilean was detained.

The videos went viral. Argentine social media has been fuming ever since, with posts insisting that Chile — which didn't even qualify for the tournament — somehow still managed to ruin the final. It's the kind of Río de la Plata grudge match that doesn't care what country you're physically located in.

Opinion & Editorial
Right-leaning

"La megarreforma, un imperativo moral"

El Líbero · Free-market conservative

A columnist at El Líbero — Chile's main free-market conservative outlet — argues that the corporate tax cut isn't just sound economics, it's a moral obligation. The piece frames Chile's investment drought and years of economic stagnation as injustices that fall hardest on the poor, echoing a Reagan-era supply-side logic: growth lifts all boats, and the tax hikes under successive center-left governments from 2014 onward killed the growth engine. The reform, the author argues, is the government finally doing what it was elected to do.

"La megarreforma y el mito del crecimiento"

BioBioChile · Center-left · July 15

A July 15 column at BioBioChile takes direct aim at that logic, calling the growth-through-tax-cuts argument a myth. The author cites research showing no consistent evidence that corporate tax reductions produce economic growth, and argues the reform will cost Chile around $4 billion annually — money that would otherwise fund education, healthcare, and pension programs. The implicit comparison drawn is to US Republican tax policy: popular with business lobbies, opposed by polling majorities, and questionable on the empirical evidence. Rock Indie

Red Rockódromo 2026 — Chile's national indie circuit is now underway across all 16 regions

Chile's Ministry of Culture selected 95 bands from 1,628 applicants for the 2026 Rockódromo circuit — a government-funded national indie rock network running July through November. Think of it as a well-run Battle of the Bands at a regional scale, with artists getting actual workshop mentorship from Denisse Malebrán and Cristóbal Briceño (Juana Fe). The circuit kicked off July 10 in Atacama (logistics complicated by the storm) and spans named festivals across all 16 regions: Astromúsica in Coquimbo, Día del Rock Chileno in the RM, Ruido Austral in Magallanes, and more. One finalist per region advances to the XXII Rockódromo Festival in Valparaíso in November. Good entry point for discovering regional acts that don't make it onto commercial radio.
