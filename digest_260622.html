Immigration Crisis

More Than 200 Haitian Children Are Missing in Chile's System

Chile is in crisis mode after investigators found that more than 200 Haitian minors who arrived in the country on charter flights between 2022 and 2025 cannot be located in official government records. UNICEF issued an urgent public demand on June 17, calling on Chile to account for the children immediately. The immigration and child-welfare agencies processed the arrivals — spread across 76 charter flights carrying a total of 2,142 minors — but never maintained adequate follow-up to confirm children were safe and living with appropriate adults.

Chile's Servicio de Migraciones filed a criminal complaint alleging possible child trafficking connected to the organized charter flights. But the Defense Minister walked back the alarm: police (PDI) have located most children found so far in good condition, pointing to "administrative disorder" rather than an active trafficking network. The political fallout spans two governments — the flights and failures occurred under Boric, but the Kast administration now owns the investigation and cleanup. The Senate is expected to summon ministers to testify this week.

Economy

Chile's Economy Shrinks for a Fourth Straight Month

Chile's monthly GDP indicator (IMACEC) fell 1.2% in April — the fourth consecutive month of contraction. Unemployment has climbed to 9.1%, the highest since the pandemic peak of 2021, with women bearing the brunt at 10.5% jobless. Mining output, usually Chile's economic engine, cratered 11.8%. Think of it as a prolonged US recession concentrated in the industrial heartland: the headline numbers keep coming in negative and the government's playbook for reversing it remains contested.

Fiscal Policy

Kast Asks Congress to Borrow $6.2 Billion More — While Also Cutting Taxes

The Kast government asked Congress to raise Chile's debt ceiling by an additional US$6.2 billion on top of the US$17.4 billion already authorized for 2026, citing lower tax revenues and pending spending obligations. The Chamber (lower house) approved it, but Bloomberg warned the move carries a "reputational cost" for Chile's fiscal credibility. Critics note the uncomfortable timing: the government is simultaneously pushing to cut corporate taxes from 27% to 23%. Chile's public debt is projected to breach its own self-imposed "prudent" ceiling by 2028.

Public Security

Chile Launches 7 Specialized Task Forces Against Organized Crime

Security Minister Martín Arrau unveiled a sweeping crackdown: seven task forces targeting kidnapping, hired assassinations, cybercrime, border and port trafficking, financial crime, illicit markets, and violence in the southern Macrozone (the Mapuche conflict zone). The plan also deploys police saturation to 50 "critical neighborhoods" and proposes extending police detention authority from 12 to 24 hours — a controversial "stop and hold" expansion. Kast also hosted foreign ministers from Argentina, Bolivia, Ecuador, and Peru for a regional summit on transnational organized crime.

Demographics

Chile's Birth Rate Falls Below One Child Per Woman — A First for the Americas

Chile's National Statistics Institute projects a fertility rate of just 0.92 children per woman for 2026, placing it among the ten lowest on Earth — below South Korea and Japan. By 2028, more Chileans will die each year than are born: a demographic milestone no Latin American country has ever crossed. The gap between how many children Chileans say they want (~2) and how many they actually have is one of the widest globally. On June 17, the government launched the "Chile Renace" (Chile Reborn) plan and convened an expert commission, but demographers say the trend is nearly impossible to reverse in the short term.

Science from Chile

The World's Biggest Camera Is in the Atacama — and It Already Changed Astronomy

The Vera C. Rubin Observatory, perched in Chile's Atacama foothills at 2,600 meters, began full survey operations this year — and in a single 10-hour test run captured 10 million galaxies and discovered 2,104 previously unknown asteroids, including 7 near-Earth objects. Its 3.2-gigapixel camera is the largest ever built. Chile's Atacama Desert hosts more of the world's major telescopes than anywhere else on Earth, a fact of genuine national pride: the country literally sits under the clearest skies on the planet.

Opinion & Editorial
El Mostrador · Left-Leaning · June 21

"A Country Administered, But Intellectually and Morally Stunned"

A columnist at the left-leaning El Mostrador argues that Chile isn't a failed state — it's a stunned one. Administration is everywhere: programs get executed, crises get processed, ministers get shuffled. But intellectual ambition and moral courage have gone missing across the entire political spectrum, from Boric's cautious center-left to Kast's security-first right. The Haitian children crisis is cited as emblematic: a slow-moving catastrophe that wasn't unfixable — it just required someone to decide to fix it, and nobody did.

Libertad y Desarrollo · Right-Leaning · June 2026

Right's Take: Kast's Security Agenda Gets Credit — But Fiscal Discipline Is Non-Negotiable

Libertad y Desarrollo — Chile's most influential right-wing think tank, roughly analogous to the Heritage Foundation — offered a broadly positive read of Kast's State of the Nation address, praising the security focus and the corporate tax cut proposal. But the analysts issued a pointed fiscal warning: Chile built its reputation as Latin America's most credible economy on disciplined public finances, and the combination of expanded borrowing and tax cuts risks squandering that advantage. The message from the right's economic wing: the security agenda is welcome, but the fiscal math has to add up.
