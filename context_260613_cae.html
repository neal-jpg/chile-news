What is the CAE?

The short version

The CAE (Crédito con Aval del Estado, or "state-guaranteed credit") is Chile's main student-loan program. Created in 2005, it let students borrow from private banks to pay for university, with the government guaranteeing the loan. It became the dominant way working- and middle-class Chileans paid for college — and, over time, a political lightning rod.

Why people hate it

Interest was steep, banks profited heavily, and hundreds of thousands of graduates ended up buried in debt — some paying for decades on degrees that never paid off. "Forgive the CAE" became a rallying cry of the 2011 student protests that launched the careers of today's left, including former president Gabriel Boric.

Think US federal student loans, but with private banks collecting fat margins under a government guarantee — so it carries the resentment of America's student-debt fight plus the anger at Wall Street, rolled into one.

Why it matters today

Boric promised to wipe out the CAE and replace it with a new system (the FES). That replacement stalled, and now the courts are the battleground: judges are issuing scattered, contradictory rulings on debt-collection cases — some siding with borrowers, some with the state. Meanwhile, former Boric ministers are publicly admitting they over-promised on forgiveness they couldn't deliver.

The bottom line

"End the CAE" was one of the Chilean left's signature pledges. The fact that it remains unresolved — and is now playing out one court ruling at a time — is a running embarrassment for the outgoing government and an easy target for the new Kast administration.
