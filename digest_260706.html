Politics & Legislature

Kast's flagship megarreforma hits its critical Senate amendment deadline today

Today is pivotal for the biggest legislative bet of President José Antonio Kast's young government. By 8 PM tonight, the Senate's Environment Committee must finalize amendments to the so-called megarreforma — the National Reconstruction and Economic Development bill bundling tax cuts, investment incentives, fire-reconstruction aid, and deregulation into one sweeping package. The Labor Committee has until Wednesday; Finance closes July 10. Then the full Senate votes article by article.

The stakes are high. The bill cleared its first Senate vote June 24 by a razor-thin 26–23 — essentially party-line, so every defection matters. Negotiations have been rough: Finance Minister Jorge Quiroz resisted a cross-party table for weeks before Senate President Paulina Núñez (RN) brokered one, extending the deadline for deals on the tax provisions. Think of this as Chile's version of US budget reconciliation: one or two defections can sink the whole thing.

Politics

Libertarian leader fires warning shot at Kast's coalition partners

Johannes Kaiser, head of the small but vocal Libertarian National Party (PNL), declared Sunday that President Kast "cannot do without Chile Vamos but shouldn't let them set the pace." Inside an already tense governing alliance, the comment landed like a grenade. Chile Vamos — the traditional center-right bloc, the largest wing of the coalition — fired back: "Either join the government or stop trying to steer it from outside."

The spat was ignited days earlier when Chile Vamos senators voted to sink a constitutional accusation against former Boric-era finance minister Nicolás Grau — infuriating the libertarian and Republican wings who had pushed it. Think of it as libertarian-aligned Republicans telling an establishment GOP president his moderates are too slow, while refusing to formally join the caucus.

Economy

Finance minister: Chile dodges technical recession as activity turns positive

Finance Minister Jorge Quiroz said Sunday that Chile will avoid a "technical recession" — two straight quarters of negative GDP — because June's monthly activity index (Imacec) should come in positive, ending a five-month losing streak. It's a relief for a government that won partly on a promise to revive growth, only to face a near-recession in its first months.

The caveats are big. The government already cut its full-year growth forecast from 4% to 1.8%, and Quiroz conceded unemployment could hit 10%. He pointed to positives — a record $16 billion in investment projects approved through permitting, a capital-goods pipeline up 27% over five years — but those won't reach jobs or spending fast. The megarreforma's tax cuts are meant to be the catalyst, making today's Senate deadline especially consequential.

International · Humanitarian

45 Chilean rescuers return after pulling a man alive from Venezuela rubble — 8 days in

Chile's USAR (Urban Search and Rescue) firefighter team returned home July 4 to a hero's welcome after one of its most dramatic missions: finding Hernán Gil Flores alive in the underground parking of a collapsed mall in La Guaira, Venezuela — eight days after the June 24 double earthquakes. The rescue took more than 70 consecutive hours. Chile also sent three humanitarian flights with 35,000 vaccines, medicine, food and supplies.

It's diplomatically notable: Chile–Venezuela relations have been frigid, and Kast is loudly anti-Maduro — yet the government fast-tracked the mission, and Kast phoned the brigadiers from Montevideo. Worth knowing: Chile's bomberos are entirely volunteer-run. These 45 people have day jobs and gave 10 days to save a stranger in a country their president routinely condemns.

Culture · K-Pop

BTS fans march in 11 cities, demand government let the band play Estadio Nacional

On Sunday, thousands of BTS fans ("ARMY") marched in 11 Chilean cities — Santiago, Valparaíso, Concepción, Temuco, La Serena and more — demanding sports authorities allow the Korean group to perform at Estadio Nacional. The Sports Institute (IND) had rejected the permit, citing turf protection ahead of athletic events. Protesters carried purple balloons all the way to La Moneda, in what looked very much like a political rally.

By evening, the Sports Ministry signaled it would reconsider if the promoter met technical requirements — leaving the shows in hopeful limbo. A neat snapshot of Chilean culture: the capacity to mount a nationwide, politically-styled demonstration in 48 hours, over the logistics of a K-pop concert. Chile's ARMY is not messing around.

Herrera argues the unsung heroes of the megarreforma talks are politicians who put Chile ahead of partisan scoring — singling out Senate President Paulina Núñez (RN), a right-wing senator who brokered the cross-party table when Kast's own finance minister stonewalled compromise for weeks. "Un Chile que no giró hacia la derecha"

BioBioChile · Center-Left · Jul 2

Despite Kast's decisive win, survey data shows Chileans across the spectrum still want a robust, interventionist state — not a market-led retreat. Strong majorities back more state infrastructure investment (66%), restoring fuel-price stabilization (56%), and universal employer-funded childcare (61%), even among center-right voters. "Chileans voted against the previous government's failures, not for a libertarian overhaul," it argues — a direct challenge to the premise of the megarreforma.

🎬 Cinema

Arthouse anchors: Centro Arte Alameda & Cineteca Nacional

For special screenings and repertory cycles rather than the multiplex, the two reliable stops are Centro Arte Alameda (now in its Estación Mapocho home) and the Cineteca Nacional inside Centro Cultural La Moneda — restored classics, festival holdovers and director retrospectives. Worth checking their weekly calendars for one-off screenings; both program independent and Latin American film you won't find elsewhere in the city.
