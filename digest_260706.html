Politics & Legislature

Kast's flagship megarreforma hits its critical Senate amendment deadline today

Today is a pivotal day for the biggest legislative bet of President José Antonio Kast's young government. By 8 PM tonight, the Senate's Environment Committee must finalize its proposed amendments to the so-called megarreforma — the National Reconstruction and Economic Development bill that bundles tax cuts, investment incentives, fire-damage reconstruction aid, and deregulatory changes into a single sweeping package. The Labor Committee has until Wednesday; the Finance Committee closes July 10. After that, the full Senate votes the bill article by article.

The stakes are high. The bill passed its first Senate vote on June 24 by a razor-thin 26–23 margin — essentially a party-line vote where every defection matters. Negotiations have been contentious: Finance Minister Jorge Quiroz initially resisted a cross-party working table for weeks before Senate President Paulina Núñez (RN) brokered one last week, extending the amendment deadline to allow more time for deals on the tax provisions. The government's central ask — a gradual reduction in corporate tax rates and a new form of tax stability for investors — remains contested even among center-right senators. Think of this as the Chile equivalent of a US budget reconciliation process: it only takes one or two defections to collapse the whole thing.

Politics

Libertarian leader fires warning shot at Kast's coalition partners

Johannes Kaiser, head of the small but vocal Libertarian National Party (PNL), declared Sunday that President Kast "cannot do without Chile Vamos but shouldn't let them set the pace." The comment landed like a grenade inside an already tense governing alliance. Chile Vamos — the traditional center-right bloc that is the largest wing of the coalition — fired back: "You can't have it both ways. Either join the government or stop trying to steer it from outside."

The spat was ignited days earlier when Chile Vamos senators voted to sink a constitutional accusation against former Boric-era finance minister Nicolás Grau — a move that infuriated the libertarian and Partido Republicano wings, who had pushed the accusation to punish Grau over disputed fiscal figures. Think of it as libertarian-aligned Republicans telling a more establishment GOP president that his party's moderates are too slow — while refusing to formally join the caucus. Kast, who met coalition leaders last week to try to smooth things over, floated the idea of creating a formal coalition structure, but no deal has been reached.

Economy

Finance minister: Chile dodges technical recession as activity finally turns positive

Finance Minister Jorge Quiroz said Sunday that Chile will avoid a "technical recession" — two consecutive quarters of negative GDP growth — because June's monthly activity index (Imacec) should come in positive, ending a five-month losing streak. The news is a relief for a government under pressure: Kast won the presidency partly on a promise to revive growth, and a near-recession in his first months has been a political liability.

The caveats are substantial. The government has already cut its full-year growth forecast from 4% down to 1.8%, and Quiroz acknowledged unemployment could reach 10%. Quiroz did point to some positives: $16 billion in investment projects have been approved through the permitting system (SEIA), a record, and the capital-goods investment pipeline is up 27% over five years. But those numbers won't show up in jobs or consumer spending quickly. The megarreforma's tax cuts and investment guarantees are intended to be the growth catalyst — making today's Senate deadline especially consequential.

International · Humanitarian

45 Chilean rescue workers return after pulling a man alive from Venezuela rubble — 8 days in

Chile's USAR (Urban Search and Rescue) firefighter team returned home July 4 to a hero's welcome after one of the unit's most dramatic missions. They found Hernán Gil Flores alive in the underground parking level of a collapsed shopping mall in La Guaira, Venezuela — eight days after devastating double earthquakes struck on June 24. The rescue required more than 70 consecutive hours of work. Chile also dispatched three humanitarian air missions carrying 35,000 vaccines, medicine, food, and emergency supplies.

The mission is diplomatically notable. Chile–Venezuela relations have been frigid for years — Kast is openly and loudly anti-Maduro. Yet the government quickly authorized the rescue operation, and Kast personally called the brigadiers from Montevideo to express support. Worth knowing: Chile's bomberos (firefighters) are entirely volunteer-run — one of the distinctive features of Chilean civil society. These 45 people have day jobs and gave 10 days of their lives to save a stranger in a country their president routinely condemns.

Culture · K-Pop

BTS fans march in 11 cities, demand government let band play at Estadio Nacional

On Sunday, thousands of BTS fans ("ARMY") took to the streets in 11 Chilean cities — Santiago, Valparaíso, Concepción, Temuco, La Serena, and more — demanding the sports authorities allow the Korean pop group to perform at the Estadio Nacional. The Institute of Sport (IND) had rejected the permit, citing concerns about protecting the stadium turf ahead of scheduled athletic events. Protesters marched bearing purple balloons (the BTS fan color) all the way to La Moneda, the presidential palace, in what felt very much like a political rally — complete with chants and handmade signs.

By evening, the Sports Ministry signaled it would reconsider if the promoter (DG Medios) met specific technical requirements — leaving the concerts in a hopeful limbo. The episode is a nice snapshot of Chilean culture: the organizational capacity to mount a nationwide, politically-styled demonstration in 48 hours, over the logistics of a K-pop show. Chile's ARMY is not messing around.

Opinion / Editorial

"Los verdaderos republicanos"

Danilo Herrera  ·  The Clinic  ·  Left-Leaning  ·  July 4

Herrera argues the unsung heroes of the megarreforma negotiations are the politicians who put Chile ahead of partisan scoring — pointing specifically to Senate President Paulina Núñez (RN), a right-wing senator who brokered the cross-party working table when Kast's own finance minister had been stonewalling compromise for weeks. "True republicans are those who think of Chile before the next headline," Herrera writes, taking an implicit swipe at the libertarian and Partido Republicano hard-liners who stoked the coalition crisis with the doomed Grau constitutional accusation. It's a left-leaning outlet praising a right-wing senator — which is itself worth noticing.

"Un Chile que no giró hacia la derecha"

BioBioChile  ·  Center-Left  ·  July 2

Despite Kast's decisive election win, survey data shows Chileans across the political spectrum still want a robust, interventionist state — not a market-led retreat. Strong majorities support more state infrastructure investment (66%), restoring fuel price stabilization mechanisms (56%), and universal employer-funded childcare (61%) — positions that hold even among center-right voters. "Chileans voted against the previous government's failures, not for a libertarian economic overhaul," the column argues. The piece lands as a challenge to the premise of the megarreforma: roughly half of respondents are unconvinced that cutting corporate taxes creates jobs.
