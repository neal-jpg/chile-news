Politics · Coalition Fracture

The Right Cracks: Republicans Call Matthei "Irrelevant," UDI Erupts — and the Grau Impeachment Hangs in the Balance

Today the Chamber of Deputies votes on whether to formally censure former Finance Minister Nicolás Grau — Boric's longtime finance chief — over alleged manipulation of fiscal deficit figures. Think of it as a congressional impeachment: if it passes, Grau is barred from public office for five years. The odds look bad for the accusation's backers: the review commission voted 3-to-1 to reject it, the centrist Christian Democrats (DC) announced they'll vote no, and former Finance Minister Andrés Velasco called the whole proceeding "irresponsible theater." Even so, the vote isn't settled — the PDG populist bloc and UDI are both leaning yes, and the math is tight.

But the bigger story of the week is what the fight exposed inside the ruling right-wing coalition. Republican Party deputy Agustín Romero — from President Kast's own party — publicly dismissed former UDI presidential candidate Evelyn Matthei as "not a relevant figure" for the Republicans. UDI erupted, demanding a public apology and warning that if none came, relations between the two parties would be "significantly affected." La Tercera called it the right at "boiling point." The rift is a familiar one in US terms: Kast's hard-line Republican Party is the Freedom Caucus; Chile Vamos (UDI and Renovación Nacional) is the House establishment. They won together, but they do not agree on much beyond that.

Immigration · Security

Interior Minister Announces Wave of New Crackdown Measures — Including 6-Month Migrant Detention

Interior Minister Martín Arrau announced the Kast government is rushing six new security and immigration bills to Congress by the end of June. The headline measure: extending the maximum detention of irregular migrants from 5 days to up to 6 months — a 36-fold increase — to make deportations actually enforceable. The government also confirmed it is digging roughly 50 kilometers of anti-crossing trenches along the northern border and has deployed drones and thermal surveillance. A first deportation flight has already taken off since March. Officials say irregular border crossings are down 90% since Kast took office. Critics, including human rights groups, warn that multi-month detention of migrants without conviction has serious legal risks.

Economy · Monetary Policy

Central Bank Slashes Growth Forecast — and the Fiscal Council Warns of a Debt Crunch

Chile's Central Bank — the local equivalent of the Federal Reserve — cut its 2026 GDP growth forecast to just 1.0%–1.75% in its June monetary policy report, down from 1.5%–2.5% three months ago. The bank kept its benchmark rate at 4.5%. The gloomier picture reflects weaker private investment, reduced government spending, and slower global demand. Separately, Chile's Autonomous Fiscal Council — the independent watchdog equivalent of the Congressional Budget Office — delivered a pointed warning this week: if current trends hold, government debt could hit 48% of GDP by the early 2030s. Investment banks are already penciling in a fiscal deficit of roughly 2.2% of GDP for this year, well above official targets.

Crime · Security

Nearly 4 Billion Pesos Seized from Organized Crime in Two Years — Where Does It All Go?

Chile's PDI (the equivalent of the FBI) has seized close to 4 billion Chilean pesos (roughly US$4.3 million) from organized crime over the past two years, with 2024 marking the highest total yet. An investigation by Emol traces the bureaucratic journey of that money: it's frozen, assigned to legal proceedings, and — if conviction follows — can be redirected toward policing and social programs. The reporting offers a rare inside look at how a mid-sized Latin American country is trying to systematically dismantle the finances of transnational criminal organizations like the Tren de Aragua, rather than just making arrests.

Colombia's New Right-Wing President Elected — Markets React Cautiously

Abelardo de la Espriella won Colombia's presidential election, continuing the regional trend toward center-right and right governments following Chile, Argentina, and Ecuador. Colombian markets reacted with cautious relief rather than jubilation: the dollar fell 2% against the peso and the stock exchange opened up 2.6%, but investors remain watchful about the new government's economic program. BioBioChile also reported the curious story of Peru's presidential runoff, where overseas votes — including from Chile's large Peruvian community — proved decisive in overcoming Keiko Fujimori's slim lead in domestic results.

Culture · Architecture

A Santiaguino Just Won the Nobel Prize of Architecture

In March, Chilean architect Smiljan Radić Clarke was named the 2026 Pritzker Prize laureate — architecture's most prestigious honor, roughly its Nobel Prize. He's only the second Chilean to win the prize, after Alejandro Aravena took it home in 2016. Radić Clarke, 61, is better known in architectural circles than to the general public, which is somewhat the point: his best-known work internationally is the 2014 Serpentine Gallery Pavilion in London, an impossibly delicate translucent fiberglass shell perched on rough, massive stones. The Pritzker jury praised him for "reminding us that architecture is an art, in that it touches the very core of the human condition." Not a bad week to be Santiago.

El Mostrador · Column · Center-Left

"The Constitutional Politics of Chile's National Reconstruction"

A June 22 column in El Mostrador dissects Kast's flagship "Proyecto de Reconstrucción Nacional" — the omnibus bill that bundles taxes, immigration enforcement, housing, employment law, and higher-education financing into a single legislative package. The author argues that by framing Chile as economically and institutionally "broken," the government creates artificial urgency to justify bypassing the normal deliberative process each major reform deserves on its own. It's the Chilean equivalent of attaching every controversial rider to a single must-pass reconciliation bill and daring Congress to reject the whole thing. Critics from both left and right are uncomfortable with the all-or-nothing approach, even if they agree with parts of the agenda.

Diario Financiero · Editorial · Center-Right / Business

"The IPoM Adjustment" — Reading the Central Bank's Pessimism

DF.cl's editorial board takes stock of the Central Bank's June downgrade and calls it a "reality shock." The piece argues that the bank is right to revise down but that the government cannot hide behind global headwinds — weak domestic investment and fiscal uncertainty are homegrown problems that policy can actually address. It calls on the Kast administration to accelerate the structural reforms in the Reconstrucción Nacional rather than letting the legislative fight drag on: a 1% growth economy does not give the government the luxury of time.
