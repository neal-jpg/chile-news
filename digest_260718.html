Politics

Megarreforma Clears Senate in Narrow Night Vote — Chamber Showdown Next

In a marathon session that ended at 2:40 a.m. Wednesday, the Chilean Senate passed President Kast's sweeping Ley de Reconstrucción Nacional — universally nicknamed the megarreforma — by 26 votes to 24. Think of it as Chile's version of a major tax-and-stimulus package forced through a Senate with a one-seat working majority.

Key provisions include a gradual corporate income tax cut (from 27% to 23%), property-tax exemptions for homeowners over 65, and a mechanism allowing businesses to seek restitution when government agencies cancel their environmental permits. The bill now returns to the Chamber of Deputies — Chile's lower house — for a third and final ratification vote expected this week. If deputies approve the Senate's changes, Kast can sign it into law. The opposition has already vowed to challenge multiple clauses at the Constitutional Tribunal, Chile's answer to the Supreme Court.

One subplot: the Senate also approved a clause banning anatocismo — charging interest on interest in financial contracts — but the Finance Ministry says it will challenge that specific provision at the Tribunal itself, setting up the unusual scenario of a government filing a constitutional complaint against its own bill.

Economy

Government Slashes Growth Forecast — Recession Murmurs Grow

The government cut its 2026 GDP growth estimate from 2.1% to 1.8% in its Q2 public-finance report, now matching the IMF's own downgrade. Private economists are gloomier still, projecting just 1.3%, citing weakness in mining and fishing and sluggish consumer spending.

Unemployment sits at 9.4% — nearly 980,000 Chileans out of work — while inflation runs at 4.3%, above the Central Bank's 3% target. BioBioChile published a pointed analysis this week asking whether Chile is quietly entering a "technical recession" (two consecutive quarters of contraction) that the government has not yet acknowledged.

Politics

Kast Huddles With Coalition Allies to Patch Legislative Cracks

The razor-thin Senate vote on the megarreforma exposed real friction between the Kast administration and its congressional partners. This week the president sat down with lawmakers from Renovación Nacional (RN) — his coalition's largest partner, comparable to mainstream establishment Republicans — at the presidential retreat in Viña del Mar.

RN put two proposals on the table: a 48-hour mechanism for the executive and the legislature to resolve policy disputes before they become public brawls, and a standing weekly political committee to keep coordination tight. The meeting reflects the core challenge of Kast's governing model — running with a slim majority that requires near-constant management to hold together.

National

Biggest Winter Storm in Years Sweeps Chile — Southern Frost Follows

A massive frontal system moved through 12 Chilean regions between July 14 and 17 — the most powerful winter storm in recent memory. Santiago received up to 70 mm of rain, gusts hit 80 km/h in exposed areas, and Andean snowfall exceeded 90 cm in some cordillera sectors. Export logistics in the Bio-Bío region were disrupted. President Kast traveled to the region Wednesday to assess damage.

As of today the Metro Region is mostly past it, but the cold wave is pushing south. SENAPRED issued frost alerts for Los Lagos (Futaleufú, Cochamó, Osorno, Castro) through July 21, with minimum temperatures potentially reaching −9°C in Patagonian sub-Andean zones. Mountain passes remain restricted.

Sky / Science

Storm-Scrubbed Skies Set Up Perfect Meteor Viewing This Week

The winter storm left one gift behind: uncommonly clean air over Santiago. The timing is excellent — the Delta Aquarid meteor shower is building toward its annual peak (maximum around July 28–29) and already producing visible streaks after midnight. Head south toward Cajón del Maipo to escape city glow. Chile sits in the southern hemisphere's prime viewing corridor for this shower, and the freshly washed atmosphere makes tonight one of the better low-light opportunities of the winter season.

El Líbero's columnist flips the left's framing: opposing the megarreforma isn't principled — it's a moral failure.
