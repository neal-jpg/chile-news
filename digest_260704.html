Economy

Chile Slides Toward Technical Recession: Five Consecutive Months of Contraction

Chile's central bank confirmed that the Imacec — the country's monthly economic activity index, roughly equivalent to a monthly GDP reading — contracted 0.9% in May compared to the same month last year. That's the fifth consecutive month of decline, a streak last seen 17 years ago during the 2008 global financial crisis. If June comes in negative too, Chile will formally enter a technical recession (two consecutive quarters of contraction). President Kast did not sugarcoat it: "We realized too late that we have an economic illness," he said, warning that the economic indicators show "more than one warning light."

The primary culprit is mining. Copper production — Chile's single largest export and economic engine — fell more than 11% year-over-year in May, dragging the overall number into the red even as retail and automotive commerce showed modest growth. Strip out mining, and the rest of the economy would have grown around 0.7% — not robust, but not a contraction. Unemployment for the March–May period hit 9.4%, the highest in nearly five years, with roughly 950,000 Chileans currently out of work. The Confederation of Production and Commerce (CPC) — Chile's equivalent of the US Chamber of Commerce — said bluntly that "the year is relatively decided": they expect growth of at most 1–1.5% for all of 2026, well below the targets Kast promised voters.

Politics

Kast's Approval Falls to 38% as Economy Becomes Chile's Defining Concern

The weekly Cadem poll — Chile's closest equivalent to Gallup — has President José Antonio Kast at 38% approval and 58% disapproval, down three points from the week before. The economy and unemployment now dominate as the country's primary concern at 67% of mentions, edging ahead of security and crime at 61%. The trigger is clear: the figures landed immediately after Imacec and unemployment data made headlines.

The poll also found 69% of Chileans believe deploying the military to city streets would raise the feeling of safety, and 84% want the armed forces to play a role in citizen security — figures that give Kast political space to advance a hard-line security posture even as his economic numbers slip. On a separate question, 51% said they oppose pardons for former police and military officers convicted of human rights abuses during Chile's 2019 social uprising, suggesting the public is not yet ready to close that chapter.

Congress

Reconstruction Bill Races Toward Senate Vote — Kast Wants It Done This Month

Kast's flagship economic legislation — the Plan de Reconstrucción Nacional — passed the Senate's general concept vote 26–23 last month (think: a 51–49 Senate cloture vote), and now moves to the article-by-article fight in committee. The amendment deadline is Monday, July 6, after which the specific-article debate begins. Kast has said he wants the bill dispatched from the Senate by the end of July, warning he will push it through by a single vote if the opposition forces his hand.

The bill bundles more than 40 measures: a corporate tax cut from 27% to 23%, a 25-year tax stability guarantee for large investment projects, tax credits for companies that hire low-wage workers, and faster environmental permitting for mines and infrastructure. The opposition calls it "a disguised tax reform" dressed up as reconstruction. Even Evelyn Matthei — former mayor and the leading critical voice within the right-wing coalition — has questioned the employment-credit design as too costly for its expected results. Kast says with five months of economic contraction, there's no time for a prolonged debate.

Economy / Security

Illicit Markets Cost Chile $5.7 Billion a Year — Business Leaders Declare "Structural Threat"

Chile's top business federation, the CPC (Confederación de la Producción y del Comercio — roughly analogous to the US Chamber of Commerce), released a major report this week warning that illegal markets have grown from a contained problem into a "structural threat" to the country's development. Drug trafficking, contraband, piracy, and informal labor together move an estimated US$5.7 billion annually — and cost the state more than US$1.5 billion in lost tax revenue every year.

The report, titled "Por un Chile sin Economía Ilícita," argues that illicit markets now compete directly with legal businesses, undercutting prices in sectors from food to construction materials, and that the damage is compounding: weakened formal businesses pay less tax, which reduces funds for security and regulation, which allows illicit markets to expand further. The CPC called on the government to treat illegal-economy enforcement as economic policy — not just a law-and-order matter — and to coordinate anti-money-laundering efforts with regulatory reform.

Interesting & Offbeat

Chile Is Building the World's First Southern-Hemisphere Gamma-Ray Observatory in the Atacama

Construction of the SWGO — Southern Wide-field Gamma-ray Observatory — is set to begin this year in the Parque Astronómico Atacama, a high-altitude reserve in Chile's far north. With a budget of approximately US$60 million and backing from a consortium of 55 institutions across 17 countries, the SWGO will be the only gamma-ray observatory in the southern hemisphere and the first capable of studying the center of the Milky Way directly overhead. Chile already hosts more than 40% of the world's astronomical observation infrastructure, largely because the Atacama's high altitude, near-zero humidity, and 320+ clear nights per year make it the best place on Earth to look at the sky. SWGO adds a new layer: detecting the highest-energy photons produced by supernovae, neutron star collisions, and supermassive black holes at the galactic core — events invisible from the northern hemisphere.

Santiago's Quietest Fortnight Is Almost Here — City Drains, Ski Slopes Pack

Winter school vacation (vacaciones de invierno) begins Monday, July 7, and runs through July 18 for most Santiago-region schools. The practical effect on the city is immediate and welcome: rush-hour traffic drops noticeably, metro cars are half-empty by 8am, and the city center quiets. Restaurant tables open up at peak hours, museum queues shrink, and the infamous Costanera Norte gridlock largely disappears. The inverse is happening at altitude: Valle Nevado, La Parva, El Colorado, and Portillo are packed with families taking advantage of what's shaping up to be a solid snow season. If you're staying in the city, the next two weeks are among the easiest of the year to move around — enjoy it before August brings everyone back.

"Invierta en Chile S.A.: The Worker Pays the Tab"

A sharply worded column in BioBioChile argues that Kast's government has become "a government of general managers" — one that applies business logic to institutions designed to guarantee rights, not generate returns. The piece takes direct aim at the Reconstruction Plan: the tax credits and corporate incentives flow upward to companies and large investors, while workers facing 9.4% unemployment are offered no direct relief. The author draws a pointed distinction between a company, which exists to generate economic value, and a state, which exists to balance competing interests, preserve social cohesion, and ensure conditions of well-being for the whole population — not just the most productive portion of it.

"Security: A Necessary Agenda for Long-Term Growth"

Columnist Roberto Cases makes the case that Chile's security crisis and its economic crisis are not separate problems to be addressed on parallel tracks. Organized crime, extortion of small businesses, and the general sense of institutional weakness are deterrents to investment as tangible as tax rates or regulatory red tape. Cases argues that every month spent without a credible state response to organized crime is a month in which companies delay expansion, foreign investors stall on projects, and the informal economy expands to fill the security vacuum. The piece implicitly supports Kast's security-first posture — including the growing demand for military deployment — as a prerequisite for the economic reactivation the Reconstruction Plan promises, not a distraction from it.
