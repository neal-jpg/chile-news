Politics · The 100-Day Reset

Past Its First 100 Days, the Government Floods the Zone With New Bills to Retake the Agenda

Having cleared the symbolic 100-day mark, the Kast administration is opening the week with a burst of legislation aimed at controlling the narrative: a "Vandals Registry" (a database to track people convicted in violent protests), a long-debated Sala Cuna Universal (universal employer-funded daycare, with a per-child cost reported above 300,000 pesos a month), and a new child bonus. It's the classic move of an executive that wants the conversation to be about its own priorities rather than the opposition's attacks.

Think of it as a White House dropping a stack of signature bills the same week it's taking fire — flooding the zone so the headlines are about what you're proposing, not what your critics are saying. The daycare bill is the politically loaded one: business groups and municipalities are already warning they can't absorb the cost, a fight that cuts across the usual left-right lines.

Local · Air & Daily Life

Third Straight Smog Alert in Santiago — and the Car You Can Drive Today Depends on Your Plate

Santiago is under its third consecutive "environmental pre-emergency," the winter routine where cold, windless air settles over the valley and traps pollution against the Andes. Authorities respond with a driving restriction tied to the last digit of your license plate, plus limits on wood-burning heaters. Nearby Cerrillos even suspended classes Monday after a rubber-warehouse fire fouled the air further. It's the local equivalent of a US smog alert paired with odd-even gas rationing — a recurring fact of life that quietly reorganizes everyone's commute.

Politics · The Impeachment Fight

Grau Braces for Impeachment as His Old Party Loads "Dynamite" in Return Fire

The constitutional accusation (Chile's version of impeachment) against Nicolás Grau, finance minister in the previous left-wing Boric government, is heading for a real fight in the Chamber of Deputies — the push by libertarians and Republicans has picked up support from the mainstream right. Grau, meanwhile, is weighing life after government (including whether he can return to his university post), while his Frente Amplio party signals it's preparing political "dynamite" of its own if the accusation advances. A reminder that, as in the US, impeachment is as much a partisan weapon as a legal process.

Economy · Student Debt

A Deputy Minister Defends the Student-Loan Collections — and Points the Finger Backward

The CAE, Chile's state-backed student loan and a decade-long political sore, is back in the headlines. Deputy minister Pavez publicly defended the government's continued debt collection and turned blame on the previous administration over separate irregularities in the national Census. It's a notable posture: the current right-leaning government is enforcing repayment on a loan the prior left-wing government had promised to forgive but never fully did — letting today's officials cast themselves as the adults cleaning up an unkept promise.

Labor · Ports

Port Workers Call a National Strike for Thursday, Accusing the Government of Breaking Its Word

Chile's port-workers confederation has announced a nationwide walkout for Thursday, charging that the government failed to deliver on agreed commitments — pensions, benefits for widows, and pending decrees. In a country whose economy runs on shipping copper, fruit, and salmon out through its Pacific ports, a coordinated port strike is a serious pressure point, closer in stakes to a US longshore or rail shutdown than a routine protest.

City · Remaking Downtown

The Team Behind Santiago's Buzzy "MUT" Wants to Reinvent a Downtown Block Next

The developers of MUT — a glossy mixed-use complex that became a hit in the well-heeled east side — are now eyeing the city center. Their planned "NIDO Lucía" would transform the block around the old Enel building on Santa Rosa into an urban market, restaurants, and innovation spaces. It's a real bet that downtown Santiago, long associated with grit and decline, can draw the same crowds as the shiny new developments uptown.

Culture · Santiago Institutions

The Chilean Restaurant Built Inside an Old Drive-In Cinema Turns 35

Divertimento Chileno, perched at the foot of Cerro San Cristóbal, is marking 35 years as a family-run temple of Chilean cooking — pastel de choclo, empanadas, the works — in a building that started life as a drive-in movie theater. It's the kind of unpretentious local landmark that outlasts trends, the Santiago equivalent of a beloved decades-old diner everyone has a story about.

The Clinic's editorial board argues that Chile keeps treating mental health as an afterthought — underfunded, stigmatized, and absent from the policy conversation even as demand surges. "Peru and Its Three Halves"

Political scientist Carlos Meléndez dissects Peru's deadlocked runoff, arguing the country is split into thirds with no candidate commanding a real mandate — a recipe for another weak, contested presidency next door. For Chile, it's a nervous glance at a neighbor's chronic instability; for an American reader, picture a presidential race where the eventual winner takes office with neither side believing they truly lost.
