Karen Rojo: Chile's Longest-Running Fugitive Mayor — and What "Fraude al Fisco" Means

Who she is. Karen Rojo is a pharmacist-turned-politician who served as mayor of Antofagasta from 2012 to 2020. Antofagasta is Chile's copper-export capital — a desert city of roughly 400,000 people in the far north, roughly the size of Tampa, Florida. It sits atop the world's richest copper deposits and is one of Chile's wealthiest regions per capita. Rojo was a popular two-term mayor before her conviction.

The crime: fraude al fisco. In Chile, "fraude al fisco" (fraud against the treasury) is a criminal charge for misappropriating public funds — government money paid to the wrong purpose, often for personal or political benefit. In Rojo's case, a court found that she authorized roughly 23 million pesos (about $25,000 USD at the time) in city funds to pay a private communications firm that worked on her political image during her 2016 reelection campaign. Public money used for campaign purposes is strictly illegal under Chilean law.

The American analogy. Picture a Chicago alderman quietly rerouting city road-maintenance contracts to a PR firm that was simultaneously producing his reelection mailers. The dollar amount is modest by US standards, but the principle — that a public official cannot use taxpayer money to benefit their own political future — is taken seriously in Chilean courts. The charge doesn't require large-scale theft; even a clear misuse of intent is enough.

The getaway. In January 2021 a court sentenced Rojo to five years of effective imprisonment. She appealed all the way to Chile's Supreme Court, which rejected the appeal in March 2022. She was photographed boarding a flight to Europe the following day. She turned up in the Netherlands, where she lived freely until Interpol coordinated her arrest in July 2022. She spent the next four years in a Dutch women's prison while extradition proceedings played out across two countries' legal systems.

Why today matters. Her arrival in Santiago in handcuffs on July 15, 2026 is a symbolic moment for Chilean rule of law: proof that even officials who physically flee the country face eventual accountability. Her defense argues she has already served about 80% of her sentence during Dutch detention, and hopes a Chilean court will grant supervised release. That battle plays out in Antofagasta's courts in coming weeks.
