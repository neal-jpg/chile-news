Economy

Chile's Unemployment Hits 9.4% — A Five-Year High the Government Won't Pretend Away

Chile's national statistics agency (INE) confirmed that the national unemployment rate for the March–May 2026 trimester reached 9.4%, the highest level in five years and 0.5 percentage points worse than a year earlier. The Santiago metropolitan region is even harder hit: 9.8%. Nearly 950,000 Chileans are currently out of work. Informality also worsened — 27% of Chilean workers are now in informal jobs, up a full percentage point in twelve months. The government's own Economy Minister Daniel Mas didn't spin it: "having almost 950,000 people without work is an authentic social drama."

The political battle lines are predictable. Mas and the Kast administration blamed the Boric government for handing them "a stagnant and battered economy from the second half of last year." El Mostrador pushed back, noting that Chile has had unemployment above 8% for more than three consecutive years — which demands a structural diagnosis, not just a new government to blame. That argument cuts to the heart of the debate over Kast's Ley Miscelánea (his sweeping economic reform bill, currently in Senate committee with an amendment deadline of July 6): the government says cutting the corporate tax rate from 27% to 23% will unlock investment and drive job creation; the opposition says a trickle-down tax cut won't fix a labor market fractured by gender gaps, weak regional economies, and chronically low productivity growth. With Kast's stated goal of reaching 6.5% unemployment by 2030, the gap between today's number and that target is very large.

Security

Chile Moves to Criminalize Using Children as Gang Soldiers — But the Debate Has a Twist

A cross-party group led by Senate President Paulina Núñez (center-right RN) introduced a bill July 1 creating a specific criminal offense for any adult who recruits or uses minors in organized crime. The proposal emerged in parallel with a fraught national debate: after the homicide of a 12-year-old in San Bernardo — in which teenage perpetrators were involved — the Kast government proposed allowing 16- and 17-year-olds to be tried as adults in serious crimes. Former Justice Minister Luis Cordero (Boric era) then raised an uncomfortable counter-argument: if older teens become legally equivalent to adults, criminal organizations will simply shift recruitment toward younger children, who remain under the lighter juvenile justice system. He called it "an extremely perverse circuit."

The new bill attacks the problem from the supply side — punishing the adult recruiters rather than the children themselves. Penalties are more severe when victims are under 14, when violence or intimidation is used, or when the minor is deployed to carry weapons, transport drugs, or conduct surveillance. Think of it as a legal response to the observation that gangs — including transnational networks like Venezuela's Tren de Aragua — are deliberately exploiting the lighter legal treatment of juveniles as a feature, not a bug.

Foreign Policy

Kast and Uruguay's Left-Wing President Seal a Regional Anti-Crime Pact

The Kast-Orsi bilateral meeting concluded with a formal joint commitment by Chile and Uruguay to deepen cooperation against organized crime and narcotrafficking. Kast has been building what he calls the "Compromiso de Santiago" — a regional security architecture with shared intelligence, coordinated border enforcement, and unified anti-money-laundering frameworks — pitching the model country by country across South America. The two delegations also signed bilateral agreements on mutual recognition of digital signature certificates for commerce, and an exchange program between Chile's Andrés Bello Academy and Uruguay's Artigas Institute for foreign service training.

The political optics of the meeting are deliberate: Kast (far-right Republican Party) chose Uruguay specifically because President Yamandú Orsi (left-wing Frente Amplio) is ideologically his opposite. Kast framed it as security "beyond political differences." Orsi, for his part, described organized crime as a shared threat to the region's democracies. Think of the diplomatic posture as Kast trying to build a regional security coalition the way post-WWII Republican presidents built NATO commitments — emphasizing shared interests over ideology. Kast addresses a Uruguayan business association today before returning to Santiago.

Congress

Left and Right Agree: Eight Years of Weaponized Impeachments Have to Stop

A cross-party group of senators and deputies — spanning center-right RN and Evópoli to center-left PS and left-wing Frente Amplio — is formally pushing legislation to raise the bar for constitutional accusations (Chile's version of congressional impeachment). The bill would require 20 parliamentary signatures instead of the current 10 to file an accusation, mandate a prior formal questioning (interpelación) of the minister before any libelo can proceed, and require a two-thirds Chamber quorum of 78 deputies to approve it. Sponsors include Senators Diego Schalper (RN), Luciano Cruz-Coke (Evópoli), Diego Ibáñez (Frente Amplio), and Juan Luis Castro (PS) — a coalition that spans most of the political spectrum.

The motivation is obvious: Chile has processed 23 constitutional accusations since 2018 — ten under Piñera, thirteen under Boric — a pace of roughly three per year. Tuesday's failed Grau accusation (which collapsed in embarrassment when Kast's own coalition senators broke ranks) was the most recent example of the process being used as a political weapon rather than a genuine accountability tool. Backers frame the reform explicitly as restoring gobernabilidad — governance stability — regardless of which party holds the presidency. In American terms, it would be like bipartisan legislation to raise the Senate's impeachment trial standard after years of politically motivated House impeachments.

Interesting & Offbeat

Acquitted and Moving On: Ex-Finance Chief Nicolás Grau May Return to the University

After all four charges of the constitutional accusation against him were rejected Tuesday by wide margins (the closest vote was 16 in favor, 25 against — far short of the 26 needed), former Finance Minister Nicolás Grau is reportedly weighing a return to his professorship at the Universidad de Chile's economics faculty. The Clinic's profile of him after the vote noted the quiet irony: Grau was one of the most technically capable ministers in recent memory, a University of Toronto–trained economist who navigated Boric's fiscal tightrope without a major scandal. He entered the constitutional accusation process as a political target and walked out with his reputation intact — if anything, enhanced. Kast's coalition accused him of understating the fiscal deficit on his way out of office; the Senate decided, by wide margins and with senators from Kast's own bloc voting against, that the accusation didn't hold up. Grau's response was characteristically understated. Whether he stays in public life or returns to the classroom, the outcome is one of the stranger political results of the week.

Comic Con Starts Tomorrow, School Break Starts July 7 — Santiago's Quietest Weeks Are Coming

Two things converge this weekend: Comic Con Chile 2026 opens tomorrow (July 3) at the Espacio Riesco convention center and runs through Saturday (July 5), bringing together cosplay, gaming, K-Pop competitions, film and TV guests, and approximately every teenager in the metropolitan region. Then on Monday, July 7, winter school vacation (vacaciones de invierno) officially begins for most Santiago schools, lasting two weeks through July 18. The practical effect on life in Santiago is significant: rush-hour traffic drops noticeably, the metro becomes more comfortable, and the city center quiets. On the flip side, Valle Nevado, La Parva, El Colorado, and Portillo (all within an hour of the city) will be packed — snowfall has been decent this season, and conditions at altitude should be solid. If you're not heading to the mountains, this is the best two weeks to use Santiago's parks, museums, and restaurants without fighting crowds. Comic Con tickets run 10,000–20,000 CLP (~$10–20 USD).

Diario Financiero  ·  Center-Right  ·  Editorial

"Unemployment and the Real State of the Economy"

DF's editorial board weighs in on the 9.4% figure with unusual directness: "the numbers are not good and there is no point pretending otherwise." The editorial argues that three years above 8% unemployment demonstrates structural failure — not a cyclical dip — and that the Ley Miscelánea's corporate tax reductions, while necessary, are not sufficient on their own. The board calls on the government to accelerate the permitting reforms and capital repatriation window in the bill and to push the full package through before September, warning that if the Ley Miscelánea is gutted in committee or delayed past the August recess, Chile's 2030 growth and employment targets become unreachable. The implicit message to the opposition: blocking the reforms prolongs the very unemployment crisis they are complaining about.

El Mostrador  ·  Left-Leaning  ·  Análisis

"An Employment Crisis Combining Low Growth, Regional Gaps, and Systemic Rigidities"

A detailed piece in El Mostrador's weekly supplement argues that Chile's labor crisis can't be explained by any single variable or fixed with any single policy lever — making the government's framing ("Boric's fault + corporate tax cut = jobs") an oversimplification. The piece maps three interlocking problems: chronically weak GDP growth, persistent regional disparities (unemployment in Biobío and La Araucanía runs 2–3 percentage points above the national average), and gender gaps that haven't meaningfully closed in a decade (women's labor force participation trails the OECD average by roughly 15 points). The key argument: cutting corporate taxes may eventually increase investment, but it does nothing for the structural barriers keeping women, young workers, and workers in peripheral regions locked out of the formal economy. The article calls for targeted active labor market policies — publicly funded job training, subsidized childcare for working mothers, and regional investment incentives — as necessary complements to the government's business reform agenda, not alternatives to it.
