Economy

Chile's Economy Posts Its Fifth Straight Monthly Contraction as IMF Cuts Growth Forecast

The Banco Central de Chile reported that the IMACEC — Chile's monthly economic activity index — fell 0.9% in May compared to the same month last year, the fifth consecutive negative reading and the worst streak since 2009. Unemployment stands at 9.4%. The IMF this week trimmed its 2026 Chile growth forecast from 2.2% to 1.8%, citing weak domestic demand and softer copper export revenues. Economists are openly debating a "technical recession," which requires just one more quarterly GDP contraction.

Finance Minister Jorge Quiroz pushed back, telling reporters Monday that June marked a "point of inflection" for the economy and that fuel prices will drop roughly $100 per liter on gasoline and $150 per liter on diesel later this week as global oil costs ease — offering some relief to households squeezed by March's historic fuel price hike.

Politics · Congress

Kast's Reconstruction "Megarreform" Enters Final Senate Stretch — Votes Set for July 21–22

The Senate began its article-by-article review of Kast's flagship economic omnibus bill Monday, with floor votes scheduled for July 21 and 22. The ~40-measure package — officially the Plan de Reconstrucción Nacional — includes cutting the corporate tax rate from 27% to 23%, a 25-year tax-stability guarantee for large investors, and rebuilding aid for regions devastated by Chile's 2024 wildfires. The bill scraped through the Senate's general-concept vote on June 24 with a razor-thin 26–23 margin.

Mayors from across the political spectrum remain furious: they met with Finance Minister Quiroz last week demanding changes to property tax provisions, only to leave empty-handed. Quiroz is still trading amendments with wavering senators to hold his paper-thin majority.

Politics

Kast's Right-Wing Coalition Cracks: The Johannes Kaiser Problem

Johannes Kaiser — the libertarian firebrand who won roughly 10% in last year's first-round presidential vote before endorsing Kast — spent this week publicly sniping at Kast's main coalition partner, Chile Vamos. Kaiser accused the center-right bloc of "imposing conditions at the cost of the government program" and said Chile Vamos senators were "fighting battles from the 1990s." Chile Vamos hit back: "you can't have your cake and eat it too — either support the government or don't." Think of Kaiser as Chile's version of the Freedom Caucus: outside the formal cabinet, ideologically to the right of the governing coalition, and able to cause headaches on key votes.

Kast stepped in personally this week, calling an emergency roundtable with all coalition party leaders at La Moneda and announcing fortnightly coordination meetings. Analysts warn that coalition discipline is essential for the megarreforma votes later this month.

Immigration

Senate Votes to Make Illegal Border Crossing a Criminal Offense

The Chilean Senate approved legislation this week reclassifying irregular border entry from an administrative infraction to a criminal one — a signature Kast campaign pledge. Under the new law, anyone caught crossing clandestinely faces criminal charges rather than just a fine and deportation order; repeat offenders and those who use false documents face aggravated penalties.

Critics, including constitutional law professors, say the bill "criminalizes migration" and creates a legal framework that treats asylum-seekers like criminals. Supporters argue it targets the trafficking networks who profit from clandestine crossings. Context: Chile's foreign-born population roughly doubled over five years to about 9% of the population — a demographic shift that drove immigration to the center of the 2025 election campaign, much as it has in recent US elections.

Economy · Shadow

Chile's Underground Economy Moves $5.7 Billion a Year — Bigger Than Paraguay's Entire GDP

Chile's main business confederation (the CPC, roughly equivalent to the U.S. Chamber of Commerce) published a report this week estimating that illicit markets — drug trafficking, contraband cigarettes, counterfeit goods, illegal gambling, and fuel adulteration — generate US$5.7 billion annually inside Chile and cost the state more than US$1.5 billion in lost tax revenue each year. The report calls it a "structural threat" to Chile's development rather than a law-enforcement problem alone. For reference, Chile's formal GDP is about US$320 billion, so the shadow economy is roughly 1.8% of it — not catastrophic by global standards, but large enough to distort competition and fund criminal networks.

Opinion

"El etiquetado frontal de alimentos: una política que funciona"

El Mostrador · Left-leaning · July 7, 2026

Chile's mandatory black-octagon warning labels on unhealthy foods — introduced under the Boric government, one of the world's first such systems — demonstrably cut purchases of sugary drinks and high-calorie snacks, the columnist argues. With the Kast administration reviewing Boric-era regulations, this piece urges against rolling back a public health policy that studies show actually changed consumer behavior. The American parallel would be if the FDA's calorie-label requirements had been introduced, worked, and then faced repeal under a new administration.

Kids 🦜 Wildlife

Buin Zoo: Chile's First Hyacinth Macaws + Animal Shows During Winter Vacation

Buin Zoo, about 45 minutes south of Santiago in the town of Buin, recently welcomed the only Hyacinth Macaws in Chile — the world's largest parrot species, with a wingspan up to 1.2 meters and an electric cobalt-blue plumage. Daily animal programming includes bird-of-prey flight shows, "Zoolompiadas" (animal Olympic games for kids), and the Buin Marino educational displays. Budget CLP $15,000–$20,000 per adult for entry; it's a solid half-day family trip. Parking is easy and there's a decent food court on-site.

Releases & Scene News
Rock Folk / Folklórico

Los Tres — XCLNT

If you haven't listened yet, fix that today. Los Tres — Chile's defining rock band of the 1990s, who broke up in 1999 after four classic albums — reunited their original four (Henríquez, Lindl, Parra, Molina), flew to Abbey Road Studios in London last summer, and dropped XCLNT in April. It's their first proper record together in 26 years. Critics called it "the same old magic": direct rock-and-roll structures laced with traditional cueca rhythms, tight band interplay, no studio gloss getting in the way. Think of them as Chile's Pearl Jam, the band that defined what the local rock scene could be. For fans of: Pixies, early Pearl Jam, cueca rock hybrids.

Three months out, XCLNT is still dominating Chilean music conversation — this is the cultural event of the year for the rock listener. Every new review and interview is worth reading.
