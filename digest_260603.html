Politics · Cuenta Pública 2026

Kast Delivers His First State of the Nation — and Gets Heckled at the Close

President José Antonio Kast addressed Congress yesterday in his first Cuenta Pública — Chile's equivalent of the State of the Union, delivered annually on June 1. He leaned hard into the principles that got him elected: tougher immigration, economic growth, and law and order. His main announcements included an immigration "Plan Retorno" (a voluntary return program for undocumented migrants), housing expansion tied to clearing vacant lots, and a push to reform the Indigenous Land Law to let communities lease or mortgage their own property. As he finished, a shout from the gallery — "¡Mentiroso!" ("Liar!") — briefly interrupted the close.

The reaction is already split along predictable lines. The left says the immigration plan amounts to "Plan Devuélvase Solo" — go home yourself — with no real support for migrants. The opposition PS party president said Kast seemed "disconnected from the country." His own coalition called the tone "constructive." The centerpiece, a broader reconstruction mega-reform, still faces a tough road through a divided Congress.

Security

Kast's Security Minister Fires Both of His Undersecretaries

Interior Minister Luis Arrau asked for the resignation of both undersecretaries at the Security Ministry — Ana Victoria Quintana and Andrés Jouannet — after just 83 days on the job. Quintana had been dogged by reports linking her partner to the defense of drug trafficking suspects. Arrau is calling it a routine team reconfiguration, but the timing — the day after the Cuenta Pública — raises eyebrows. The government quickly named Pilar Giannini as the replacement undersecretary; early reactions from opposition senators have been cautiously positive.

Crime

A Bank Executive Was Arrested in a Major Tren de Aragua Crackdown

José Pérez Asencio, a banking sector executive, was among those detained in a sweeping operation targeting the Venezuelan criminal gang Tren de Aragua. Investigators say a network connected to the gang used Chilean bank accounts and cryptocurrency to move roughly $78 billion pesos (~$87M USD) out of the country. The arrest of a white-collar professional signals the gang has moved well beyond street-level crime into the financial system.

Economy

Chile's Treasury Is Pushing Toward Its Debt Ceiling — and Growth Is Slipping

Finance Minister Jorge Quiroz is asking Congress to authorize $6.2 billion more in borrowing for 2026, which would push Chile's public debt to between 44.6–44.8% of GDP — right up against the self-imposed 45% ceiling. The timing is uncomfortable: the OECD this week cut its Chile growth forecast to just 1.7% for the year, and Banco de Chile's chief economist said the first half of 2026 will likely show negative GDP growth. Copper, at least, is near historic highs ($6.61/lb) due to uncertainty over US tariffs.

Trade

Trump's New Forced-Labor Tariffs Would Hit Chile at 12.5%

The Trump administration is proposing a new round of tariffs tied to a country's stance on forced labor. Countries without a legal prohibition on importing goods made with forced labor — Chile included — would face a 12.5% tariff. The US has set a June 22 deadline for stakeholder testimony. Chile's export economy depends heavily on copper, lithium, fruit, and wine shipped to the US, so this is a live threat that the Kast administration will need to respond to.
