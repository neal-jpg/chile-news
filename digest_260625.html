Economy · Congress

Senate Passes Kast's Sweeping Economic Reform — But Just Barely

Chile's Senate approved the first-round (general) reading of President Kast's flagship economic package on Wednesday, 26 votes to 23, with one abstention. The bill — branded the "National Reconstruction" mega-reform and sometimes called the megarreforma — would cut the corporate tax rate, introduce full tax integration (letting shareholders fully credit corporate taxes paid against their personal income-tax bill), and lock in 25-year tax certainty for large investments. Think of it as Chile's version of the 2017 Trump tax cuts: a right-wing government with a thin legislative majority pushing supply-side reform and daring the opposition to block growth. Finance Minister Jorge Quiroz, who has become the face of the reform, framed the vote in stark terms before it was cast: "If we don't grow at 3% or more sustainably, the fiscal problem has no solution."

The margin tells the whole story of Kast's governing position: he got exactly his coalition's votes and not one more. Senate President Paulina Núñez (from Kast's own party, RN) had actually tried to postpone the vote to build a broader majority — Quiroz overruled her and pushed forward. The opposition, which includes the left-of-center Frente Amplio and Communist Party, called the process a shove job: "They had no dialogue," said one opposition senator. The bill now goes back to the Finance Committee for article-by-article review; the deadline to submit amendments is July 6. The detailed debate — where the real political horse-trading happens — is just beginning.

Economy · Fiscal Policy

Chile's Budget Watchdog Calls Quiroz's Bluff — and Warns of a Real Debt Problem

Chile's Consejo Fiscal Autónomo (CFA) — think of it as Chile's CBO, a nonpartisan independent body that monitors the government's budget — released a blunt report this week with two findings that caught attention. First, it rejected Finance Minister Quiroz's claim that the previous Boric government hid $10.6 billion in fiscal deterioration; the CFA found "no arithmetic inconsistency" in Boric-era debt projections, directly undercutting Quiroz's political narrative. Second — and more alarming — the CFA warned that Chile's structural deficit is in "persistent deterioration" and that on its current trajectory, public debt would reach 48% of GDP by the early 2030s. That's not catastrophic by global standards (the US is well over 100%), but it would cross thresholds that make borrowing significantly more expensive for Chile and limit the government's ability to respond to crises. Congress is simultaneously advancing the largest single-year public borrowing authorization in Chilean history — roughly $6.2 billion — deepening the tension.

Politics · Congress

The Grau Impeachment Reaches the Senate — Vote Set for June 30

The constitutional accusation against Nicolás Grau, who served as Economy Minister under ex-President Boric, cleared the Chamber of Deputies 77–68 on Tuesday and is now the Senate's problem. The Senate votes on June 30 — just five days away. A constitutional accusation is roughly Chile's version of a congressional impeachment: it requires a majority in both chambers to result in censure and suspension from public office. Grau is already out of government, so the practical stakes are career-level rather than power-level. The accusation was filed by the hard-right Republicans and Libertarians — Kast's far-right flank — who allege Grau concealed the true state of the fiscal deficit. The center-right core of Kast's coalition (RN, UDI) is visibly uncomfortable; many consider the process an overreach. Multiple analysts put the accusation's chances in the Senate at low — the left has unified behind Grau and several governing-coalition senators have expressed reservations.

Justice · Corruption

Caso Audios: Ex-Judge and Star Lawyer Formally Charged in Corruption Scandal

Chile's most talked-about legal scandal added a major chapter this month: former Court of Appeals judge Verónica Sabaj and superstar attorney Luis Hermosilla were formally charged in a new branch of the Caso Audios investigation. Hermosilla — Chile's most connected and expensive lawyer, whose secretly recorded conversations triggered the entire scandal — faces charges of bribery and breach of confidentiality. Sabaj, a now-former judge, is accused of leaking confidential court information to Hermosilla in exchange for career favors; prosecutors allege she gave him inside access to sealed rulings, including one related to ex-President Piñera's controversial Dominga mining project. Sabaj received full house arrest; Hermosilla nighttime house arrest. The case spans the business elite, the judiciary, and the political world — Chile's version of a sprawling corruption inquiry that keeps expanding the further investigators dig.

Politics · Kast Government

Kast Convenes Cabinet at 100-Day Mark: 44% Approval, Mixed Balance

President Kast convened his fifth cabinet council this week as his government crosses the 100-day milestone, announcing a target to create 50,000 new jobs by October. His government is pointing to security improvements — homicides down 11.7%, sexual crimes down 27.1% versus the same period last year — as its clearest early wins. But the economic picture is murkier: Kast campaigned on 3.5–4% GDP growth, while independent forecasters are projecting closer to 1.6% for 2026 (Fitch). Polling is mixed: one Cadem survey this week puts approval at 44%, though other recent polls have shown figures in the mid-30s depending on methodology and timing. Kast's own characterization: "These have been quite intense 100 days." That much, all sides agree on.

A 34-Year-Old Santiago Artisan Fair Was Given One Week to Pack Up and Leave

The Pío Nono artisan fair — a fixture in the Bellavista neighborhood for 34 years, directly in front of the University of Chile's law school — received notice this week that vendors have until July 1 to clear out. Metro Line 7 construction begins on that date. The 18 remaining vendors, most of them elderly men and women who've worked the fair for decades, say a promised spot inside the future station never materialized. "We were told we'd be integrated," one told The Clinic. "Now they send us a paper saying we have a week." The vendors are considering legal action. The Pío Nono fair isn't just nostalgia — it was the kind of affordable neighborhood institution that made Bellavista Bellavista before the real estate developers and the organized crime and now the Metro construction arrived to remake it simultaneously.

"Constitutional Accusation or Institutional Trivialization?"

El Mostrador — Chile's left-leaning digital outlet, free to read — published a column Thursday arguing that the constitutional accusation against Nicolás Grau has crossed a line from accountability into institutional abuse. The argument: when a serious constitutional tool gets fired off reflexively as a political weapon, it loses its legitimacy — not just for this use, but for every future use. The American analogy is close at hand: after years of impeachments deployed for partisan signaling rather than genuine high-crimes, the tool's credibility in the US has eroded to near zero. The columnist warns Chile is heading the same direction. Worth reading if you want to understand why even some in the center-right are nervous about what the Republicans and Libertarians set in motion.

"The Price of Political Noise — and the Need for Deals"

La Tercera's editorial pages — center-right and broadly supportive of Kast's economic direction — published a piece this week urging the governing coalition to dial down the confrontation. The argument is essentially pragmatic: ideological fights, constitutional accusations, and culture-war signaling have a cost in legislative currency that Kast can't afford right now. The megarreforma's 26–23 Senate margin is thin; one defection kills it. Getting the economic reform agenda across the line requires building bridges, not burning them. The column lands as a gentle warning from a natural ally: you're making enemies you'll need later.
