Economy

IMF Downgrades Chile's 2026 Growth to 1.8% and Presses Kast to Cut More

The International Monetary Fund wrapped up its annual review of Chile's economy — a process called the Article IV Consultation, basically an independent audit by the world's top financial watchdog — and cut its 2026 GDP growth forecast from 2.2% to 1.8%. The Fund praised Chile's solid institutions and noted that inflation had returned near the Banco Central's 3% target, but warned it has started drifting back up due to higher energy prices tied to the Middle East conflict.

The IMF's main message to Finance Minister Javiera Quiroz: more fiscal tightening is needed. Specifically, the government must make "additional fiscal efforts" to keep public debt below 45% of GDP and reach a structurally balanced budget by 2030 — Kast's stated goal. The Fund flagged the National Reconstruction Plan (fire recovery spending from earlier this year) as a priority but said it needs careful sequencing to avoid blowing the deficit. The Banco Central is expected to cut its 4.5% benchmark rate within a year, with markets pricing it at around 4.26%.

Markets

Foreign Investors Are Piling Into Chilean Bonds at the Fastest Pace on Record

Foreign holdings of Chilean peso-denominated government bonds surged a record US$2.93 billion in May, hitting US$20.2 billion — a 36% increase since January, the biggest jump in any Latin American country. International money managers are betting on Chile because the peso has weakened about 7% since U.S. airstrikes on Iran in late February (now near 920 per dollar vs. 852 then), making bonds cheaper in dollar terms; the Banco Central is expected to cut rates; and Kast's pro-market government is seen as more fiscally disciplined than his predecessor.

One-year inflation breakevens fell more than 200 basis points since early May to just 2.33%, reinforcing the rate-cut bet. For context, think of it as Wall Street saying "Chilean government debt is on sale." That's a vote of confidence in Kast's economic direction — though the IMF's 1.8% growth forecast (see above) shows the bonanza hasn't translated to Main Street yet.

Politics

Kast Steps In to Cool Right-Wing Infighting After Failed Constitutional Attack

Chile's right-wing governing coalition cracked open after ultra-right lawmakers aligned with Kast's Partido Nacional Libertario (PNL) — think Freedom Caucus — pushed an acusación constitucional (a congressional censure process that can remove officials, roughly like the Senate impeachment mechanism) against a government figure, only to have moderate lawmakers in the Chile Vamos coalition — think mainstream GOP establishment — refuse to back it. The attempt failed, and the finger-pointing between the two blocs followed immediately.

PNL senator Johannes Kaiser said Chile Vamos couldn't simultaneously hold cabinet positions and "hit the government" — echoing the Freedom Caucus complaint that establishment Republicans undercut their own president. Kast flew to Antofagasta and met with regional allies in an attempt to restore "political coordination." His government controls only about 76 of 155 lower-house seats, meaning any coalition fracture threatens his legislative agenda.

Environment

El Niño Is Back — and Could Turn 'Very Strong' by Late Spring

Chile's meteorological services confirmed that El Niño has returned and will accompany the country for the next 9–12 months. The phenomenon — a periodic warming of the Pacific Ocean that reshuffles global weather — is currently rated "strong" but could escalate to "very strong" by late spring or early summer 2026–27, which would significantly raise the probability of above-normal rainfall for central Chile in the spring months (September–November).

For Santiago, that means a wetter-than-usual spring is likely on the horizon, after years of devastating drought. El Niño years can also bring flooding to already-affected zones south of Santiago and disrupt the ski season in the Andes. The last very strong El Niño (2015–16) produced severe flooding across Chile's central valley.

Health · Society

Chile on Track to Become One of the World's Fattest Countries by 2040

A new public health projection found that 31% of Chilean adults are already classified as obese — and if current trends continue, that figure will hit 48% by 2040, placing Chile among the world's most obese nations. The country already ranks as one of Latin America's most overweight populations, driven by a dramatic diet shift toward ultra-processed food over the past two decades. Chile was actually a global pioneer in junk-food labeling laws (those "stop sign" warning labels on packaging, introduced in 2016), but the data suggests the policy hasn't been enough to reverse the trajectory.

Rock
Rock Indie

Los Tres — XCLNT

The biggest Chilean rock album in a generation finally landed in April. Los Tres — Álvaro Henríquez, Ángel Parra, Roberto "Titae" Lindl, and Francisco "Pancho" Molina — recorded XCLNT at Abbey Road with their original lineup for the first time since the 1990s, ending a 26-year wait for a studio album from the group that essentially defined Chilean rock. The lead single "Cantar y Amar" set the tone: the classic Los Tres energy intact, not chasing trends. For fans of: Soda Stereo's later period, Caetano Veloso's rock experiments, early Pearl Jam. This is the album you put on if you want to understand what Chilean rock means to Chileans.

Compilado Chile 2026 — Hotel Records

Hotel Records put out a compilation of 15 Chilean independent bands in May, drawn from a call-out that attracted over 100 applicants. If you want a single-album tour of the current Chilean indie underground without having to curate your own playlist, this is the one.
