Politics

Senate Rejects Impeachment of Boric's Ex-Finance Chief — A Stinging Defeat for Kast's Coalition

Chile's Senate voted overwhelmingly Tuesday to reject the constitutional accusation (Chile's version of an impeachment proceeding) against Nicolás Grau, who served as Finance Minister under former President Boric. The ruling coalition had pushed the charge, claiming Grau misrepresented fiscal deficit projections on his way out the door. Every chapter of the accusation failed by wide margins — the closest vote was 16 in favor and 25 against on Chapter 1, far short of the 26 needed to convict. Grau walks away clear.

The fallout inside Kast's camp is ugly. Coalition senators broke ranks to vote against their own side's accusation, leading to an immediate round of finger-pointing. Senator Rodolfo Carter called it a "bochorno" — an embarrassment — and demanded that future actions of this magnitude be coordinated and discussed beforehand within the coalition. Senator Andrés Longton echoed the criticism: "there needs to be a coalition culture." With the vote coming as Kast is simultaneously trying to pass his sweeping economic bill through the same Senate, the optics could not be worse. Think of it as the Senate Republican caucus publicly humiliating a Republican president on his signature issue — the same week he needs those votes for a tax bill.

Foreign Policy

Kast Makes His Mercosur Debut — Pushes Regional Anti-Crime Alliance, Then Flies to Uruguay

President Kast attended the LXVIII Mercosur Summit in Asunción, Paraguay on June 30 — his second international trip since taking office in March. Standing before the heads of state of Brazil, Argentina, Uruguay, and Paraguay, Kast made a pointed call for a coordinated regional security architecture to fight organized crime: shared intelligence systems, joint border controls, and a crackdown on money laundering. Mercosur is roughly the South American equivalent of a trade and diplomatic bloc — think a less integrated version of the EU. Chile is an "associate" member, not a full member, but participates in summits.

On July 1 Kast flew to Montevideo for a bilateral meeting with Uruguay's left-wing President Yamandú Orsi — a notable ideological contrast. The two leaders made joint statements and their foreign ministers signed bilateral cooperation agreements. Kast's framing of organized crime as the region's top crisis is politically useful at home, where security is his strongest issue in polling — but his bilateral diplomacy with a left-wing Uruguayan president signals pragmatic engagement over ideology.

Legislation

Kast's Big Economic Bill Enters Its Toughest Week — Amendment Deadline Is July 6

The "Ley Miscelánea" — Kast's sweeping National Reconstruction and Economic Development bill — moves into its article-by-article Senate debate this week, with the deadline for amendments falling on July 6. The bill, which passed the Senate's general vote by only 26–23 on June 24, packages about 40 economic measures: cutting the corporate tax rate from 27% to 23% over three years, a one-time 10% capital repatriation window for overseas assets, employer subsidies to reduce labor costs, and reconstruction funding for 4,400 homes lost to last year's Biobío and Ñuble wildfires. Kast has said it's "very important" to have the law dispatched before August.

The problem: that 26–23 margin means a single defection kills any provision. The opposition — the Frente Amplio and Socialist parties, think the left flank of the Democratic Party — has signaled it plans to fight the corporate tax cuts and labor flexibility measures clause by clause. After this week's Grau debacle, the government needs to quickly rebuild goodwill it just burned through, and has publicly signaled it is now open to bipartisan dialogue on specific articles.

School Break Kicks Off With Bookfairs, Comic Con, and a K-Pop Cosplay Competition

Chile's winter school vacation officially begins next week (most Santiago schools break July 7–17), and the capital is front-loading the fun. This week alone: a book fair at Plaza Ñuñoa with 50+ publishers running July 1–5; "Lluvia de Libros," the beloved indie press fair at the Centro Cultural Gabriela Mistral (GAM) in downtown Santiago; and the 15th edition of Comic Con Chile, the biggest pop-culture event of the year, at the Espacio Riesco convention center July 3–5. The Comic Con features international guests, cosplay tournaments, K-Pop competitions, and gaming zones. Tickets range from 10,000 to 20,000 CLP (~$10–20 USD). If you've been avoiding the city center, this is the week it comes alive — despite the cold.

Inti Raymi Returns to the Atacama: 37 Dance Groups Celebrate the Indigenous New Year

While Santiago shivers through generic winter, the city of Calama in Chile's northern Atacama Desert recently celebrated Inti Raymi — the Quechua "Festival of the Sun," the Andean New Year tied to the winter solstice. This year's edition brought together 37 indigenous dance groups, making it one of the largest gatherings of Atacameño and Aymara cultural expression in the country. Inti Raymi has been observed in Chile's north for millennia: communities light bonfires at dawn on the solstice, welcoming the sun's gradual return. The Clinic's coverage noted the implicit editorial point: "Santiago is not Chile" — the cultural life happening in the north and south rarely makes the capital's radar. Worth remembering the next time the news feels very Santiago-centric.

"A Government That Can't Define Itself"

José Joaquín Brunner — one of Chile's most respected public intellectuals and a former minister — published a sharp column this week arguing that Kast's government, four months in, still lacks a coherent governing identity. He identifies at least four ideological strains pulling in different directions: a Guzmanista corporatism that distrusts political parties, a national-security doctrine that prizes "protected democracy," a Christian-family moralism, and a full confidence in deregulated capitalism. These don't coexist harmoniously, and the result is a government that swings between perpetual crisis mode and aspirational normality without settling on either. His most pointed question: when the "emergency" narrative runs out of steam, what is left? The piece isn't a polemic — Brunner grants that governing is hard — but it's a diagnosis the government would be wise to take seriously. Today's Senate Grau debacle looks, in retrospect, like Exhibit A.

"Security in Chile in Perspective: Organized Crime Has Changed the Country's Risk Calculus"

Ex-Ante's analyst Pablo Urquizar makes the empirical case for why Kast's security-first agenda is grounded in real numbers: homicides in Chile are up nearly 80% over the last decade; extortion has risen more than 7,000%. The piece pushes back on the idea that Kast is manufacturing a crisis for political purposes — the crime surge is documented and structural, driven by sophisticated transnational organizations (including Tren de Aragua from Venezuela and local narco networks) that have moved beyond reactive street crime into strategic territorial control and money laundering. Urquizar argues Chile needs exactly the kind of regional security architecture Kast championed at the Mercosur summit — bilateral intelligence sharing, coordinated border controls, and a unified financial crimes framework. His implicit critique of the Boric years: treating security as a social problem rather than a law-enforcement one cost Chile four years it didn't have.
