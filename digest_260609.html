Politics · Congress vs. the Old Guard

Congress Moves to Impeach the Former Finance Minister — and Kast's Own Team Is Nervous About It

The right-wing majority that now controls Congress is filing a constitutional accusation against Nicolás Grau, who was finance minister under the previous left-wing Boric government. A constitutional accusation is Chile's closest equivalent to impeachment, and it can be aimed at former ministers for actions taken in office. With President Kast's coalition holding the votes, it has a real chance of passing.

The twist: Kast's own government is wary of it. Claudio Alvarado, the senior "bi-minister" running Interior and the government's communications, pointedly declined to cheer it on — saying Congress is "sovereign" to investigate but warning it "could affect the climate" of legislative debate and risk a "spiral of accusations" that "discredits politics." His real worry is practical: the government wants the opposition's votes for its big reconstruction reform, and a partisan impeachment fight could blow up those negotiations. Think of a US administration quietly wishing its own congressional allies would drop an impeachment so it can keep a bipartisan bill alive.

Economy

The Government Is Draining the Bank Accounts of Student-Loan Defaulters — and Won't Stop

Finance Minister Jorge Quiroz is defending the Treasury's move to seize the bank accounts of people who defaulted on the CAE, Chile's state-backed student loan program (a rough cousin of US federal student loans). Borrowers say accounts were "emptied" overnight. Quiroz's response: most of the roughly 1,500 people seized so far earn over 3 million pesos a month, the unpaid loans cost taxpayers US$500 million a year, and "debts get paid — we'll keep collecting." He did promise a softer option for those earning under 3.5 million pesos. The left is furious; one bloc called it "a tremendous immorality."

Crime & Security

A State-Bank Employee Was Feeding Account Data to the Tren de Aragua

New details from "Operation Tokio": a BancoEstado employee allegedly collaborated with the Venezuelan crime syndicate Tren de Aragua, and her partner took part in the extortion scheme directly. BancoEstado — Chile's only state-owned bank — is distancing itself, calling her an "external" contractor. Fourteen people are now in preventive detention, and a congressman is pushing for an investigative commission into how deep the gang's reach into the banking system goes.

Immigration

From the Northern Border, Kast Rolls Out Two New Immigration Bills

Speaking from the Chacalluta border crossing near Arica, President Kast announced two bills delivering on his core campaign promise: one would extend how long the state can detain foreigners who already have deportation orders, and the other would stiffen penalties for human smuggling. It's a familiar US-style pairing — get tougher on the people being moved illegally and on the networks moving them. Expect a fight in Congress over the detention provision in particular.

Trade · Chile & the US

Chile's Government and Business Leaders Team Up Against a US Tariff Threat

Chile's Foreign Ministry and the country's main business guilds agreed to present a united front against the threat of new US tariffs, after Minister Pérez Mackenna met with industry leaders. "We're confident," the ministry said. For a country whose economy runs on copper, lithium, fruit, and wine sold into the US market, even the possibility of tariffs is enough to get the public and private sectors rowing in the same direction — a notable show of cross-sector unity under a government that usually leans hands-off on the economy.
