National Crisis · Immigration · Child Safety

31 Haitian Children Still Missing as Government Races Through the Weekend to Find Them

Chile's most alarming story of the week is playing out this weekend: investigators are working to locate 31 Haitian minors who cannot be found at the addresses they were registered under when they entered the country. The case began with a Contraloría preliminary report — Chile's constitutional government watchdog — that revealed a stunning lapse in the national migration service (Sermig): in 2024, under the outgoing Boric government, officials processed the entry of 486 Haitian children on charter flights accompanied by only 12 adults, with minimal documentation checks. Adults who claimed to be the children's relatives weren't verified. Addresses were taken at face value. Children were registered in batches.

The Contraloría's report triggered a criminal investigation by federal prosecutors into possible human trafficking. Investigators from Chile's Criminal Investigation Police (PDI) — the country's equivalent of the FBI — have so far located 33 of the 64 children flagged in the pre-report. Minister of Interior Carolina Wulf briefed the press on Saturday, and the government announced it expected to locate the remaining 31 this weekend through door-to-door checks by local municipalities. The Undersecretary of Childhood said no reports of harm had emerged, and that some of the "missing" children may simply be at addresses that weren't accurately recorded. But prosecutors have cautioned the number of cases under review could grow — with 200 cases mentioned as a conservative estimate and possibly double that.

Legislation · Family Policy

Universal Daycare Bill Clears Senate Committee — Full Floor Vote Comes Monday

Chile's Universal Sala Cuna (daycare) bill — one of President Kast's flagship social policies — clears a major hurdle this week as the Senate Education Commission approved it and forwarded it to the full Senate floor with "Immediate Discussion" urgency, meaning a vote must happen fast. The bill overhauls the existing system, which only requires companies with 20 or more female employees to provide or fund daycare. Under the new law, every employer — from a large corporation to a small business — must fund daycare access starting from their first hire. The benefit also extends for the first time to fathers and to self-employed workers who pay into the system.

It's funded through a new 0.35% employer payroll contribution, offset by an equal reduction in unemployment insurance contributions, so employers see no net increase in labor costs. Kast signed the amendments himself in a ceremony at the Palacio de La Moneda, with the Senate President present — a sign the government is treating this as a signature win. For working mothers and families, it would be the most significant change to childcare access in decades. The Senate floor vote is expected to begin Monday, June 22.

Economy · Markets

Copper Holds, Oil Cheap, IPSA Up: Chile's Markets End the Week on a Quiet High

Chile's benchmark IPSA stock index closed Friday up 0.47% at 10,888.43, a second consecutive day of gains driven by firm copper prices. Copper — which accounts for roughly half of Chile's export revenue and is effectively the country's economic pulse — is holding steady as global demand stays resilient. Meanwhile, Brent crude oil fell about 8% on the week after an Israel-Hezbollah ceasefire tamped down energy market jitters. Since Chile imports nearly all of its oil, cheaper fuel is a quiet gift to Chilean households and businesses.

The bright market news sits in deliberate contrast with the domestic picture: the Chilean economy grew near-zero in the first quarter, and unemployment is stuck at a five-year high of 9.1%. Kast's government is betting that the copper supercycle, combined with its deregulation agenda, will eventually translate that resource wealth into domestic jobs — but that transmission hasn't happened yet. For now, Chile's prosperity remains more visible in financial markets than in the labor market. The peso is also holding relatively firm; watch for currency pressure if the US dollar strengthens this week.

Fiscal · Congress

$6.2 Billion Debt Authorization Enters the Senate — This Week's Vote Will Define the Tone

After the Chamber of Deputies approved it 94–52, the Kast government's emergency borrowing bill — authorizing up to $6.2 billion in additional debt for 2026 — now faces the Senate, where the math is tighter and the debate is expected to be sharper. The bill cleared the Senate Finance Committee this week and goes to the full chamber as a "misceláneo" (a procedural stage where senators can debate and propose last-minute changes). Finance Minister Jorge Quiroz argues the borrowing is unavoidable: the inherited fiscal gap includes $3.8B in revenue shortfalls, $1.5B in overdue payments to government suppliers, and nearly $900M in exchange-rate losses.

The left — Frente Amplio and Partido Socialista, think of them as the Bernie Sanders and moderate-Democrat wings of Chile's Congress — is pushing back hard, accusing the government of using the fiscal crisis as political cover to squeeze future spending. The government needs centrist votes (from the DC and PDG parties, the rough equivalent of moderate independents) to cross the finish line in the Senate. Think of it as a Chilean debt-ceiling fight where both sides are scoring political points while time runs out. A vote is expected later this week.

Culture · Mapuche New Year

The Winter Solstice Is Also New Year's Day for Chile's Largest Indigenous Community

Today is not just the astronomical winter solstice — it's also the We Tripantu, the Mapuche New Year. Chile's Mapuche people, roughly 1.8 million strong and concentrated in the Araucanía and Bio-Bio regions (think: the Pacific Northwest of Chile), mark the solstice as the point when the sun begins its return and the Earth renews itself. Ceremonies include community bonfires, ritual cleansing in rivers, and shared meals that begin before dawn and carry through to sunrise. June 21 was officially designated Chile's Día Nacional de los Pueblos Indígenas in 2021 to formally recognize this tradition. Since it falls on a Sunday this year, it's not generating an extra day off — but it remains a meaningful day in Chilean public life, particularly as the Araucanía conflict between the Mapuche and the Chilean state simmers as a persistent political backdrop.

Education · Good News

Record 97,000 Students Enter Chilean Universities in 2026 — And It's the Poorest Quintiles Leading the Surge

The 2026 university admissions cycle broke records: 97,000 students who finished high school last year started higher education, the most ever recorded in Chile. What makes the number particularly striking is where the growth is coming from — the lowest-income quintiles (the bottom 40% by household income) posted their highest intake figures ever. Chile has invested heavily in tuition-free university access (gratuidad) over the past decade, and the numbers suggest it's working: kids from families that historically couldn't afford university are now getting in at record rates. Chile still has significant gaps in education quality and completion rates, but this intake number is a genuine bright spot worth noting.

The Clinic · Left-Leaning

"¿Dónde están?" — Where Are They?

The Clinic's editorial board published a scathing piece on the Haitian children case that cuts to the most uncomfortable questions: Why were the warnings ignored? A Sermig official who flagged the irregular charter flights internally was apparently brushed off. The Defensoría de la Niñez (Chile's equivalent of a Children's Ombudsman) sent formal letters requesting information months before the crisis became public — and got nothing back. The editorial traces a pattern of bureaucratic indifference under the previous government and asks, with barely contained fury, how a country that prides itself on rule of law allowed hundreds of children to pass through its borders with virtually no accountability for where they ended up. The sharpest line: "The permits were processed in series, the minors transferred in groups, the data provided by the applicants themselves, and the addresses declared where some children later disappeared."

The Timeline of the Haitian Children Scandal: Who Knew What, and When

The news site Ex-Ante published a comprehensive chronology of the Haitian children case on June 20, detailing how the chain of failures unfolded over more than two years. Starting with the first flagged charter flight, through the DGAC official who tried to sound the alarm, to the Contraloría pre-report that finally forced the issue into public view. The chronology is important because it establishes that this was not a sudden discovery — it was a slow accumulation of ignored alerts. For Kast's government, the political upside is clear: this happened on Boric's watch. The political risk is that their own response is now on the clock, and any child who remains unaccounted for next week becomes their problem.
