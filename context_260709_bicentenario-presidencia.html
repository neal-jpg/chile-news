200 Years of the Chilean Presidency

The short version: On July 9, 1826, Admiral Manuel Blanco Encalada became Chile's first constitutionally elected president — marking the formal birth of the executive branch as a distinct institution. Today, in 2026, Chile marks that bicentennial under its 36th president, José Antonio Kast.

How it works in Chile: Chile gained independence from Spain in 1818 (after the Battle of Chacabuco a year earlier). The first years were messy — the country cycled through various forms of governance before the 1826 election gave the presidency its permanent shape. The Chilean presidency has historically been one of the strongest executive offices in Latin America, giving the president broad powers to initiate legislation, declare emergencies, and control the budget. The current constitution (2005, itself a rewrite of Pinochet's 1980 charter) preserves this strong-executive tradition. Presidents serve a single four-year term and cannot be immediately re-elected.

American analogy: Imagine if the U.S. marked the 200th anniversary of the first presidency under George Washington, with every living ex-president gathering at the White House — Obama, Bush, Trump, Biden — for a formal state lunch. That's roughly what's happening today at La Moneda: President Kast is hosting former presidents Gabriel Boric (2022–2026) and Eduardo Frei Ruiz-Tagle (1994–2000), plus the family of the late Sebastián Piñera. The notable absence is Michelle Bachelet, who served twice (2006–2010, 2014–2018) but is abroad pursuing her candidacy for UN Secretary-General.

Why it matters today: The ceremony is also a reminder of Chile's unusual political stability for a Latin American country — 200 years of unbroken constitutional presidents (with the painful exception of the 1973–1990 Pinochet dictatorship). Kast, who is ideologically on the right, hosting Boric, who is on the left, in a shared moment of institutional respect carries weight in a politically divided country. The event also falls on the same calendar date as the annual commemoration of the Battle of La Concepción — July 9, 1882 — where 77 Chilean soldiers were surrounded and killed in Peru during the War of the Pacific, refusing to surrender. A flag ceremony this morning honors both milestones.
