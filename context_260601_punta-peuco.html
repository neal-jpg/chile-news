Background

What is Punta Peuco — and why does moving prisoners there matter?

June 1, 2026 · 3-minute read

The basics

Punta Peuco is a maximum-security prison located north of Santiago, built in the 1990s specifically to house elderly and ill perpetrators of crimes against humanity committed during the Pinochet dictatorship (1973–1990). It was designed as a humane facility for aging convicts — better medical care, smaller cells, calmer environment — so that imprisonment wouldn't amount to a death sentence by neglect.

Why it's politically explosive

For survivors and human rights advocates, Punta Peuco has always been a flashpoint. Critics argued for decades that former military officers convicted of torture, disappearances, and murder were living in relatively comfortable conditions while their victims never received full justice. The facility became a symbol of impunity for Chile's unresolved past.

In recent years, the prison's population has been mixed — some ordinary criminals have served time there alongside the Pinochet-era convicts. That's what makes today's story significant: President Kast admitted he personally ordered the transfer of three ordinary criminals out of Punta Peuco. Critics, including prominent human rights lawyer Héctor Gajardo, argue this effectively segregates the Pinochet-era convicts again, restoring a degree of the special treatment that human rights groups fought so hard to end.

American analogy: Imagine if a president quietly transferred ordinary inmates out of a federal facility that housed convicted war criminals — leaving only the war criminals in a more comfortable setting. Even if technically legal, it would read as a political signal about how the government views those convicts.

Why it matters today

President Kast is from the far right and has historically been associated with defenders of Pinochet's legacy (his brother Miguel Kast was a Pinochet-era official). His decision to intervene in Punta Peuco's prisoner mix — on the eve of his first Cuenta Pública — has reignited a debate that Chile has never fully closed: accountability for dictatorship-era crimes, and whether the current government is rolling back progress on that front.

Gajardo's reaction — "this is a very bad decision that takes us backwards" — captures the alarm among human rights circles. Kast's defense was blunt: "It's my prerogative."
