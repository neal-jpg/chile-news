Politics · The Impeachment Math

The Move to Impeach Boric's Old Finance Minister Now Has the Votes — and It's Splitting the Right

The constitutional accusation (Chile's version of impeachment) against Nicolás Grau, finance minister under the previous left-wing Boric government, is no longer a long shot. Vote-counting in the Chamber of Deputies suggests it could draw up to 90 votes — comfortably past the majority needed to send it to the Senate. The charge centers on how public-debt figures were calculated and disclosed to Congress.

The interesting fault line is on the right itself. The mainstream center-right bench (RN) is openly divided — "in favor, undecided, and against," as one outlet put it — even though the right broadly controls Congress under President Kast. Some see a clean shot at the old guard; others worry a partisan impeachment will sour the climate just as the government needs opposition votes for its own agenda. On the left, even Pamela Jiles is asking the awkward question out loud: why go after Grau and not Boric himself? Picture a US House where the majority has the votes to impeach a former cabinet secretary but can't agree among themselves whether it's worth the blowback.

Politics · Opposition

Boric Reappears Swinging — Hammering Kast on the Public Debt

Three months into Kast's presidency, former president Boric has popped back into the spotlight to attack the new government's economic numbers, throwing the rising public-debt figures back in its face. La Moneda (Chile's White House) is playing it down, reading the opposition's sudden noise as a sign of disorganized messaging rather than a coordinated offensive — several left-wing factions talking over each other rather than landing a unified hit. It's the familiar spectacle of a just-departed president refusing to fade quietly while his side figures out who actually speaks for it.

Economy · Student Debt

The Courts Are Now Deciding the Student-Loan Fight, One Contradictory Ruling at a Time

Chile's appeals courts are issuing scattered, inconsistent verdicts on the CAE — the state-backed student loan that's been a political wound for over a decade. Dozens of borrowers have filed legal challenges over debt collection, and judges are landing all over the map, some for the borrowers, some for the state. At the same time, former Boric ministers are publicly admitting they over-promised on the forgiveness they couldn't deliver, while Boric defends the record. A signature left-wing pledge, still unresolved and now being settled courtroom by courtroom.

Crime & Security

16 Police Officers Arrested in Weeks — and the General Wants Them Gone

Sixteen Carabineros (Chile's national police force) have been detained over the past few weeks on suspicion of crimes ranging from theft to ties with organized crime. General Araya didn't mince words: those arrested "don't deserve to be in the institution." For a force that Chileans have historically trusted more than most of their government, the steady drip of officer arrests is a real reputational problem — roughly the equivalent of a wave of federal agents being cuffed by the very system they're supposed to uphold.

Government · Diplomacy

Kast's Pick for Ambassador to Israel Is Under Investigation — and the Government Is Standing Firm

The Kast government named prominent lawyer Gabriel Zaliasnik as ambassador to Israel, but the appointment opened a flank: he's the subject of a prosecutorial investigation. The foreign minister is refusing to reverse course, saying the probe is no reason to pull the nomination. It's the kind of confirmation-fight optics Americans know well — a controversial appointee, an inconvenient legal cloud, and an administration betting it can ride out the criticism rather than start over.

NASA Is Studying Farms in Northern Chile to Figure Out How to Grow Food on the Moon and Mars

NASA scientists are out in the high desert near Arica examining how crops survive the brutal altiplano — thin air, intense radiation, punishing dryness — as a model for growing food on the Moon and Mars. Chile's far north already doubles as a stand-in for other planets (its skies host the world's great observatories); now its dirt is auditioning for the role too.
