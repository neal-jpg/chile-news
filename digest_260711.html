Diplomacy · Politics

Kast Flies to Lima July 28 as Keiko Fujimori Takes Power — A Right-Wing Moment for the Pacific

President Kast held a video call with Peru's president-elect Keiko Fujimori on Friday and confirmed he'll travel to Lima for her July 28 inauguration — his first state visit to a neighboring country as president. Fujimori, 51, is the daughter of former Peruvian president Alberto Fujimori (a strongman who ruled 1990–2000 and died in prison in 2023) and is the first woman elected to Peru's presidency. She ran three times before winning this year's election. Despite her father's shadow — Alberto was convicted of human rights abuses and corruption — Keiko has built her own political identity as a tough-on-crime, pro-market conservative. The political parallel to Kast is unmistakable: both come from families associated with Chile's and Peru's respective authoritarian eras, and both ran explicitly right-of-center campaigns against what they called the "socialist drift" of their countries.

The two leaders agreed to revitalize the Pacific Alliance — a trade bloc including Chile, Peru, Colombia, and Mexico — and coordinated on migration enforcement and organized crime, shared priorities that align the two governments closely. For context, think of it as the formation of a right-leaning bloc within Latin America's equivalent of NAFTA. Kast called it "a new era for the relationship between Chile and Peru," and analysts expect the two governments to present a joint front at upcoming international forums. The optics are also pointed: Colombia's left-wing President Petro and Mexico's ruling Morena party are the other two Alliance members, so the arrival of a Fujimori government reshapes the Alliance's internal balance considerably.

Economy · Politics

Four Days Out: Senate Faces Marathon Vote on Reconstruction Law as Pressure Mounts

Wednesday July 15 is shaping up as the most consequential Senate session of Kast's presidency — the floor vote on the Plan de Reconstrucción Nacional, the sweeping economic reform package that cuts corporate taxes from 27% to 23%, creates investment stability guarantees, and includes a senior property-tax exemption. Senate President Núñez has said the chamber will vote through the night if needed until every article is decided. The PPD centrist opposition struck a deal with Finance Minister Quiroz last week that softened the contested "tax invariability" provisions, giving the government enough votes on paper — but only barely, and only if Senator Calisto (currently on medical leave and facing a separate fraud investigation) either participates or his absence doesn't tip the balance.

The Constitutional Court angle adds complexity: both the Socialist Party and the PPD's national leadership (overruling its own Senate caucus) say they'll still challenge the invariability clause at Chile's TC — roughly equivalent to the US Supreme Court — after the bill passes. The government is betting the bill becomes law and the TC either upholds it or only trims the edges. The left calls it a tax giveaway that will cost the treasury over $4 billion a year; the right says Chile desperately needs the investment signal after five straight months of economic contraction.

Economy

Senate OKs $6.2 Billion Emergency Borrowing as IMF Cuts Chile's Growth Forecast to 1.8%

In a parallel move last week, the Senate approved a law authorizing the government to borrow up to $6.2 billion (US dollars) for the current fiscal year — passed under urgency rules with little debate as the clock ran out. The measure gives the Treasury room to finance basic government operations during a period when tax revenues are underperforming forecasts due to the economic slowdown. Think of it as a congressional debt-ceiling authorization, similar in function to the annual US debt limit negotiations, though the amounts and political dynamics are different. The International Monetary Fund added to the pressure by cutting its growth forecast for Chile to just 1.8% for 2026 — a downgrade tied both to the economic contraction and to what the IMF called "uncertainty around fiscal policy" generated by the Reconstruction Law debate.

With five consecutive months of IMACEC decline (Chile's monthly economic activity index, roughly equivalent to monthly GDP) and unemployment at 9.4% — the highest in five years — the government's economic team is under intense pressure heading into the weekend. Finance Minister Quiroz continues to insist there are "no signs of recession" and points to preliminary June data he says shows a turning point. Most private analysts are less optimistic: they expect the central bank to hold its policy rate at 4.5% through year-end, and project any meaningful recovery not until 2027.

Politics

Kast Holds Regional Summit, Pledges to Cut Red Tape for Investment Outside Santiago

President Kast convened Chile's 16 regional governors at La Moneda on Friday for a working summit on investment, entrepreneurship, and "regional competitiveness." The meeting included Economy Minister Daniel Mas, Labor Minister Tomás Rau, and the Women's Ministry — a broad cross-ministry gathering signaling the government wants to decentralize the economic recovery push beyond the capital. Governors raised concerns about permitting delays for mining, renewable energy, and infrastructure projects in their regions, and Kast committed to a permitting-reform track that would run parallel to the Reconstruction Law. Chile's regional governance structure is relatively new — directly elected governors only date to 2021 (before that, all were presidential appointees) — so these summits have a slightly different character than a US governors' meeting, where regional political independence is much older and more entrenched.

Interesting & Offbeat

Forty-Five Chileans Are Now Legally Named "Messi" — and Two Are Named "Mbappé"

With the 2026 World Cup still in full swing across North America, Chilean birth registrars have been logging an unusually high volume of soccer-themed names. According to Chile's Civil Registry, at least 45 children have been formally registered with "Messi" as a first name this year — some parents going so far as to use it as a surname-style first name rather than Lionel. Two babies have been named Kylian (after Mbappé), and 972 more have been named Kylian in variant spellings. It's a phenomenon familiar to other World Cup cycles — a spike of "Ronaldos" in 2002, "Thierry" in 2006 — but the sheer number registering a surname (Messi) as a given name is new territory for Chilean civil law. The registry hasn't blocked any of them.

Opinion & Editorial
El Mostrador · Left · July 11

"El punto ciego del crecimiento" — Chile's Economic Blind Spot

Published today, this column argues that the Kast government's entire economic framework — and really the entire Chilean political debate — is fixated on the wrong variable: investment volume. The author contends Chile has spent decades asking "how much capital do we need?" without asking the harder question: "what capabilities do we need to sustain growth over the next decade?" The piece draws a distinction between adding investment and building organizational quality, R&D infrastructure, and the ability to convert knowledge into innovation. For an American analogy: it's the argument that just cutting taxes and building highways isn't enough if you haven't invested in your workforce, your universities, and your small-business ecosystem. The column lands as a direct implicit critique of the Reconstruction Law, which is largely supply-side: tax cuts and investor guarantees, with less attention to the human-capital and productivity side.

El Líbero · Right · Recent

"Chile en emergencia económica" — The Case for Acting Now

El Líbero — the most prominent explicitly right-wing digital outlet — has been running a drumbeat of columns arguing that Chile's situation constitutes a genuine emergency requiring urgent action, and that the Reconstruction Law is precisely the right tool at the right moment. The argument mirrors the classic supply-side case: when private investment stalls (Chile's investment-to-GDP ratio has fallen several years running), lowering the cost of capital and reducing regulatory uncertainty is the only lever government has to restart the engine. The column draws a direct analogy to Chile's early-1980s economic stabilization and argues that those who oppose the tax cuts on equity grounds are putting ideological purity ahead of the workers who need jobs today. The right-of-center framing here is broadly similar to the US Chamber of Commerce line during debates over corporate tax reform.

Indie Post-Rock Spoken Word

teodioteodio — Echaremos el Cielo Abajo a Patadas

Still the most-talked-about Chilean debut of 2026, three months after its March 27 release. teodioteodio — a six-piece from Santiago — released eight sprawling, noisy, emotionally charged songs that sit somewhere between Godspeed You! Black Emperor's post-rock architecture, early Refused-style hardcore, and Chilean spoken-word poetry. The album title itself is a quote from Rodrigo Lira, a canonical Chilean avant-garde poet who died in 1981; the opening track pulls lyrics directly from a Roberto Bolaño poem. The band has now received write-ups in Paste Magazine and Album of the Year alongside Chilean coverage in Rockaxis and La Rata — that cross-hemisphere reach for a debut with zero label infrastructure is genuinely unusual. For fans of: Explosions in the Sky, IDLES, or anyone who thinks rock music should feel like it means something.

Rockaxis x SCD: New Chilean Rock Cycle Launches at the SCD

Rockaxis — Chile's longest-running rock media platform — has partnered with the SCD (Sociedad Chilena del Derecho de Autor, roughly Chile's ASCAP) to run a curated live series showcasing emerging Chilean rock and indie acts. 📊 Charts

Charts: Urbano Dominates, But Chilean Acts Are Holding Spots

The Spotify Chile Top 50 this week is, as usual, dominated by Puerto Rican and Colombian reggaeton and urbano — Bad Bunny, J Balvin, Feid, Karol G. That's the commercial mainstream across all of Latin America right now. The more interesting signal for the local scene is that a handful of Chilean acts — mostly in the urban/trap category — are consistently appearing in the top 30, a share that has grown compared to five years ago. The independent rock and indie scene represented by acts like teodioteodio operates almost entirely outside the streaming-chart conversation and gets its audience through Bandcamp, YouTube, and word of mouth. Two different musical economies, mostly non-overlapping.
