Economy · Competitiveness

Chile Drops Another Spot in the Global Competitiveness Ranking — and the Employment Numbers Are a Damning Verdict

The International Institute for Management Development (IMD) released its 2026 World Competitiveness Ranking Thursday, and Chile slipped from 42nd to 43rd among the 70 economies measured — the third straight year without meaningful recovery. The headline figure understates the rot beneath it: Chile's employment sub-ranking collapsed from 47th to 55th, its domestic economy score fell from 44th to 50th, and productivity and efficiency sit at a dismal 62nd place. Investment — the one area where Chile had held moderately respectable ground — also slipped. The report is compiled in Chile by the University of Chile's Faculty of Economics and Business (FEN), whose researchers warned the results signal stagnation that market reforms alone won't fix.

The context makes this sharper: in 2005, Chile sat at 19th globally — a peer of Spain and South Korea. Two decades of slow drift later it has dropped 24 spots. Chile still leads a Latin American pack that includes Argentina (58th), Colombia (59th), Peru (60th), Mexico (62nd), and Brazil (65th) — but being the best-performing economy in a struggling region is cold comfort. The timing is brutal: the Central Bank just slashed its 2026 growth forecast to 1%–1.75%, and President Kast's stated goal of 4% annual growth looks further away than ever. Kast ran on restoring business confidence; Thursday's ranking is an early-term report card on how far that work remains.

Policy · Women's Work

Universal Daycare Bill Reaches Senate Floor — and the Financing Fight Has Begun

Chile's Senate Education Committee began active review this week of President Kast's amended Sala Cuna Universal bill, which would guarantee childcare access to all workers — male, female, or self-employed — and eliminate a century-old Labor Code rule that effectively imposed an extra cost on companies for hiring women. The government's funding model — a 0.35% levy channeled into a new Childcare Fund, offset by an equal reduction in Unemployment Insurance contributions — is generating pushback from opposition senators, who say it robs one safety-net fund to pay another. The plan phases in over four years. Senator Yasna Provoste (Christian Democrat) summed up the concern: "We don't think it's good policy to undress one saint to dress another."

Justice · Caso Audio

Ángela Vivanco Walks Out of Preventive Detention — Oral Trial Is Now About 90 Days Away

Former Supreme Court Justice Ángela Vivanco left preventive detention last week after months in custody, citing health problems serious enough to require complex medical treatment that prison conditions couldn't provide. Her lawyers have asked the court to set a date for her testimony at the upcoming oral trial. The prosecution expects the main oral trial in Chile's landmark "Caso Audio" corruption case — involving lawyer Luis Hermosilla, former Court of Appeals judge Verónica Sabaj, and others — to begin within roughly 90 days. Hermosilla faces up to 14 years in prison for bribery, tax fraud, and money laundering. For a US reader: this is Chile's equivalent of a federal corruption trial implicating sitting federal judges and a high-profile Washington attorney — the whole judicial establishment is under scrutiny.

Markets · Global Impact

Dollar Hovers Near 900 Pesos as US–Iran Agreement Moves Global Markets

The signing of a US–Iran accord on Thursday rippled through global markets: equity markets climbed, US Treasury yields fell, and the dollar weakened broadly. For Chile, the peso — under pressure from slow growth and a wide fiscal deficit — picked up a temporary tailwind, keeping the exchange rate near the psychologically significant 900-peso-per-dollar level. Copper, Chile's dominant export and a primary driver of government revenue, also firmed as risk appetite improved globally. Chilean financial analysts noted that the durability of any relief hinges on whether the deal holds and whether global investors regain enough confidence to bet on emerging markets. The peso has been one of the year's weaker currencies in the region, tracking Chile's tepid economic outlook.

Politics · Fiscal Battle

Kast's Tax-Cut Agenda Faces Growing Resistance as $6.2 Billion Debt Request Hits Congress

The Kast government has sent Congress a request to authorize US$6.2 billion in additional borrowing for 2026 — a move that arrived just weeks after the administration announced its sweeping megarreforma tributaria, which would cut corporate tax rates and reduce levies on higher earners. Opposition lawmakers from the Frente Amplio and Socialista blocs are pressing the obvious question: how do you cut taxes on top earners and then ask the public to cover the gap with more debt? Finance Minister Jorge Quiroz argues the borrowing is temporary while the reform generates investment and growth — the standard supply-side defense. Critics note that Chile's fiscal position is already strained and that the Central Bank's growth downgrade makes the growth-will-pay-for-it argument harder to sell.

The World's First Southern-Hemisphere Gamma-Ray Observatory Is Being Built in Chile's Atacama Desert

The Southern Wide-field Gamma-ray Observatory (SWGO) — a project drawing scientists from more than a dozen countries — is advancing toward construction at Pampa La Bola, 4,770 meters above sea level in Chile's Atacama region. Unlike conventional telescopes, SWGO will detect high-energy cosmic gamma rays using 6,000 sealed water tanks spread across the high plateau. When particles cascade from a distant gamma-ray burst, they disturb the water in ways the tanks can measure. The result will be a wide-angle view of the Milky Way's center, black holes, and the universe's most violent events — from a vantage point no observatory has ever had. First installations are expected in 2026, with full operations in roughly five years. Chile, already home to more optical observing capacity than any country on Earth, is adding gamma rays to the list.

Tech · Mapping

"Chile Fuera del Mapa": Photographing the Parts of Chile That Don't Exist in Google Maps

Samsung Chile launched an initiative called "Chile fuera del mapa" (Chile Off the Map) that invites people to use the Galaxy S26 Ultra to document landscapes so remote or overlooked that they don't appear in mapping apps. The project started in San Pedro de Atacama, with volunteers uploading imagery directly to Google Street View to fill in digital blind spots. It's a sharp reminder that for a country stretching 4,300 km north to south — tundra, desert, Patagonian steppe, Andean altiplano — enormous swaths of territory remain effectively invisible to the online world. Think of it as the crowdsourced version of what the USGS does for blank spots on American maps, except here a Galaxy camera is the instrument and volunteers are the fieldworkers.

"¿Dónde están?" — Where Are the Children?

The Clinic's Pamela Castro published a stinging editorial Thursday demanding accountability for the 64 Haitian children whose whereabouts remain unknown after entering Chile on irregular charter flights. Castro's argument is that what the Contraloría's (government watchdog's) pre-report actually reveals is a systemic failure — not a bureaucratic accident. The state authorized the flights, accepted the paperwork, waved the children through, and then lost track of them. That isn't negligence at the margins; it's a failure of the state's core obligation to protect children in its territory. The piece is sharp even by The Clinic's provocative standards, framing this not as an immigration story but as a child-protection scandal that demands answers from every institution involved — including the Kast administration that has made border security its flagship issue.

Chile in Two Speeds: The Capital Outperforms, the Regions Fall Further Behind

Alongside the IMD global ranking release, a regional competitiveness index published this week shows that Chile's stagnation isn't uniformly distributed: the Santiago metropolitan area continues to pull sharply ahead of the regions, and the gap is widening. Analysis from Poder y Liderazgo frames this as a structural problem no single administration can fix quickly — a country that relies on one metro area for most of its economic momentum is fragile, and the IMD data suggests Santiago's advantages are not spreading outward. For a US reader: picture a Chile where the New York–versus–everywhere-else disparity has been compounding for two decades, with no effective equivalent of federal regional development policy large enough to close it.
