What is Chile's Plan de Reconstrucción Nacional?

What it is

The Plan de Reconstrucción Nacional y Desarrollo Económico y Social — widely called the "proyecto misceláneo" (miscellaneous bill) by critics — is President José Antonio Kast's flagship economic legislation. Introduced in March 2026, it bundles more than 40 policy measures into a single omnibus law. The Chamber of Deputies approved it in May; as of July 2026 it is before the Senate, where the government hopes to pass it this month.

What's in it

The bill has five main pillars: (1) cutting the corporate income tax rate from 27% to 23% over several years; (2) a 25-year tax invariability guarantee for large investment projects — meaning companies that invest above a threshold lock in their tax terms for a quarter century; (3) hiring incentives, including tax credits for employers who bring on workers near the minimum wage; (4) streamlining environmental and permitting approvals to reduce the time it takes to build mines, infrastructure, and energy projects; and (5) rebuilding assistance for communities affected by the 2024 Viña del Mar wildfires. The "reconstruction" framing is essentially political cover for a sweeping tax and regulatory overhaul.

The American analogy

Think of it as a combination of the 2017 Tax Cuts and Jobs Act (the corporate rate cut) fused with a permitting-reform bill — packaged together with hurricane-relief funds so it couldn't easily be voted down as just a tax break for corporations.

Why it's contentious

The opposition — primarily the left and center-left parties of the former Boric coalition — argues that embedding a structural tax reform inside a reconstruction bill bypasses the normal legislative debate such changes deserve. They call the tax invariability clause "an extreme giveaway" to mining and energy multinationals. Even within Kast's own right-wing coalition, former Santiago mayor Evelyn Matthei has openly criticized parts of the plan, particularly the employment-credit design, saying it is "extremely expensive" relative to its expected impact on job creation.

The government's counter: Chile is sliding toward technical recession, with five consecutive monthly contractions and unemployment at 9.4%. There is no time for a year-long congressional debate while the economy stalls. Kast has warned he will advance the bill by a single vote if necessary — and in the Senate, that margin is essentially what he has (26–23 was the general-concept vote).

Why it matters today

The Senate's amendment deadline is July 6. After that, the particular debate begins — article by article, vote by vote — with Kast aiming to send the final bill to the Chamber before the end of July. Whether the Senate approves it intact, strips out key provisions, or blocks it entirely will define the economic trajectory of the Kast administration.
