Politics · Congress

A Key Senator Recuses Himself — and the Grau Impeachment May Die in the Senate Next Week

The constitutional accusation against former Finance Minister Nicolás Grau — which barely cleared Chile's Chamber of Deputies 77–68 on Tuesday — is now heading to the Senate for a final vote on June 30, but its prospects may already be sunk. Senator Luciano Cruz-Coke (Evópoli, part of the governing Chile Vamos coalition) announced Thursday that he would recuse himself from the vote due to a personal conflict of interest. That recusal is potentially decisive: to convict Grau, the accusation needs 26 votes in the Senate. With Cruz-Coke out, the coalition appears to be one vote short — which means an accusation that was billed as a knockout blow against the former left-wing government may end up as a self-inflicted wound for Kast's right-wing coalition instead.

A constitutional accusation is roughly Chile's version of congressional impeachment — it requires a majority in both chambers to result in censure, which would bar the accused from public office for five years. Grau is already out of government (he served as Economy Minister under ex-President Boric), so the stakes are career-level rather than power-level. The accusation was filed by the Republican Party and the Libertarian National Party — Kast's hard-right flank — who allege Grau misrepresented Chile's fiscal deficit in the government's final budget report. But the tactic has backfired politically: the center-right core of Kast's coalition (RN, UDI, Evópoli) is visibly uncomfortable, with RN accusing Republicans of "political bullying." The opposition left has unified behind Grau, and the independent Consejo Fiscal Autónomo already said it found no arithmetic inconsistency in Boric's fiscal projections — undercutting the accusation's core premise. Watch the June 30 Senate floor vote; it may turn out to be a decisive moment for who controls the narrative inside Kast's own coalition.

Immigration · Security

62 Migrants Expelled on Charter Flight as Kast's Deportation Push Grinds On

The Kast government flew 62 migrants out of the country Thursday in a charter flight to Colombia and Bolivia — the latest in a series of deportation operations that have become the administration's most visible immigration enforcement action. The director of the National Migration Service, Frank Sauerbaum, confirmed this week that 743 administrative expulsions have been completed since Kast took office in March. The gap with campaign rhetoric is enormous: Kast promised to expel 340,000 undocumented migrants; at the current pace, that would take roughly 150 years. Even Sauerbaum has acknowledged the math: "Expulsions are incredibly expensive," he said recently, which is partly why the government is pushing a voluntary return program alongside the forced removals. Meanwhile, legislation to extend the detention window for undocumented migrants from 5 to 180 days is advancing through Congress. Think of it as the Chilean equivalent of a tough immigration crackdown — the optics are there, but the scale is a rounding error on the actual problem.

Economy · Monetary Policy

Central Bank Holds Rates, Cuts Growth Forecast to as Low as 1% for 2026

Chile's Central Bank voted unanimously to hold the monetary policy rate at 4.5% at its June meeting — but the more significant move was a sharp downgrade to the growth outlook. The Bank's June monetary policy report now projects GDP growth of just 1.0%–1.75% for 2026, slicing off the possibility of reaching 2% that had been in the March forecast. The primary culprit is external: the Middle East conflict has pushed oil prices above $80 per barrel, driving inflation back up and above the 3% target. Inflation is now not expected to return to target until the second quarter of 2027. Kast's government had promised 4% annual growth — what the June report makes clear is that 2026 will deliver less than half of that. The Bank also flagged that unemployment, currently at 9.1%, is the highest since 2021, with women and youth hit hardest.

Security · Organized Crime

Organized Crime Is Now Targeting Mayors — 8 Communes Have Armed Police Guards

A troubling snapshot of how organized crime has escalated its tactics in Chile: 40 investigations are currently active into threats against local officials, and eight mayors now have permanent armed police escorts. The mayor of Lo Espejo, Javiera Reyes, went public this year saying a criminal gang offered a large sum for her life. Intimidation tactics have moved well past threatening phone calls — they now include sending funeral wreaths to municipal buildings and burning government vehicles. Chile's Contraloría General (roughly equivalent to the Government Accountability Office) stepped in this spring to clarify that municipalities can use emergency procurement procedures to buy personal security equipment for threatened officials, without going through normal bidding processes. The pattern tracks with broader trends across Latin America, where criminal organizations have moved from avoiding the state to actively trying to condition what it does — a playbook pioneered by cartels in Mexico and Colombia.

Politics · Kast Government

100+ Days In: Kast's Promises vs. Reality — a Growing Gap

Now past the 100-day mark, Kast's government has begun adjusting the language around its most ambitious promises. The president campaigned on expelling 340,000 undocumented migrants (so far: ~743), hitting 4% GDP growth (Central Bank now projects 1%–1.75%), and a rapid improvement in public safety. On safety, results are better — homicides are down 11.7% year-over-year and sexual crimes down 27.1%. His June 1 Cuenta Pública (State of the Nation address, roughly the Chilean State of the Union) laid out a 7-pronged task force approach to organized crime and pledged to create 300,000 jobs by 2030. Approval polling varies wildly by firm — one Cadem survey showed 44%, while other polls have shown figures in the mid-30s. The consensus: Kast has had a turbulent, difficult start, the economic tailwinds he needed haven't materialized, and the constitutional accusation fracas has consumed political capital he can't afford to waste while the megarreforma economic bill hangs in the balance.

"For Some Falls, Gravity Doesn't Exist" — A New Show About Fallen Dictators Opens at the Museo de la Memoria

A joint exhibition by Argentine artist David Lamelas and American artist Hildegarde Duane opened Thursday at Santiago's Museo de la Memoria y los Derechos Humanos — the museum built to document the Pinochet dictatorship's atrocities. The show, titled "Para algunas caídas no existe la gravedad" (For some falls, gravity doesn't exist), uses five video installations that deploy satire, irony, and performance to examine how political power is constructed and performed through media — featuring characters like fallen dictators, TV news anchors, and entertainment spectacle. The timing is hard to miss: a museum that commemorates one of the continent's most notorious dictatorships is now hosting an art show that asks how strongmen rise, perform, and fall. Both artists are making their Chilean debut. The exhibition is free and open to the public through August.

"The State Chile Needs: Let's Stop Arguing from False Premises"

El Mostrador — Chile's center-left digital paper, free to read — ran a column Thursday pushing back hard on the Kast government's framing of state reform as state reduction. The argument: modernizing how the state works is a legitimate goal, but the debate has been hijacked by a premise that equates smaller government with effective government, which the columnist calls historically false. Chile's state isn't too large by OECD standards; its problems are in quality of delivery, not quantity of spending. The American analogy: imagine debating the US Postal Service by saying the solution is always fewer employees, regardless of the actual logistical problem. The column is a direct counter-narrative to the Kast administration's fiscal adjustment framing and worth reading to understand the opposition's strongest economic argument.

El Líbero · Right-Leaning · June 2026

"Kast's Strategy: What We Know and What We Still Don't"

El Líbero — a conservative Chilean outlet, free to read — published a column this week from a favorable-but-demanding perspective on the Kast government. The argument: Kast's cabinet reshuffle earlier this year and his disciplined June 1 Cuenta Pública successfully repositioned him as a reasonable conservative focused on security and economic stability — not the ideological firebrand his critics caricature. The column notes that recent polling improvements suggest the repositioning has worked with voters, even if the megarreforma battle in Congress remains uncertain. The writer frames it as a government that had a rough first month (fuel price protests, deportation promises vs. reality) but found its footing. What's still missing: a clearer theory for how Kast builds legislative majority on economic reform. The column is a useful window into how supporters who can do math are evaluating the government.
