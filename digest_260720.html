Top Headlines
Emergency

Storm System Kills Five, Leaves 33,000 Isolated Across Chile

Chile's worst winter storm in years tore through ten regions from Atacama to La Araucanía over the weekend, killing five people, displacing nearly 2,000, and cutting off more than 33,000 from roads and basic services. Desert-dry Atacama and Coquimbo took the hardest hits — areas that rarely see significant rainfall received months' worth in days, collapsing ravines and flooding hospitals.

The government declared disaster zones across dozens of communes, suspended classes (some northern regions only return to school today), and deferred SII tax payment deadlines to October 30 for businesses in affected areas without penalty. President Kast has been coordinating by teleconference with ministers deployed across the country. More than 34 homes were completely destroyed; Valparaíso alone saw over 210,000 customers lose power. Santiago itself was largely spared — the storm tracked north and east of the capital — but expect residual road closures if you're driving out of the city.

Economy

Kast's Economic Megarreform Heads to Its Final Vote

The Chilean Senate narrowly passed (26–24, after a 12-hour overnight session) Kast's sweeping economic reform bill on July 16 — the Ley de Reconstrucción y Desarrollo Económico y Social, known universally as the "megarreforma." The bill now returns to the Chamber of Deputies this week for a final ratification vote. Think of it like a budget reconciliation bill clearing the Senate by the skin of its teeth and going back to the House for a final stamp.

Key provisions: a gradual cut in corporate income tax from 27% to 23% (bringing Chile toward the low end of OECD developed-nation rates), and a 20-year tax freeze for investments over $350 million — a big incentive aimed at drawing mining and energy megaprojects. The opposition, which passed an amendment banning "anatocismo" (charging interest on interest), says it will file Constitutional Court challenges by today on environmental grounds. The government believes the Chamber will hold and the law will be enacted this week.

Public Safety

After Officer's Death, Right Pushes "Ley Naín 2.0"

Cabo Marco Cosme, 32, died on July 18 after being shot in Valdivia while trying to arrest a suspect connected to the 2020 murder of Cabo Eugenio Naín — the killing that originally inspired Chile's police-protection law. President Kast declared three days of national mourning and the right immediately revived calls for a tougher follow-up law. Right-wing legislators are proposing expanded self-defense presumptions for officers and mandatory maximum sentences for anyone who kills a police officer.

Libertarian MP Johannes Kaiser floated reinstating the death penalty — a non-starter given Chile's constitution, but a sign of the political mood. The left-wing Communist Party has meanwhile been pushing to partially repeal the existing law's self-defense presumption, a collision that makes the political moment especially charged. Think of this as the "Blue Lives Matter" sentencing debate, but in a country where the 2019 civil unrest left deep wounds on both sides of the argument.

Justice

UN Accused of Meddling in the Gustavo Gatica Appeal

On July 13, the UN High Commissioner for Human Rights' representative in Chile made a formal presentation before Santiago's Court of Appeals in support of Deputy Gustavo Gatica's appeal — the student who lost both eyes to Carabineros buckshot during the 2019 uprising. The move is unprecedented: UN country offices don't typically make submissions inside active judicial proceedings. Former Interior Minister Juan Francisco Galli (Piñera era) called it an improper interference that could compromise judicial impartiality, and noted the UN is citing legal standards issued after 2019.

The left sees legitimate international human rights oversight of a system that acquitted the officer who blinded Gatica; the right calls it foreign meddling. The appeal ruling — which could overturn the acquittal and send the case to a new trial — is expected in coming weeks. Gatica, now a congressman, said the UN's support "gives us hope that justice still has a chance."

Interesting / Offbeat

Two Artists Ask: Who Decides What Power Looks Like?

An exhibition at the Museo de la Memoria examines how media constructs political authority — not through journalism, but through five video-art installations that use parody and irony to pull the curtain back. Argentine artist David Lamelas and American artist Hildegarde Duane are showing "Para algunas caídas no existe la gravedad" (For Some Falls, There Is No Gravity) — their first time exhibiting in Chile. It's sharper and funnier than the museum's usual fare, and it's completely free.

Runs through September 27, Tuesday–Sunday, 10 a.m.–6 p.m., at the Museo de la Memoria y los Derechos Humanos (Matucana 501). No tickets needed.

Opinion / Editorial
The Clinic · Left-leaning editorial · July 19

"Chile ante el cambio climático"

Editor Pamela Castro argues that the frontal system crisis is not a weather event but a diagnosis: Chile has entered a "new climate normal" that alternates between prolonged drought and sudden, violent rainfall — and the country's infrastructure and planning have not kept pace. She notes that the tools for discussing the emergency exist but the institutional will to address it structurally does not, calling for a national adaptation strategy with the same urgency as the Kast government brought to its economic reform.

The Clinic (guest) · Right-leaning column · July 19

"El Caso Gatica y una interferencia impropia de Naciones Unidas"

Former Interior Minister Juan Francisco Galli (a veteran of the Piñera government, writing as a guest at the left-leaning Clinic) argues that the UN Human Rights representative overstepped her mandate by filing a brief in the Gatica appeal. Galli frames it as a sovereignty question: Chile granted the UN office a specific operational mandate, and making submissions in active court proceedings exceeds it — regardless of one's views on the Gatica case itself. Lean: firmly right-wing; outlet: left-leaning, but the guest column gives it an unusual cross-aisle platform.

Ania Ivania — "Sana Sana"

Still Buzzing
Rock Alternative

Los Tres — XCLNT (April 2026)

Three months since its release and Los Tres' return is still the biggest story in Chilean rock. The original lineup — Álvaro Henríquez, Ángel Parra, Roberto "Titae" Lindl, Francisco "Pancho" Molina — recorded XCLNT at Abbey Road after 26 years of silence, and the result is as uncompromising as the production is immaculate. It sounds like the band picked up mid-sentence from where they left off in 1999, which is either miraculous or exactly what you'd expect from musicians who were always that good.

For fans of: La Ley, Soda Stereo, the entire 1990s Chilean alternative rock era. If you haven't listened yet, start with the singles before the full album — it earns the longer commitment.
