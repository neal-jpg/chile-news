Politics · The Impeachment Fight

Grau's Impeachment Heads to Committee as His Defense Rushes Out Its Rebuttal

The constitutional accusation (Chile's version of impeachment) against Nicolás Grau — finance minister under the previous left-wing Boric government — entered its decisive phase Monday, when the review committee in the Chamber of Deputies began its work. Grau's defense moved up the filing of its written rebuttal, trying to get ahead of the narrative, while the centrist Christian Democrats made a point of saying that, having sat in neither Boric's nor Kast's government, they'll judge the case "on the merits" — a swing bloc that could decide whether this advances.

As in the US, impeachment here is as much a partisan weapon as a legal process: the push came from the libertarian and Republican right, mainstream conservatives are split, and the math now hinges on a handful of undecided centrists. Watch the Christian Democrats — they're playing the role of the wavering senators everyone courts before a high-stakes vote.

Policy · Childcare

Kast Unveils His Universal Daycare Plan — No New Tax, Funded by Tapping the Jobless Fund

The government released the fine print of Sala Cuna Universal, its plan to guarantee free daycare for every working parent's child. The politically clever twist: instead of a new payroll tax (which earlier versions proposed and business hated), it phases the benefit in gradually and pays for part of it by redirecting a slice of Chile's unemployment-insurance fund. Big-business groups promptly endorsed it, since their labor costs don't rise — but critics warn that raiding the jobless fund weakens the safety net. It's a rare case of a conservative government delivering a long-standing wish of the left, but strictly on the right's terms.

Politics · Law & Order

The "Vandals Registry" Lands in Congress: 33 Offenses, 17 Penalties — Including Losing Free Tuition

Kast's proposed Registro Nacional de Actos Vandálicos is now formally before Congress, and the details are striking: 33 types of conduct can land you on the list, triggering up to 17 penalties — being barred from public housing programs, blocked from registering property, banned from stadiums, and even having state-funded university tuition suspended. Supporters frame it as accountability for rioters; civil-liberties critics see a punitive blacklist that strips benefits without a clear path off the list. Think of a US "three-strikes" law fused with a no-fly list, aimed squarely at protest violence.

Economy · Global Spillover

A US–Iran Ceasefire Reaches Chilean Wallets: Cheaper Gas, a Stronger Peso, a Rising Market

The newly announced ceasefire between the US and Iran — and the reopening of the Strait of Hormuz — sent a wave of relief through global markets, and Chile felt it fast. Oil tumbled, the local stock index climbed, and the dollar fell about 11 pesos to 890. The Finance Ministry said pump prices for gasoline and diesel should drop roughly 100 pesos a liter. For an economy that imports nearly all its fuel, a Middle East de-escalation is the kind of distant event that quietly shows up in everyone's grocery and gas bill.

A Chilean Composer Turned Music Theory Into a Board Game

After years of research, composer and academic Daniela Medel released "Tone Cluster," a board game designed to teach the hidden logic of musical harmony to students, teachers, and hobbyists — making chords and scales something you play with rather than memorize. A neat reminder that some of the best teaching tools come from people who got tired of explaining the same thing the hard way. (If you've ever fumbled through learning theory on the guitar, this one's for you.)
