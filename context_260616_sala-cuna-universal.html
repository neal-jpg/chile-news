What is Sala Cuna Universal?

The short version

"Sala cuna" is the Chilean term for nursery daycare for babies and toddlers. Today, the law only forces employers with 20 or more women on staff to provide it — which, perversely, gives companies a reason to avoid hiring women. Sala Cuna Universal is the long-promised fix: guarantee free daycare for every working parent's child, regardless of the employer's size or the parent's gender.

How it works in Chile

The fight has always been about who pays. Earlier versions floated a new payroll tax, which business groups attacked as a job-killer. The Kast government's new version, unveiled this week, drops the new tax and instead funds the benefit gradually — partly by redirecting a slice of the existing Seguro de Cesantía (Chile's unemployment-insurance fund). That's why big-business groups came out in favor: their labor costs don't go up.

Think of the US debate over universal pre-K or subsidized childcare — broadly popular in principle, but stuck for years on the same question: do you fund it with a new tax, or by reshuffling money that's already being collected? Kast chose the reshuffle.

Why it matters today

This is a rare issue where a conservative government is advancing a benefit the left has wanted for years — but on the right's terms (no new taxes, funded from an existing pot). Critics warn that raiding the unemployment fund weakens the safety net workers rely on when they lose a job. So the political fight has shifted from "should we do this?" to "is this the responsible way to pay for it?"
