Politics · The Trafficking Scandal

486 Haitian Children on Irregular Charter Flights — 64 Still Missing, Three Airlines Named, Kast Convenes All Three Branches

Chile's most alarming story is accelerating. An official pre-report by the Contraloría General de la República — Chile's independent government watchdog, the equivalent of the US GAO and a federal inspector general rolled into one — found that 486 Haitian minors were flown into Chile between 2024 and 2025 aboard irregular charter flights organized by just 12 accompanying adults. Today, video footage from one of those flights — filmed in October — surfaced publicly for the first time. Three airlines are under criminal investigation: Arajet, Aruba Airlines, and Galistair. Sixty-four children still cannot be located. UNICEF has called on Chile to clarify their whereabouts "as quickly as possible." A former official at Chile's national migration agency is suspected of involvement.

President Kast responded by summoning the Supreme Court, both chambers of Congress, the national prosecutor's office, and the Contraloría to a single emergency coordination meeting. For a US reader: picture a president calling the Chief Justice, Senate leadership, the Attorney General, and the GAO to the same table over a child-trafficking case at the border — a deliberate "all hands, all branches" signal. For Kast, whose hard-line immigration message was central to his campaign, the case simultaneously validates his warnings and tests whether the state can actually act on them.

Politics · The Corruption Spillover

Kast's Ambassador Nominee to Israel Is Under Criminal Investigation — Linked to Chile's Biggest Corruption Scandal

Gabriel Zaliasnik — Kast's pick for ambassador to Israel and a prominent Santiago attorney — is under criminal investigation by the Valparaíso prosecutor's office. The probe centers on alleged WhatsApp chats with Luis Hermosilla, the attorney at the center of Chile's sprawling "Caso Audio" corruption case, in which Zaliasnik is said to have sought favors around judicial appointments. The government is standing by Zaliasnik for now but publicly left the door open to pulling his nomination if he is formally charged. The socialist and Frente Amplio opposition is demanding his immediate removal. For a hard-right administration that ran on clean-governance messaging, having a key early appointee linked to Chile's premier bribery scandal is a politically costly headache.

Economy · Alarm Bell

Central Bank Slashes Chile's 2026 Growth Forecast to Near-Stall Speed — and Raises the Inflation Outlook

In its June Monetary Policy Report — the quarterly equivalent of the Fed's semi-annual Humphrey–Hawkins testimony — Chile's independent Central Bank cut its 2026 GDP growth projection from 1.5%–2.5% all the way down to just 1%–1.75%. The bank blamed a terrible first quarter: copper mining underperformed, tourist arrivals fell, and investment simply did not materialize. The investment growth estimate was slashed from 4% to 2.2%, and inflation was nudged up to 4.2% for the year. The plain-language takeaway: 2026 is shaping up as a write-off, any real recovery is pushed to 2027, and Kast's stated goals of 4% annual growth and 6% unemployment look very far away.

Policy · Wages

Congress Signs Off on a Minimum-Wage Bump to 553,553 Pesos

Congress finalized an increase that lifts Chile's monthly minimum wage to 553,553 pesos (roughly US$600), up about 14,000 pesos, applied retroactively to May 1. Labor Minister Rau pledged the raise would fully offset this year's inflation. Chile sets its floor wage nationally through an annual government-labor negotiation rather than the state-by-state patchwork of the US — so when it moves, it moves for the entire country simultaneously, and the number becomes an instant political scorecard.

Politics · The 100-Day Verdict

At 100 Days, Kast Has 52% Disapproval — and His Own Coalition Is Already Showing Cracks

Chile's premier CEP polling firm surveyed the country in April and May: 52% disapprove of Kast's government, just 34% approve, and 67% say they have little or no confidence he'll deliver on his campaign promises. That's a bruising number at the 100-day mark. Adding to the friction, Kast this week quietly removed a cabinet official tied to Renovación Nacional (RN) — the more establishment-conservative party in his coalition — triggering what one outlet called "contained irritation" from RN. A governing bloc spanning the hard-right Republicans and the old-guard center-right was always going to show strains; they're showing now.

Society · Animal Welfare

After "Ley Cholito," Here Comes "Ley Balto": Jail Time for Animal Abusers

Building on the earlier "Ley Cholito" animal-protection law, a group of deputies wants a new "Ley Balto" that would put serious animal abusers behind bars for real and ban them from working with animals. Chile has a deep affection for its street dogs (the quiltros), and naming laws after specific animals is a very Chilean way of turning public sentiment into legislation — a reminder that not every bill in Congress involves the budget.

Culture · History on Film

Lost Footage From Chile's 1960s News Program Is Back on TV — and It Hits Different in 2026

UChile TV's new series Chile en marcha: noticias de un futuro imaginado (Chile on the Move: News of an Imagined Future) resurrects the original "Chile en marcha" newsreel from 1965–1969 — footage of land reform, expanding schools, and urban growth during the optimistic Frei Montalva years. Think PBS recovering lost CBS News footage from the Kennedy and Johnson eras and airing it now. The juxtaposition of that era's ambitions against today's economic stagnation and political polarization seems to be very much the point. Premiered June 11.
