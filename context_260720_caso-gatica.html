The Gustavo Gatica Case — and Why the UN's Intervention Is Controversial

What happened to Gustavo Gatica. On November 8, 2019, during Chile's massive social uprising (the estallido social), Gustavo Gatica — then a 21-year-old university student — was shot in both eyes by a Carabineros officer wielding a riot shotgun loaded with buckshot pellets. He lost his sight entirely. His case became one of the most visible symbols of alleged police brutality during the protests, which left more than 30 people dead and hundreds injured by police fire across Chile.

The trial and acquittal. The Carabineros officer responsible was tried in a criminal court. In 2025, the tribunal acquitted him, ruling that the prosecution had not met the standard of proof required. Human rights organizations — including Amnesty International and Chile's own INDH (National Human Rights Institute) — condemned the verdict as evidence of systematic impunity for officers who used excessive force during the 2019 uprising. Gatica, now an elected member of Congress (he ran on an independent human-rights platform), appealed the acquittal before Santiago's First Court of Appeals.

Where the UN comes in. On July 13, 2026, the representative of the UN High Commissioner for Human Rights in Chile submitted a formal amicus-style brief to the Court of Appeals in favor of Gatica's appeal. This was unprecedented: UN country offices in Chile typically monitor, report, and advise — they don't make formal presentations inside active judicial proceedings. Think of it as the US Justice Department filing a friend-of-the-court brief in a state court case: technically possible, but unusual enough to raise separation-of-powers questions.

Why the right is angry. Former Interior Minister Juan Francisco Galli (a Piñera-era conservative) argues the move violates the mandate the Chilean state gave the UN office and could compromise the impartiality of the proceedings. He also notes that some of the legal standards the UN cited — guidelines on less-lethal weapons issued in 2020, use-of-force manuals from 2024 — postdate the 2019 shooting, making their retroactive application legally questionable. To Chile's right, this reads as a foreign body putting a thumb on the scale of Chilean justice.

Why the left disagrees. Human rights advocates say the UN presence is entirely consistent with international oversight of countries that have made human rights commitments. They argue that with 30+ deaths and hundreds of injuries from state forces, Chile's courts have a track record of failing victims, and international scrutiny is exactly what the system needs. The debate maps neatly onto the larger unresolved question of 2019: was it a police force defending the state, or a state that turned its force against its own people?
